## POLICY

### 1. Why consent matters

Patients have a fundamental legal and ethical right to determine what happens to their own bodies. Valid consent is central to all healthcare, from taking a pulse to a course of treatment. Seeking consent is also a matter of common courtesy.

### 2. What consent is — and is not

**Consent** is a patient's agreement for a health professional to provide care. It may be given non-verbally (offering an arm for a blood pressure reading), orally, or in writing.

For consent to be **valid**, the patient must:

- **Have capacity** to take that particular decision
- **Have received sufficient information** to take it
- **Not be acting under duress**

Consent obtained by fraudulent means is not valid.

> **A signature is not consent.** A signed form is evidence that a conversation happened. Where the conversation did not happen, or the patient did not understand it, the form is worth nothing. **The conversation is the consent; the form is the record of it.**

In many cases 'seeking consent' is better described as **joint decision-making** — the patient and clinician reaching agreement based on the patient's values and preferences and the clinician's knowledge.

**No one can give consent on behalf of another adult**, even where that adult lacks capacity. Treatment may still be given if it is in their best interests, provided the Mental Capacity Act 2005 is followed and it has not been refused in a valid and applicable advance decision.

### 3. The information a patient must be given — the Montgomery test

Following **Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board (2015)**, clinicians must ensure patients are aware of any **material risks** in a proposed treatment, and of **reasonable alternatives, including doing nothing.**

The Supreme Court's test:

> *"The test of materiality is whether, in the circumstances of the particular case, a reasonable person in the patient's position would be likely to attach significance to the risk, or the doctor is or should reasonably be aware that the particular patient would be likely to attach significance to it."*

This replaced the **Bolam test** (whether the conduct would be supported by a responsible body of medical opinion) for consent. Bolam continues to apply to other allegations of negligence.

**In practice this means the move is from the *reasonable doctor* to the *reasonable patient*.** A risk that is statistically small may still be material to a particular patient — a small risk to fine motor function matters differently to a musician. **The clinician is expected to know enough about the patient to know what matters to them.**

> **Montgomery applies to every prescriber and clinician, not only to doctors.** The judgment concerned a doctor, but the duty attaches to the clinical relationship. A pharmacist prescriber is held to it exactly as a GP is.

Every patient must be given:

- The diagnosis or working impression, and the degree of uncertainty in it
- The proposed treatment, and what it involves
- The material risks, including those this particular patient would find significant
- The expected benefit, and how likely it is
- **Reasonable alternatives, including no treatment at all, with their risks and benefits**
- What happens if they choose not to proceed
- **The cost** — see S12. In a private service, cost is part of an informed decision
- Who will carry out the treatment
- That they may change their mind at any point

### 4. Consent is a process, not a form

Formally giving consent is the **end point** of a process of information, discussion and decision-making. It may take place at one appointment or over several, depending on the seriousness of what is proposed and the urgency of the patient's condition.

**Single stage.** Where the risk is low, a clinician may discuss and proceed in the same appointment, with oral consent recorded in the notes.

**Two or more stages.** Where written consent is appropriate, options are generally discussed in advance:

- **Stage one** — information, discussion of options, initial oral decision
- **Stage two** — confirmation that the patient still wants to proceed

Where a form was signed in advance and time has passed, a member of the clinical team **must check whether the patient has further questions and whether their condition has changed** before proceeding.

> When confirming understanding, use a form of words requiring more than yes or no. **"Tell me what you're expecting to happen"** is worth more than **"is everything all right?"**

**For consent to be valid, the patient must feel that refusing or changing their mind was genuinely possible.** It is rarely appropriate to seek a signature after preparation for treatment has begun.

### 5. Duration of consent

Consent remains valid indefinitely unless withdrawn. However, **where new information becomes available** — new evidence of risk, or a new treatment option — between consent being given and the intervention taking place, the patient must be told and their consent reconfirmed.

`[CONFIRM: maximum interval after which written consent is re-taken rather than reconfirmed. Default: 3 months.]`

### 6. When written consent is appropriate

It is rarely a legal requirement, but it is good practice where:

- The treatment or procedure is complex, or involves significant risks
- There may be significant consequences for the patient's employment, social or personal life
- Providing clinical care is not the primary purpose of the intervention
- The treatment is part of a research programme

**A patient may withdraw consent after signing.** The signature is evidence of the process, not a binding contract.

## PROCEDURE

### 7. Who may seek consent

**The clinician carrying out the procedure is ultimately responsible for ensuring the patient is genuinely consenting.** It is that clinician who is held responsible in law if it is later challenged.

Consent may be sought by another clinician only where that person:

- Carries out the procedure themselves, **or**
- Has received specific training in advising patients about that procedure, has been assessed as competent, is aware of the limits of their knowledge, and is subject to audit

This is **delegated consent**. The delegating clinician **retains accountability for the information given**, even where they did not give it personally.

Where the person confirming consent cannot answer a patient's question, **they must not guess.** They contact the clinician carrying out the procedure, or a colleague competent to perform it, so the information is provided before the patient decides.

Patient information leaflets are a useful means of setting out risks, benefits and alternatives, but **do not replace the conversation.** All leaflets must be produced under the clinic's process for patient information and reviewed on the same cycle as this policy.

### 9. Refusal of treatment

**If consent is to mean anything, refusal must be a real option.**

A patient with capacity is entitled to refuse any treatment, **for any reason or for no reason**, even where the decision appears unwise and even where it may result in harm to them.

Where a patient refuses:

- Record the refusal, the discussion, and the information given about the consequences
- Do not treat refusal as a failure to be corrected — a patient may not be pressured
- Make clear the door remains open, and record any review arranged

### 10. Advance decisions to refuse treatment

A person may make an **advance decision** to refuse a particular treatment in anticipation of future incapacity (formerly a 'living will' or 'advance directive').

- A valid and applicable advance decision has **the same force as a contemporaneous refusal**
- **Clinicians must follow it, even where it may result in the person's death.** Failing to do so risks criminal prosecution or civil liability
- It need not be in writing **unless** it refuses life-sustaining treatment, in which case it must be **in writing, signed and witnessed**, and state that it applies even if life is at risk
- It will not be valid or applicable if the patient has withdrawn it, has since acted inconsistently with it, or if circumstances now exist that they did not anticipate
- Where it is not valid or applicable, it is still **considered as part of the best-interests assessment**
- Where there is genuine doubt about existence, validity or applicability, the case is referred to the **Court of Protection**. The court cannot overturn a valid and applicable advance decision. While a decision is awaited, life-sustaining treatment or treatment to prevent serious deterioration may be given

Advance decisions made before the Mental Capacity Act came into force may still be valid where they meet its provisions.

### 11. Mental capacity

Treatment of a person who lacks capacity is governed by the **Mental Capacity Act 2005**.

**The five statutory principles:**

1. **A person is assumed to have capacity** unless it is established that they lack it
2. A person is **not to be treated as unable to make a decision** unless all practicable steps to help them have been taken without success
3. **An unwise decision does not mean a lack of capacity**
4. Anything done for a person lacking capacity must be in their **best interests**
5. Before acting, consider whether the purpose can be achieved in a way **less restrictive** of their rights and freedom

**Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific.** A person may have capacity for one decision and not another, and capacity may fluctuate. It is never assessed once and recorded as a permanent characteristic.

**The two-stage test:**

1. Is there an impairment of, or disturbance in the functioning of, the mind or brain?
2. If so, is the person unable to **understand** the relevant information, **retain** it, **use or weigh** it as part of the decision, or **communicate** their decision?

**Where a patient lacks capacity:**

- Determine whether anyone holds a **Lasting Power of Attorney for health and welfare**, or whether a **Court of Protection deputy** has been appointed — and **verify the LPA is registered** before relying on it
- Check for a valid and applicable **advance decision**
- Where neither exists, treatment may be given in the person's **best interests**, following the MCA best-interests checklist — including the person's past and present wishes, beliefs and values, and the views of family, carers and anyone named by them
- **Record the capacity assessment, the best-interests reasoning, and who was consulted.** An undocumented best-interests decision is indistinguishable from treating without consent

> **A health and welfare LPA gives authority to refuse or consent to treatment only where it is registered and, for life-sustaining treatment, only where it expressly says so.** A property and financial affairs LPA gives no authority over treatment at all. This is a common and consequential confusion.

### 12. Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA)

An **IMCA must be instructed** where a person lacking capacity has **no family or friends** appropriate to consult, and a decision is required about **serious medical treatment** — treatment where:

- What is proposed is finely balanced in its benefits, burdens and risks, or
- There is a choice of treatments and the decision as to which is finely balanced, or
- What is proposed would have serious consequences for the patient

### 13. Children and young people

> ⚠️ **Gillick and Fraser are not interchangeable.** v01 of this policy used *"Fraser competent"* as the general test. It is not.
>
> - **Gillick competence** is the **general test** of whether a child under 16 has sufficient understanding and intelligence to consent to a proposed treatment
> - **The Fraser guidelines** apply **specifically** to giving contraceptive and sexual health advice or treatment to under-16s without parental knowledge
>
> Use **Gillick** for general competence. Use **Fraser** only for contraception and sexual health.

`[CONFIRM: complete this section only where the clinic sees under-18s — and make the answer consistent with C032, which contradicted itself on this point.]`

- **16 and 17 year olds are presumed to have capacity** to consent to their own treatment, in the same way as adults
- **Under 16s** may consent where they are assessed as **Gillick competent** for that decision. Parental involvement is encouraged but not required
- Where a child is not competent, someone with **parental responsibility** consents on their behalf — see C032 for how PR is verified
- **Where a competent child consents, a person with parental responsibility cannot override that consent**
- Refusal by a competent young person is legally complex. `[CONFIRM: the clinic's position. Default: any refusal by a competent young person of a treatment considered necessary is escalated to the clinical lead and, where the matter is serious, legal advice is sought — it is not resolved by asking a parent to sign instead.]`
- A child under 13 cannot consent to sexual activity in law — see C032

### 14. Recordings and clinical photography

Where images or recordings are made:

- Consent is obtained **separately** from consent to treatment, and is specific
- The patient is told what the recording will be used for, who will see it, how long it will be kept, and how to withdraw consent
- **Consent for clinical care, for teaching, and for publication or marketing are three different consents.** One does not imply another
- The patient is told that **once material is in the public domain it may not be possible to withdraw it**
- Withdrawal of consent for future use is honoured, and the patient is never treated differently for withdrawing
- Recordings are stored in line with C03 and C09 — not on personal devices

`[CONFIRM: whether any recordings are used for marketing, including before-and-after images. If so, consent must be explicit, separately signed, freely given, and never a condition of treatment or of a discount.]`

### 15. Emergencies

Where a patient requires immediate treatment to save life or prevent serious deterioration and cannot consent, treatment may be given in their best interests, limited to what is immediately necessary. Any known advance decision is respected. **The reasoning is recorded as soon as practicable afterwards.**

### 16. Remote consultations

The consent standard is identical. In addition:

- Confirm the patient's identity and that they are in a private place where they can speak freely
- Confirm they consent to being seen remotely, and record it
- Recognise where remote assessment is **not adequate** for the decision, and say so — offering a face-to-face appointment is part of informed consent, not a failure of the service
- See C040

## 17. Documentation

The clinical record must show, for every consented intervention:

- That a discussion took place, and **what was discussed** — specifically, the material risks and the alternatives
- Any questions the patient asked and how they were answered
- The decision, and the date
- Where consent was written, a copy of the signed form, with a copy given to the patient
- Where the patient lacked capacity: the capacity assessment, the best-interests reasoning, and who was consulted
- Where consent was refused: the refusal and the discussion of consequences

> **"Consent obtained" is not a record of consent.** It records that a form was signed, not what the patient was told. If the question is ever asked in earnest, that entry will not answer it.

## What changed from C05 v01 — and why

v01 was the strongest document in the corpus on law. Montgomery, the Mental Capacity Act, advance decisions, IMCA, the Court of Protection and delegated consent were all present and correctly stated — better than many current NHS trust policies. Almost all of that legal content is retained.

Six changes were made.

### 1. A patient-facing form went into service with a placeholder in it

Appendix 6 of v01 reads:

> *"To assist you in making an informed decision to undergo **XXXXX** procedure."*

This is on a document a patient is asked to read and sign. It is the most serious single defect found in the corpus so far — not because the legal content is wrong, but because it is the point at which an unfinished policy stops being an internal document and reaches a patient.

v02 carries a warning at the head, and the audit now asks explicitly whether any patient-facing form contains an unfilled placeholder.

### 2. Fraser was used where Gillick was meant

v01 states:

> *"For the young patient who is not deemed to be **Fraser competent**, refer to the parent/carer."*

**Fraser competence is not the general test.** The Fraser guidelines apply specifically to providing contraception and sexual health advice to under-16s without parental knowledge. The general test of a under-16's capacity to consent is **Gillick competence**.

It is an extremely common conflation, and in most consultations it makes no practical difference. It matters here because the sentence uses Fraser as the gateway to *"refer to the parent/carer"* — and applying a contraception-specific test to a general treatment decision could lead to a competent young person being sent to a parent when they had the right to decide for themselves.

### 3. The GMC consent guidance was twelve years out of date

v01 cites **GMC, *Consent: Patients and Doctors Making Decisions Together* (2008)**. That guidance was replaced in November 2020 by **GMC, *Decision making and consent*** — a substantial rewrite built around the seven principles of decision-making, not a light refresh.

Also corrected: "General Medical Council 2013" → *Good Medical Practice* 2024; Department of Health → DHSC; the `www.dh.gov.uk/consent` links, which no longer resolve; and the *"12 key points on consent"* leaflet, an archived DH publication.

### 4. There was no pharmacy regulator anywhere in it

v01 references the GMC, the Department of Health and the Medical Protection Society, and not the **GPhC** — in the consent policy of a **pharmacist-led** clinic. GPhC Standards 1 and 3 are now mapped.

Related: v01 states that *"**Doctors** must now ensure that patients are aware of any material risks."* Montgomery attaches to the clinical relationship, not to a medical degree. A pharmacist prescriber is held to it identically, and v02 says so explicitly.

### 5. It was a hospital's policy

v01 is the Department of Health model policy, largely unadapted. It covers **anaesthesia and sedation**, pre-admission clinics, out-patient departments, changing into a hospital gown, *"each consultant or specialty"*, junior staff, and *"open access clinics"*. It also contains a duplicated block of text where a graphic of patient questions was pasted twice.

Retained where relevant, removed where not. The delegated-consent machinery is kept but now defaults to **NO** — in a clinic where the treating clinician takes their own consent, an elaborate delegation framework is a liability, because it describes a system that does not exist.

### 6. Additions

- **Cost as part of informed consent.** In a private service a patient cannot make an informed decision without knowing what it costs. Links to S12
- **Remote consultation consent**, including recognising when remote assessment is not adequate
- **A cooling-off `[CONFIRM]`** for elective cosmetic and aesthetic treatment
- **The five MCA principles and the two-stage capacity test**, set out explicitly — v01 applied the Act correctly but never stated them
- **The LPA warning.** A health and welfare LPA must be *registered*, and must expressly authorise life-sustaining treatment decisions; a property and financial affairs LPA confers no authority over treatment. This is confused often enough, and consequentially enough, to be worth stating
- **Recordings split into three separate consents** — care, teaching, publication/marketing — with an explicit `[CONFIRM]` on before-and-after marketing images, which is where private clinics most often get consent wrong
- **"A signature is not consent"**, stated at the top, and the corresponding audit question. The single most common consent failure is not an absent form; it is a signed form with no record of the conversation behind it
