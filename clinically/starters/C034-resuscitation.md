To ensure that a patient, visitor or member of staff who suffers a cardiac arrest, anaphylaxis or other medical emergency at `[CLINIC NAME]` receives immediate, competent care, and that the necessary equipment, medicines and trained people are available at the time.

## POLICY

1. **Every member of staff can recognise an emergency and knows how to summon help.** This includes non-clinical staff — the person at reception is often the first to see a collapse.

2. **Every member of staff knows where the emergency equipment and medicines are kept**, and how to raise the alarm. `[CONFIRM: location, and the internal emergency call procedure]`

3. **999 is called early.** In a clinic without resuscitation facilities beyond basic life support and a defibrillator, the definitive treatment is the ambulance service. **Delay in calling is the commonest failure.**

4. **All clinical staff hold current basic life support and anaphylaxis training**, refreshed **annually**.

5. **Emergency equipment and medicines are checked on a defined schedule, and the check is recorded and signed.** `[CONFIRM: default weekly — see C07]`

6. **Every emergency is reported** as an incident under **C017**, and reviewed at `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` — including emergencies that resolved without harm.

## PROCEDURE

### Recognising and responding

`[CONFIRM: the clinic's own emergency call procedure — how a member of staff summons help from a consulting room, what is said, and who responds.]`

1. **Ensure safety** — yours, the patient's, and bystanders'
2. **Check for a response**
3. **Shout for help** and start the emergency call procedure
4. **Open the airway, check breathing** — no more than 10 seconds. Agonal gasping is **not** normal breathing
5. **If not breathing normally: call 999 (or instruct someone to), start chest compressions, and send for the defibrillator**
6. **Apply the defibrillator as soon as it arrives** and follow its prompts
7. **Continue until the ambulance service takes over**, the patient shows signs of life, or you are physically unable to continue

`[CONFIRM: compression rate, depth and ratio against the current Resuscitation Council UK guidelines rather than reproducing figures from a template.]`

### Anaphylaxis

> **The single most likely life-threatening emergency in a clinic that injects or prescribes.**

**Recognition:** sudden onset and rapid progression of symptoms, with **Airway** and/or **Breathing** and/or **Circulation** problems — usually with skin or mucosal changes, though skin changes may be absent.

**Immediate treatment:**

1. **Call for help. Call 999.**
2. **Remove the trigger** where possible.
3. **Lie the patient flat** and raise their legs — **or sit them up if breathing is difficult; never stand them up or sit them suddenly upright after collapse.**
4. **Give intramuscular adrenaline 1:1000 into the anterolateral thigh**, without delay.
5. **Repeat after 5 minutes if there is no improvement.**
6. Monitor and record.

`[CONFIRM: the adrenaline dose table by age, verified against current Resuscitation Council UK guidance. Do not reproduce doses from a template — take them from the current guideline or the BNF. This is the one place in the policy set where a wrong number could kill someone.]`

`[CONFIRM: what the clinic does about refractory anaphylaxis — where the patient is not responding after two IM doses. In a clinic setting the answer is almost always to continue IM adrenaline every 5 minutes and escalate to the ambulance service, not to attempt anything more advanced.]`

**After treatment:** all patients treated for anaphylaxis are **referred to hospital**, even where they appear to recover — biphasic reactions occur. See NICE guidance and C41.

### DNACPR and advance decisions

`[CONFIRM: the clinic's position. v01 relies on guidance from 2001 and 2004.]`

- **Cardiopulmonary resuscitation is started on anyone who suffers cardiac arrest, unless there is a valid and applicable reason not to** — a recorded DNACPR decision, a valid and applicable advance decision refusing CPR, or a ReSPECT plan
- **The presence of a DNACPR decision does not mean other treatment is withheld.** It relates to CPR only
- Where a decision is not immediately available or its validity is unclear, **start CPR** — a decision can be stopped, a delay cannot be undone
- Advance decisions are handled under **C05**

## What changed from C034 v01 — and why

v01 is a substantial and technically detailed document — the longest clinical policy in the corpus at nearly 8,000 words — and much of its content is correct. The structure, the DNACPR discussion, the recognition of the paediatric distinction, and the practical points on defibrillation are all sound.

Three changes, and the first is the most important finding in the clinical half of this review.

### 1. It is built on the 2015 Resuscitation Council guidelines

v01 cites **Resuscitation Council (UK) 2015** eleven times. Its supporting references are older still — **Decisions Relating to Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (2001)**, guidance from **2004**, and safer handling guidance from **2009**.

**Resuscitation Council UK issued new Guidelines in 2021**, which replaced the 2015 set. The organisation also **renamed** in 2020, from *Resuscitation Council (UK)* to *Resuscitation Council UK* — so every citation in the document names a body under a title it no longer uses.

The joint **BMA / RCUK / RCN** statement on CPR decisions has been through further editions since 2001, and the **ReSPECT** process — now widely used across the NHS — did not exist when the cited guidance was written and does not appear in the policy at all.

This matters more than any other stale reference found in this review. Elsewhere an out-of-date citation means a policy is untidy or an inspector is unimpressed. **Here it means the clinical content a member of staff would follow in a cardiac arrest may not match current practice.**

For that reason v02:

- Does **not** reproduce clinical figures from the template. Compression rates, ratios and drug doses are `[CONFIRM]` fields pointing to the **current guideline or the BNF**, because a template that carries a number forward is exactly how a superseded dose survives a rewrite
- **Shortens the review cycle from two years to annual.** Two years is right for a complaints policy. It is too long for resuscitation guidance, and the two-year cycle is why this document is now two editions behind

### 2. Whether it covers the patients the clinic sees is unresolved

v01 states clearly that it covers **adults only**, and refers paediatric resuscitation to C034b. That is a legitimate structure, and the policy is admirably explicit: *"if the patient group changes then this policy will need to reflect this new patient group."*

But **C032 Safeguarding Children states both that under-18s are not seen and that they are**, in the same section, four lines apart. So the clinic does not currently have a settled answer to the question this policy depends on.

And the real inspection found the concrete consequence: **the adult adrenaline strength was held, the paediatric strength was not.** In a clinic where children attend — even only as a patient's accompanying child in the waiting room — that is the gap that matters.

The scope question is now the first thing in the document, cross-referenced to C032, with the paediatric equipment and medicines requirements attached to the answer.

### 3. Additions

- **Anaphylaxis given its own procedure.** v01 mentions adrenaline twice, in a list of medicines that "may" be held. For a service that injects and prescribes, anaphylaxis is the most likely life-threatening emergency it will face, and it warranted more than a line in a stock list
- **Referral to hospital after anaphylaxis, even on apparent recovery** — biphasic reactions
- **"Do not hold equipment the team is not trained to use."** A clinic with airway adjuncts nobody can use has bought a false sense of capability
- **Call 999 early**, stated as a principle. In a clinic without advanced facilities the definitive treatment is the ambulance service, and delay in calling is the commonest failure
- **Non-clinical staff trained to recognise an emergency and summon help.** The person at reception is usually the first to see a collapse, and v01 addresses only clinical staff
- **Practical rather than online-only training**, with a manikin and the clinic's own AED — an explicit Resuscitation Council UK quality standard and a frequent inspection finding
- **An annual simulated drill**, which is the only way to discover whether the AED is where everyone assumes it is and how long help actually takes to arrive
- **Debrief separately from the incident investigation** — a debrief is for the people involved, not for the paperwork
- **Restock the same day**, recorded. The commonest way a clinic is unprepared for the second emergency is the first one
- *"Anticonvulsants as prescribed by the Medical Doctor"* has become a `[CONFIRM]`. Whether the clinic holds them, and under what legal direction, is a real decision — and the phrasing is the sixth instance in the corpus of clinical activity being attributed to doctors in a pharmacist-led service
