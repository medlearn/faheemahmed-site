## THE TWO DUTIES

> **This is the most important distinction in this policy, and it was absent from v01.**

There are **two** duties of candour, and they are not the same duty.

| | **Statutory duty** | **Professional duty** |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | CQC **Regulation 20** | GMC/NMC joint guidance · **GPhC Standard 8** |
| **Who holds it** | **The organisation** — `[CLINIC NAME]` | **Every individual registered professional** |
| **Threshold** | A **notifiable safety incident** — see below | **Anything that goes wrong**, including near misses and low harm |
| **What it requires** | Specific prescribed steps, in a prescribed order, with a written record | Be open and honest with the patient; apologise; explain; report and learn |
| **Consequence of breach** | Enforcement action against the provider; it is a **criminal offence** not to comply with parts of Reg 20 | Fitness to practise referral |

**The professional duty bites at a far lower threshold and applies to you personally.** Where an incident does not meet the statutory threshold, the professional duty still applies — the patient is still told, and still receives an apology.

> **Never use "it wasn't a notifiable safety incident" as a reason not to tell a patient something went wrong.** That reasoning confuses the statutory threshold with the professional obligation, and it is the commonest way candour fails.

## WHEN THE STATUTORY DUTY IS TRIGGERED

> ⚠️ **There are TWO definitions in Regulation 20, and most clinics are given the wrong one.**
>
> **Regulation 20(8)** defines a notifiable safety incident for **health service bodies** (NHS trusts) — death, severe harm, moderate harm, prolonged psychological harm.
>
> **Regulation 20(9)** defines it for **every other registered person** — which includes `[CLINIC NAME]`. **The thresholds are different, and they are lower.** Use the table below, not the NHS one.

A **notifiable safety incident** for a registered person **other than a health service body** is an unintended or unexpected incident occurring during the provision of a regulated activity that, in the reasonable opinion of a healthcare professional, **appears to have resulted in one of the following, where the outcome relates directly to the incident rather than to the natural course of the illness or underlying condition**:

| Category | Threshold |
|---|---|
| **Death** | Where the death relates directly to the incident |
| **Impairment of sensory, motor or intellectual functions** | Lasting, or likely to last, **28 days or more** |
| **Changes to the structure of the body** | — |
| **Prolonged pain** | Pain experienced, or likely to be experienced, for **28 days or more** |
| **Prolonged psychological harm** | Harm experienced, or likely to be experienced, for **28 days or more** |
| **Shortened life expectancy** | — |
| **Treatment required to prevent any of the above** | Where the patient **required treatment by a healthcare professional in order to prevent** death, or any of the injuries above |

> **Note what this means in practice.** The last row is wide. **An incident that would have caused harm but was prevented by treatment is still notifiable** — a wrong dose caught and corrected in a way that required clinical intervention meets the threshold even though the patient was ultimately fine.
>
> **And the durations are what matter, not severity labels.** "Moderate harm" and "severe harm" do not appear in this definition at all. The question is whether an effect lasts **28 days**, not how bad it looked on the day.

`[CONFIRM: verify against the current text of Regulation 20(9) and CQC's guidance before adopting.]`

**The duty applies regardless of:**

- Whether anyone has complained
- Whether the patient has noticed
- Whether anyone is at fault
- Whether legal proceedings have started or are threatened
- Whether the incident was caused by this clinic or identified here

## POLICY — the principles of being open

1. **Acknowledgement.** All patient safety incidents are acknowledged and reported as soon as they are identified.

2. **Truthfulness, timeliness and clarity.** Information is given truthfully and openly by an appropriate person, **as soon as practicable**, based solely on the facts known at the time. Staff explain that new information may emerge, and keep the patient updated as the investigation progresses.

3. **A single point of contact.** The patient receives clear, unambiguous information and one named contact. **They must not receive conflicting accounts from different members of staff**, and medical jargon is avoided.

4. **Apology.** The patient receives a **sincere expression of regret for the harm that has resulted**, as early as possible, **both verbally and in writing.**

   > **An apology is not an admission of liability** — section 2 of the Compensation Act 2006 says so expressly, and Regulation 20 *requires* one. Withholding an apology to protect a legal position is both a regulatory breach and, usually, the thing that turns an incident into a claim.

   **The verbal apology is not delayed for any reason** — not to arrange a formal meeting, not for fear or apprehension, not for staff availability. Delay increases anxiety, anger and frustration.

   Who apologises is decided on seniority, relationship to the patient, and experience of the type of incident. `[CONFIRM: who decides, and who is the default.]`

5. **Recognising expectations.** Patients can expect to be fully informed of the incident and its consequences, face to face, and to be treated sympathetically and with respect. Support is offered appropriate to their needs — an independent advocate, an interpreter, or additional support. Information about **AvMA** is offered where appropriate.

6. **Professional support.** Staff involved in an incident are supported. `[CONFIRM: what support is available.]` Where there is reason to believe a member of staff has committed a criminal act, the clinic takes steps to preserve its position and advises them at an early stage to obtain separate legal advice or representation.

7. **Risk management and systems improvement.** Root cause analysis, or a similar structured technique, is used to find the underlying causes. **Investigations focus on improving systems of care** — incidents usually result from system failures and rarely from the actions of one individual — and the changes made are reviewed for effectiveness.

8. **Confidentiality.** Communication outside the clinical team is strictly need-to-know and, where practicable, anonymised. The patient is told who will be involved in the investigation **before** it takes place.

9. **Continuity of care.** Patients continue to receive all usual treatment and to be treated with respect and compassion. **Where a patient asks to be cared for by someone else, that is arranged**, without resistance and without it affecting their care.

10. **Candour and complaints are separate.** Responding to a complaint does not discharge the duty of candour, and the duty arises whether or not anyone complains.

## PROCEDURE

### Step 1 — Immediate

1. **Clinical care first.** The patient's immediate clinical needs take priority over everything in this policy.
2. Report the incident under **C017**.
3. Notify `[CONFIRM: role]` the same day.

### Step 2 — Decide which duty applies, and record the decision

`[CONFIRM: role]` records, for every incident:

- Does this meet the **notifiable safety incident** threshold? Which category, and why?
- If not, does the **professional duty** apply? *(For anything that went wrong and affected a patient, the answer is yes.)*
- Is a **CQC statutory notification** required?
- Is a **safeguarding** referral required?
- Does the **indemnity provider** need to be told?

**Where it is unclear whether the threshold is met, proceed as though it is.** Nothing is lost by being open with a patient about an incident that later proves not to be notifiable.

### Step 3 — The statutory steps, where the duty is triggered

Regulation 20 requires all of the following. They are not optional and the order matters.

| # | Requirement | Timescale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Notify the patient in person** (or their representative where the patient has died or lacks capacity) | **As soon as reasonably practicable** after becoming aware |
| 2 | **Provide an account of all the facts known at that point** | At the notification |
| 3 | **Advise what further enquiries are believed to be appropriate** | At the notification |
| 4 | **Give an apology** | At the notification |
| 5 | **Keep a written record of the notification** | Immediately after |
| 6 | **Follow up in writing** — the same information, the results of enquiries, and an apology | `[CONFIRM: default within 10 working days of the notification]` |
| 7 | **Keep a copy of all correspondence** | Permanently, with the incident record |
| 8 | Keep the patient updated as enquiries proceed, and send the outcome | On conclusion |

**Where the patient or their representative cannot be contacted, or declines to engage**, a written record is kept of the attempts made and the reason. **The duty is not discharged by one unanswered phone call** — reasonable attempts must be documented.

### Step 4 — Grading the response

| Level | Description | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **No harm / near miss** | No harm reached the patient | Reported and analysed for themes under C017. **The professional duty may still apply** — where the patient was aware something happened, they are told. `[CONFIRM: default — near misses the patient did not notice are not routinely disclosed, but are always recorded and reviewed]` |
| **Low harm** | Short-term injury resolving in about a month | Open discussion between the staff providing care and the patient. Being open principles applied. Documented on the incident form |
| **Moderate harm** | Significant but not permanent harm, requiring a moderate increase in treatment | **Statutory duty likely triggered.** Formal notification, written follow-up, structured investigation |
| **Severe harm / death / prolonged psychological harm** | See thresholds above | **Statutory duty triggered.** Notification by a senior person, full investigation, CQC statutory notification, indemnity provider informed, `[CONFIRM: role]` leads |

### Step 5 — The meeting

Where a face-to-face meeting takes place:

- The patient may bring a relative, friend or advocate
- **Only facts known at the time are given.** Speculation about cause, and attribution of blame to a named individual, are avoided
- The patient is told what will happen next and when they will hear
- They are given the name and contact details of one person
- A written record is made and sent to the patient
- **The record goes in the clinical record as well as the incident file** — see C09

### Step 6 — Afterwards

- Findings, actions, owners and dates are recorded and tracked to completion
- Reported to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`
- Actions checked at the following meeting
- Lessons shared with staff, anonymised where necessary
- The patient is told what changed as a result — **this matters more to most patients than the apology itself**

### How incidents come to light

Not only through the incident reporting system. Also through:

- A complaint (C04)
- A legal claim, a Coroner's Officer, or media interest
- Records review or audit
- **Another patient, a visitor, or a non-clinical member of staff**

**Route of discovery does not change the duty.** An incident found through a complaint triggers candour exactly as one self-reported does.

## MONITORING COMPLIANCE

`[CONFIRM: audit owner and frequency. Default: Clinical Lead, six-monthly.]`

- Was the apology given verbally **and** in writing?

- Were actions completed, and was the patient told what changed?
- **Are there incidents where the professional duty applied but nothing was said to the patient?**
- Is the number of incidents reported plausible? A period with no incidents in a busy service usually means under-reporting, not safety

## Appendix 1 — Quick reference

**Something went wrong. What do I do?**

1. **Look after the patient.** Clinical need comes first.
2. **Tell `[CONFIRM: role]` today.** Report it under C017.
3. **Tell the patient — in person, as soon as you can.** Do not wait for an investigation, a meeting, or permission.
4. **Say sorry.** An apology is not an admission of liability, and Regulation 20 requires one.
5. **Say what you know, and only what you know.** "I don't know yet, and here's how we'll find out" is a complete and acceptable answer.
6. **Write it down** — what you said, when, to whom, and what you agreed next.
7. **Give them one name and number** to contact.
8. **Follow up in writing.**

**Never:** delay telling them · guess at the cause · blame a colleague to the patient · withhold an apology to protect a legal position · assume that because it wasn't "notifiable", the patient does not need to know.

## What changed from C021 v01 — and why

v01 was one of the better documents in the corpus — a full and faithful account of the **Being Open** framework, including the principles, the harm-grading, professional support, systems thinking, confidentiality and continuity of care. Nearly all of it is retained, much of it verbatim.

Four changes.

### 1. It described "being open" but never stated the statutory duty

This is the substantive gap. v01 is titled *Being Open / Duty of Candour* and uses the phrase throughout, but it never sets out:

- **What a "notifiable safety incident" is** — the four statutory categories and their thresholds
- **The prescribed steps of Regulation 20** — notify in person, account for the facts known, advise what enquiries will follow, apologise, keep a written record, follow up in writing, keep the correspondence
- That **failing to comply with parts of Regulation 20 is a criminal offence**

Being Open is a *culture* framework. **Regulation 20 is a law with defined triggers and defined steps.** A policy that has the culture but not the legal mechanics leaves staff unable to answer the question an inspector actually asks: *"show me, for this incident, that you notified in person, apologised, and followed up in writing."*

Both are now in the document, with the culture framework as the "how" and the regulation as the "must".

### 2. It did not distinguish the statutory duty from the professional duty

The two duties have different holders, different thresholds and different consequences. v01 treated candour as a single obligation.

The distinction matters most at the low end: **the professional duty applies to every registered individual and bites at any level of harm**, well below the statutory threshold. Without the distinction, the natural reading is that a moderate-harm threshold governs whether a patient is told anything at all — and *"it wasn't a notifiable safety incident"* becomes a reason for silence. It is the commonest way candour fails, and it fails in good faith.

For a pharmacist-led service the relevant professional source is **GPhC Standard 8**, which appeared nowhere in v01 — consistent with the corpus-wide absence of the GPhC.

### 3. Its framework source is defunct

v01 is built on the **National Patient Safety Agency's** Being Open framework. The NPSA was abolished in 2012. The content remains sound and is retained; the citations are now to **CQC's Regulation 20 guidance** and the **GMC/NMC joint guidance**, which are current and are what an inspector will measure against.

### 4. It was written for a hospital

*"Clinical teams"*, *"local department level"*, *"multi-disciplinary teams"*, *"incident investigation leads"*, *"the Compliance Manager"* and *"researchers observing healthcare staff"* describe an organisation `[CLINIC NAME]` is not. Retained where it translates, replaced where it does not.

### Additions

- **The apology-and-liability point, stated explicitly**, with the Compensation Act 2006 reference. v01 said apologies should be given; it did not say why staff and indemnifiers need not fear them. That fear is the main practical obstacle
- **"Where it is unclear whether the threshold is met, proceed as though it is"**
- **What to do when the patient cannot be contacted or declines to engage** — documented reasonable attempts, not one unanswered call
- **Candour continues during litigation** — a legal duty is not suspended by a claim, which also corrects the instruction in C04 v01 that investigation *"immediately ceases"* when legal action begins
- **Telling the patient what changed**, which most patients value more than the apology
- **The quick reference card**, rewritten as eight steps a member of staff can follow at the moment it happens rather than a summary of principles
