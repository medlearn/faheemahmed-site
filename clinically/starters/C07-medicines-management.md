## AIM

To ensure all staff understand the handling and management of medicines, and that measures are in place for their safe management, secure handling and storage.

---

## POLICY

1. **Medicines are handled in accordance with the Human Medicines Regulations 2012, the Medicines Act 1968 and the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971.**

2. **All staff work within their professional standards.** Pharmacy professionals work to the GPhC standards; doctors to GMC guidance; nurses to NMC standards. **Every registrant works within their own competence and scope of practice.**

3. **Doses come from the BNF or the product's Summary of Product Characteristics.** They are never worked from memory, from a previous prescription, or from an AI-generated suggestion.

4. **A named person is accountable for medicines management.**

   | | |
   |---|---|
   | **Medicines lead** | `[CONFIRM: NAMED individual]` |
   | **Deputy** | `[CONFIRM: NAMED individual]` |

5. **Written procedures exist for stock control, supply and adverse reactions**, and staff sign a register to confirm they have read them.

6. **Every medicines incident and near miss is reported** under C017 — including wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong patient, omitted dose, and errors caught before they reached the patient.

## PROCEDURE

### Emergency medicines

`[CONFIRM: where emergency medicines, oxygen and the defibrillator are held. Every member of staff must know.]`

- **Medicines and equipment are checked `[CONFIRM: default weekly]`**, and the check is recorded and signed

- **The written procedure for administering a medicine without a written direction in a life-threatening emergency — for example adrenaline in anaphylaxis — is kept with the emergency equipment**, not filed elsewhere

See C034.

### Administration and supply

Medicines are prescribed or supplied after a consultation, or administered as part of a procedure.

**Every medicine taken from stock is recorded**, showing:

- Who the medicine is for
- Who is administering or supplying it
- The medicine, form, strength, quantity and batch/expiry
- Date and time
- **The signature of the person responsible**

### Supplying medicines to take home

Where a patient is supplied a medicine to take away, it is:

- **Labelled** with the patient's name, the medicine, form, strength, dose directions, quantity, date, and the name and address of the supplying clinic
- Accompanied by the **patient information leaflet**
- Accompanied by written instructions
- **Recorded in the patient's clinical record**

See Appendix 2.

### The legal routes for supply and administration

| Route | What it is | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **Prescription** | A written direction from an independent prescriber for a named patient | Prescriber acting within their competence and scope. See C07f |
| **Patient Specific Direction (PSD)** | A written instruction from a prescriber, for a **named individual patient**, for another person to supply or administer | Must be patient-specific and in writing. **A PSD cannot be written for a patient the prescriber has not assessed.** Templates `[CONFIRM: where held]` |
| **Patient Group Direction (PGD)** | A written direction allowing supply or administration to a **group** of patients not individually identified beforehand | **Each PGD must be authorised by the organisation and signed by both a doctor (or dentist) and a pharmacist.** PGDs are legally restricted: they may only be used by named, trained, assessed professionals from the specified registered professions. `[CONFIRM: whether the clinic uses PGDs at all. | **Exemption in an emergency** | Administration of a listed medicine (e.g. adrenaline) for the immediate saving of life | Per the Human Medicines Regulations 2012. Documented immediately afterwards |

### Private prescriptions

Medicines are prescribed only after a consultation and are recorded in the clinical record. Where not dispensed in-house, the patient presents the prescription at a pharmacy.

**The patient is told what the medicine will cost them to have dispensed** — see S12. A prescription the patient cannot afford to dispense is not a treatment.

### Stock procurement

- Medicines and related equipment are procured **only from holders of a Wholesale Dealer's Licence — WDA(H)** — or from a licensed manufacturer. `[CONFIRM: how licence status is verified. It can be checked on the MHRA register — and should be, not assumed]`
- Accounts are held with approved wholesalers and manufacturers
- **No new account is opened, and no new medicine added to stock, without the approval of `[CONFIRM: role]`**
- A stock order record is held and **matched against delivery notes**
- Deliveries are checked on receipt for condition, expiry and correctness. Unsatisfactory stock is returned to the supplier and the return recorded
- Stock is entered onto the stock control system on receipt

### Storage

- **All medicines are kept in locked cupboards**, other than emergency medicines held in designated emergency boxes in clinical rooms
- Cupboard keys are held by `[CONFIRM: role]` and are not left in the lock or in an unsecured place
- **Medicines requiring cold storage are kept in a locked, dedicated pharmacy refrigerator** — never a food refrigerator
- **The refrigerator has a maximum/minimum thermometer, and current, maximum and minimum temperatures are recorded every working day and signed**
- **The cold chain range is 2°C to 8°C.** `[CONFIRM: the excursion procedure — v01 required a temperature log but said nothing about what to do when the temperature goes out of range. Discarding stock without advice wastes it; using it without advice risks giving an ineffective medicine]`
- Medical gases are stored in a secure, purpose-built cylinder store, kept dry and clean, with full and empty cylinders segregated

### Adverse drug reactions

**Suspected adverse drug reactions are reported to the MHRA via the Yellow Card scheme:**

- **All** suspected reactions for medicines under additional monitoring (black triangle ▼)
- **All serious** suspected reactions for any medicine — including those already well recognised

**Suspicion is sufficient. Certainty about causation is not required, and waiting for it is the reason most reactions go unreported.**

The reaction is also recorded in the patient's clinical record, in the allergies and adverse reactions field, and reported as an incident under C017.

## What changed from C07 v01 — and why

v01 was operationally sound — stock control, the audit trail from ordering to disposal, locked storage, the fridge log, weekly checks and the disposal sign-off are all correct and retained. Five changes.

### 1. There is no pharmacy regulator in it

v01's references are the CQC Fundamental Standards, the BNF, the **General Medical Council**, **NMC Standards**, the Medicines Act, the Misuse of Drugs Act, MIMS, and *Pharmacy Law & Ethics* (Dale and Appelbe).

**No GPhC.** In the medicines management policy of a clinic whose registered manager is a pharmacist prescriber. This is the sharpest instance of the corpus-wide finding that the GPhC appears in **0 of 119 documents** — because if there is one policy in a pharmacist-led service where the pharmacy regulator's standards belong, it is this one.

The **Human Medicines Regulations 2012** were also absent. They are the principal legislation governing the supply of medicines in the UK, and the Medicines Act 1968 alone is not a sufficient legal basis.

### 2. PGD guidance was attributed to the wrong bodies

> *"The **BMJ and RCN** guidance for Patient Specific Directions (PSD) and Patient Group Directions (PGD) are followed within this clinic."*

The BMJ is a medical journal and does not issue PGD guidance. The authoritative sources are **NICE MPG2** and the **Specialist Pharmacy Service**.

The PGD requirement was also stated imprecisely. v01 says a PGD *"must include a pharmacist in the clinical team that is responsible for compiling and signing each PGD off."* The actual requirement is that **each PGD must be authorised by the organisation and signed by both a doctor or dentist and a pharmacist** — and, critically, that it may only be used by **named, trained and assessed practitioners** from specified professions. A PGD without named authorised practitioners is not valid, and that omission is the commonest PGD failure found at inspection.

### 3. The cold chain had a log but no excursion procedure

v01 requires *"a log … for the daily monitoring of fridge temperatures."* It does not say to use a maximum/minimum thermometer, does not state the 2–8°C range, and — most importantly — **says nothing about what to do when the temperature goes out of range.**

A temperature log that nobody acts on is a record of a failure, not a control. Added: max/min recording, the range, quarantine, and the requirement to seek advice before either discarding or using excursed stock. Both wrong answers are expensive — one wastes stock, the other gives a patient an ineffective medicine.

### 4. Yellow Card reporting was absent

The same gap found in C017. v01 mentions *"adverse reaction"* only as one of the written procedures staff should have read. There is no reporting route, no threshold, and no named responsible person.

For a service running titration-based treatment, this is the single most likely medicines-safety duty to arise in practice.

### 5. References and framing

- **"Prof. Sparrow Surgery No. 9 – Emergency Meds"** is listed as a reference. This is not a citable source — it appears to be a note about another practice's arrangements, and it cannot be verified or reviewed
- **"Monthly Index of Medicines (MIMS)"** is cited correctly here. This confirms that the *"MIMMS"* appearing three times in C07f is a typographical error for MIMS, rather than a reference to Major Incident Medical Management and Support
- The in-house dispensary is described as *"used by **doctors** consulting at Ahmeys Clinic"* — the third instance in the corpus of clinical activity being attributed to doctors in a pharmacist-led service
- **"MHRA approved wholesalers"** is imprecise. The requirement is a holder of a **Wholesale Dealer's Licence, WDA(H)**, and licence status is verifiable on the MHRA register — so it should be verified rather than assumed
