## AIM

A patient is entitled to know, before they choose us, who regulates us and how we have been judged. Regulation 20A makes displaying our CQC rating a legal requirement — not a courtesy, and not conditional on the rating being one we are pleased with.

This policy sets out what must be displayed, where, who checks it, and — the part that actually fails in practice — **who updates it when the rating changes.**

---

## POLICY

1. **The current CQC rating is displayed at every premises and on every website.** This is a legal duty under Regulation 20A. It applies as soon as a rating is published following inspection.

2. **The rating is displayed conspicuously and legibly.** At the premises, that means visible to a patient in the principal area they use — the reception or waiting area — at a height and size they can read without asking. Not in a folder, not behind the desk, not in a corridor patients do not enter.

3. **On any website, the rating appears on the page a patient is most likely to land on**, or is reachable in one click from it via a clearly labelled link. Burying it in a footer several levels down does not meet the requirement.

4. **The rating displayed is always the current one.** Where a rating changes following inspection, all displays are updated within `[CONFIRM: default 21 calendar days of publication — CQC's guidance expects display "as soon as reasonably practicable"; 21 days is the outside limit, not the target]`.

5. **Ratings are displayed in full and in context, never selectively.** Where the service has an overall rating and ratings for individual key questions, both are shown. **Displaying a good rating for one key question while omitting a poorer overall rating is a breach.** The rating is displayed as CQC published it, without editing, cropping, or commentary that undermines it.

6. **We may add factual context, but not dispute the rating in the display.** Where improvements have been made since inspection, a factual statement to that effect may sit alongside the rating. It may not obscure it, contradict it, or suggest the rating is wrong or out of date.

7. **Where a service has not yet been rated, that is stated.** A newly registered service displays that it is registered with CQC and has not yet been inspected. **Absence of a rating is never presented as, or allowed to look like, a positive rating.**

8. **Registration information is displayed alongside the rating.** Patients should be able to see who regulates us and how to contact them:
   - CQC registration status and provider name as registered
   - CQC contact details
   - `[CONFIRM: where the service operates from GPhC-registered pharmacy premises — GPhC premises registration number, Superintendent Pharmacist, and the Responsible Pharmacist notice as required]`
   - `[CONFIRM: registration numbers of the professionals providing care, if displayed. Recommended: yes — it allows a patient to verify their prescriber on the public register, and a private prescribing service that does not is conspicuous by omission.]`

9. **Our public claims about regulation are accurate.** We do not describe ourselves as "approved", "endorsed", "accredited", or "recommended" by a regulator. Registration means we are registered. Inspection produces a rating. **No regulator approves or endorses a provider, and implying otherwise is a misleading commercial practice as well as a regulatory risk.**

10. **Display is checked on a schedule, not only when something changes.** Signs fall down, get covered, and are removed during redecoration. Websites are rebuilt and pages lost. `[CONFIRM: default — checked quarterly and recorded.]`

### When a new rating is published

1. `[CONFIRM: role]` is notified when the draft report arrives and again on publication.
2. Within `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` of publication:
   - Premises display replaced with the current rating
   - Website updated, including the report link and inspection date
   - Any other material carrying the old rating identified and corrected — leaflets, booking platforms, directory listings, social media profiles, printed price lists, email footers
3. The update is recorded in the display log with the date and who did it.
4. `[CONFIRM: role]` confirms completion at the next `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`.

**Point 2's third bullet is where this usually fails.** The reception sign gets changed; a third-party booking site still shows a rating from two inspections ago. The list of places the rating appears is maintained as a standing list, not reconstructed from memory each time.

### Where the rating displayed is a poor one

The obligation is unchanged. A *requires improvement* or *inadequate* rating is displayed in full, in the same places, to the same standard, within the same timescale. Failing to display a poor rating is a breach of Regulation 20A and will be treated far more seriously by the regulator than the rating itself.

An accompanying factual statement of what has been done since is permitted and often sensible — see policy item 6.

### The standing display list

`[CONFIRM: role]` maintains a list of every location where the rating or registration information appears. It includes at minimum:

- Each premises, with the specific location within it
- Website — each page
- Online booking platform(s)
- Third-party directory listings
- Printed patient information and price lists
- `[CONFIRM: any others — social media profiles, partner websites, aggregator listings]`

The list is reviewed at each quarterly check and updated whenever a new channel is created. **A new website, listing, or booking platform is not live until it carries the required information.**

### Quarterly check

`[CONFIRM: role]`, `[CONFIRM: default quarterly]`, walks the list and records:

- Is the rating displayed at each premises location, and is it the current one?
- Is it visible and legible from where a patient stands?
- Is the website display present, current, and is the report link working?
- Do all third-party listings show the current rating?
- Is registration information present and accurate?
- Are there any claims of regulator "approval" or "endorsement" anywhere in our material?

Recorded, dated, signed, and reported to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`. The record is itself the evidence of compliance — a display that is correct but never checked cannot be evidenced.

### Audit

`[CONFIRM: audit owner and frequency. Default: Registered Manager, annually.]` The annual audit repeats the quarterly check and additionally asks: when the rating last changed, how long did it take to update every channel, and was anything missed?

## Why this policy exists — and why it wasn't in the 2022 set

This policy was not written in response to an inspection question. It was found by a **reverse completeness check** — working backwards from the CQC Fundamental Standards to see which regulations had no theme and no policy behind them. **Regulation 20A had neither.** It is the reason theme 24 exists.

It is worth being clear about why a 123-document policy set missed it:

- **It is not a clinical duty**, so it does not appear in any of the clinical policies
- **It is not an HR duty**, so it is not in the HR set
- **It looks administrative** — a sign on a wall — and so reads as too small to need a policy

But it is a **specific, named, standalone legal requirement**, and it is the kind of breach that is trivially easy for an inspector to identify: they can see the waiting room, and they can open the website on their phone. There is no interpretation involved and no clinical judgement to weigh. It is either displayed or it is not.

Two provisions matter most:

- **Item 5 (no selective display).** Showing a good key-question rating while omitting a poorer overall rating is a breach, and it is the version of this failure most likely to happen by well-meaning accident on a marketing page.

- **The standing display list.** The premises sign is rarely the problem. A stale rating on a third-party booking platform, months after the update, is the realistic failure — and it only gets caught if someone maintains a list of everywhere the rating appears.

Item 9 also matters beyond CQC: **"CQC approved" and "GPhC approved" appear routinely in private clinic marketing, and neither is a real thing.** Registration is not approval. This is the same discipline recorded in [RED-LINES.md](../RED-LINES.md) for MHRA language, applied to the other regulators.
