## AIM

`[CLINIC NAME]` operates alongside, not instead of, a patient's registered GP. Care given here forms part of a wider clinical picture that we do not hold in full. Where we do not tell the GP what we have done, three risks follow: the GP may prescribe something that interacts with what we have started; the patient's record becomes incomplete; and no one holds overall responsibility for monitoring.

This policy sets out when we share information with a patient's GP, what we share, how we do it safely, and — importantly — what we do when a patient does not want us to.

---

## POLICY

1. **Every patient is asked for their GP details at registration.** Practice name, address, and the patient's NHS number where known. This is recorded in the clinical record. Where a patient is not registered with a GP, that fact is recorded explicitly, not left blank.

2. **The default position is that we write to the GP.** Patients are told this at registration, before any assessment takes place, and it is recorded that they have been told. It is not a surprise disclosed after treatment has started.

3. **We write to the GP in all of the following circumstances:**
   - We have prescribed any medicine
   - We have made or suspect a new diagnosis
   - We have started monitoring or a treatment programme requiring ongoing review
   - We have identified a safeguarding concern (see C08/C032 — safeguarding disclosures do not require consent)
   - We have identified a finding requiring investigation or referral we are not undertaking ourselves
   - The patient has a long-term condition our treatment may affect
   - `[CONFIRM: any additional service-specific trigger — e.g. for an ADHD service, always on initiation and at each titration change]`

4. **Sharing is on the basis of consent, except where the law provides otherwise.** Consent to share with the GP is sought and recorded separately from consent to treatment. The two are not bundled. Safeguarding disclosures, disclosures required by court order, and disclosures in the public interest do not depend on consent and are covered by C03 and C08.

5. **A patient may refuse GP notification, and refusal is respected — but it is not the end of the matter.** Where a patient declines:
   - The refusal, the date, and the reason given (if offered) are recorded in the clinical record
   - The clinician explains the specific clinical risks of the GP not knowing, in terms relevant to that patient, and records that this was done
   - The clinician makes a documented clinical judgement about whether it remains safe to treat without GP involvement
   - **Where it is not safe to proceed without GP involvement, we do not proceed.** Declining to treat is a legitimate and sometimes necessary outcome; it is not a failure of service
   - `[CONFIRM: services where GP notification is a non-negotiable condition of treatment. Default: controlled drugs, any long-term or high-risk medicine, and any treatment requiring blood monitoring. For these, "no GP letter" means "no treatment."]`

6. **We do not rely on the patient to pass information on.** Handing the patient a letter to give to their GP is not GP notification. Correspondence is sent by `[CLINIC NAME]` directly.

7. **Correspondence is sent by a secure route.** `[CONFIRM: the route used. Default: NHSmail (@nhs.net) to the practice's shared clinical inbox where both parties hold NHSmail; otherwise password-protected attachment or postal letter marked private and confidential. Standard unencrypted email to a non-secure address is not an acceptable route for identifiable clinical information.]`

8. **We accept information in the other direction.** Where a GP or another provider writes to us about a shared patient, that correspondence is filed in the clinical record and reviewed by a clinician — not simply stored. `[CONFIRM: who reviews incoming clinical correspondence and within what timescale. Default: the treating clinician, within 3 working days.]`

9. **Our care is not open-ended by default.** Where we are not taking on ongoing responsibility for a condition, the letter says so explicitly, and says who does hold it. Ambiguity about who is monitoring is itself a safety risk.

## PROCEDURE

### What a GP letter must contain

Every letter to a GP contains, as a minimum:

- Patient identifiers: full name, date of birth, address, NHS number where known
- Date of consultation and name and registration number of the clinician who saw the patient
- `[CLINIC NAME]`, address, and a contact route for clinical queries
- Reason for attendance and relevant history
- Findings and any investigations undertaken or requested
- Diagnosis or working impression
- **Medicines prescribed** — drug, form, strength, dose, quantity, and duration
- Monitoring required, with the parameter, the frequency, and **explicitly who is responsible for it**
- What we have asked of the GP, if anything, stated as a clear request rather than an implication
- Whether we intend to see the patient again, and when
- The date the patient consented to this letter being sent

### Timescales

| Situation | Send by |
|---|---|
| Safeguarding concern | Same day (in addition to the safeguarding referral, not instead of it) |
| Urgent clinical finding requiring GP action | Same working day |
| New prescription of a long-term or high-risk medicine | Within `[CONFIRM: default 2 working days]` |
| Routine — new diagnosis, treatment started, change of dose | Within `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` |
| Discharge from our service | Within `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` of the final appointment |

### Where the patient has no registered GP

1. Record that the patient is not registered.
2. Explain why registration matters for their safety, and record that this was explained.
3. Offer information on how to register locally.
4. Make an explicit, recorded clinical decision about whether the service can be safely provided to an unregistered patient. `[CONFIRM: services that require a registered GP. Default: any treatment requiring monitoring or repeat prescribing.]`
5. Where we proceed, we hold and document a clear plan for who provides interim and emergency care.

### Where the patient refuses notification

1. Record the refusal, the date, and any reason given.
2. Explain the specific clinical risks to that patient and record that this was done.
3. Ask whether the concern can be met another way — sharing a limited summary rather than the full letter, or delaying until after a further discussion. Record what was offered.
4. Make and record a clinical decision on whether treatment can safely proceed.
5. Where treatment proceeds, set a review point at which the question is asked again — refusal is a position at a moment in time, not a permanent instruction.
6. Where the risk is to someone other than the patient, or a safeguarding threshold is met, consent is not the deciding factor. See C03 and C08.

### Records

The clinical record shows, for every patient: GP details or the recorded absence of them; whether consent to share was given, refused, or limited; the date and route of any correspondence sent; a copy of the letter; and any reply received.

### Audit

`[CONFIRM: audit owner and frequency. Default: Clinical Lead, six-monthly.]` The audit samples records and asks:

- Were GP details recorded at registration?
- Where a mandatory trigger applied, was a letter sent, and within the timescale?
- Where consent was refused, is the discussion, the risk explanation, and the clinical decision all documented?
- Does each letter contain the minimum content, and does it state who is monitoring?
- Was incoming correspondence reviewed by a clinician, or only filed?

Findings go to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` with actions and named owners.

## Why this policy exists — and why it wasn't in the 2022 set

This policy has no predecessor in the source corpus. GP communication appeared only as a paragraph inside the prescribing policy (C07f), which meant it was framed as a prescribing task rather than an organisational duty, and said nothing about what to do when a patient refuses.

The gap matters for three reasons:

1. **It was probed directly at inspection.** The CQC interview included questions about how information reaches the GP and what happens when a patient does not consent. A paragraph inside a prescribing policy is a weak answer to "which policy is that in?"

2. **It is the single most common private-provider criticism.** Where private clinics attract regulatory attention, incomplete GP communication is the recurring theme — a patient on a medicine their GP does not know about, with no one clear on who is monitoring.

3. **Refusal needs a documented route, not silence.** The most defensible position is not "we always tell the GP" (we cannot compel it) but "we ask, we explain the risk, we record the decision, and for defined high-risk treatments we decline to proceed without it." Item 5 and the `[CONFIRM]` in it are where the clinic sets that line, deliberately.

The `[CONFIRM]` at item 5 is the most consequential decision in this document. It should be made by the clinical lead, not by whoever is completing the template.
