## Scope

**Where staff are self-employed, the checking requirements mirror those for employed staff.** A contract for services does not reduce the clinic's duty under Regulation 19.

## POLICY

1. **Recruitment is on merit, through open competition**, and the process identifies the person best suited to the job.

2. **Recruitment decisions are made solely on objective, job-related criteria.**

   > **No recruitment decision may be influenced by a protected characteristic under the Equality Act 2010** — age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, or sexual orientation.
   >
   > **This includes nationality.** Nationality is part of race under section 9 of the Equality Act. Preferring a candidate because they are British, or because they do not require sponsorship, is **direct race discrimination** and is unlawful. See the closing note.

3. **Right to work is checked for every person before they start** — for everyone, in the same way, regardless of how they look, sound or where they say they are from. **Checking only those who appear to be foreign nationals is itself discriminatory.**

4. **No one starts work before pre-employment checks are complete.** `[CONFIRM: whether any conditional start is permitted while a DBS check is outstanding, and if so with what supervision. Default: no unsupervised patient contact until DBS is returned and satisfactory.]`

5. **Reasonable adjustments are made at every stage** of the recruitment process, and for a successful candidate with a disability to undertake the post.

6. **Conflicts of interest are declared.** Anyone involved in recruitment who has a close personal or family relationship with an applicant must declare it as soon as they are aware, and take no part in the decision.

7. **Registration, indemnity and DBS are re-checked periodically, not only at recruitment.** See C010.

## PROCEDURE

### Job description and person specification

- Produced or updated for **every** vacant post
- The job description accurately reflects the post
- The person specification states **essential and desirable** criteria for skills, aptitudes, knowledge and experience, all directly related to the job and applied equally to all applicants
- **Care is taken that criteria do not indirectly discriminate.** A requirement that is not genuinely necessary for the job, but which a protected group is less likely to meet, is indirect discrimination unless it can be objectively justified

### Advertising

Posts are advertised where required — online, in print, or through an agency. `[CONFIRM: standard channels.]`

Adverts must not state or imply a preference relating to any protected characteristic, including nationality or immigration status.

### Shortlisting and interview

- Shortlisting is against the person specification, by `[CONFIRM: role]`, supported by a second person wherever possible
- **Interview questions and structure are consistently applied to all candidates** and based on the person specification
- Questions must not touch on protected characteristics. Do not ask about health, disability, pregnancy or plans to have children before an offer is made — this is restricted by section 60 of the Equality Act
- **Notes recording the salient points are taken by the interviewers**, so candidates can be assessed against the specification
- Interview notes and other records are retained for **`[CONFIRM: minimum 6 months]`** after the process, then securely destroyed
- Unsuccessful candidates are dealt with courteously and receive written or telephone notification

### References

- **Two references**, normally for the selected candidate after interview, **one from the current or most recent employer**
- Requests are structured around the requirements of the job, and the job description is provided
- Many organisations provide only factual references confirming dates and role — **where a reference is factual only, that is noted and the gap is considered rather than ignored**
- **References are verified as genuine.** `[CONFIRM: how — contact details obtained independently rather than taken from the candidate, and the referee's identity and relationship confirmed. A reference from a personal email address supplied by the candidate is not a reference]`
- References are taken up **before** the start date

### Where a candidate requires sponsorship

`[CONFIRM: whether the clinic holds a sponsor licence, and who manages sponsor duties.]`

The candidate's need for sponsorship is a **practical and administrative consideration only** — whether the clinic is able to sponsor, and the timescale involved. **It is not a factor in assessing merit, and it is not a reason to prefer another candidate.**

## What changed from C025 v01 — and why

v01 was a well-written recruitment policy. Its principles — open competition, merit, reasonable adjustments, consistent interviewing, structured references, declaring conflicts of interest — are sound and are retained.

It also contains one paragraph that must be removed immediately.

### 🔴 1. It instructs the clinic to discriminate on nationality

> *"Where there is an international candidate and a candidate from the UK who are equal after the selection process is complete and both meet the requirements of the role, then **the candidate from the UK must take precedence, and be offered the role. This reflects the statutory employment requirements.**"*

**There is no such statutory requirement, and following this instruction would be unlawful.**

Two things are wrong here:

**It describes a rule that was abolished.** The **Resident Labour Market Test** required some employers to advertise to the settled workforce before sponsoring an overseas worker. It was **abolished in December 2020** when the points-based immigration system was introduced. Even when it existed, it was an advertising requirement placed on sponsors — it never required an employer to prefer a British candidate at the point of appointment.

**Nationality discrimination is unlawful.** Under **section 9 of the Equality Act 2010**, race includes **nationality** and national origins. Preferring a candidate because they are British — or rejecting one because they are not, or because they require sponsorship — is **direct race discrimination**. Direct discrimination cannot be justified. There is no exception that makes this lawful, and the fact that a policy instructs it makes the position worse rather than better: a written instruction to discriminate is evidence in itself.

**This requires action beyond adopting v02:**

1. **Withdraw v01 from circulation now.** Do not leave it in the policy folder alongside the new version
2. `[CONFIRM: has any appointment decision been made in reliance on this paragraph? If so, take employment law advice — the limitation period for a discrimination claim runs from the act complained of, and an internal record showing the policy was applied is significant]`
3. `[CONFIRM: has this policy been shared with anyone else — another clinic, a template pack, a colleague? If so, tell them]`
4. Check any job adverts, agency briefs and interview materials for the same wording

This is the single most serious defect found anywhere in the 119-document corpus. Everything else identified has been an out-of-date reference, a missing procedure or an internal contradiction. **This is an instruction to break the law, written into a signed policy, in the document that governs who gets hired.**

It is worth being clear that this is not a reflection on the clinic's practice — it is a paragraph inherited from a template, describing an immigration rule that has since been abolished and that was never a rule about appointment decisions in the first place. But it needs removing today rather than at the next review.

### 2. Pre-employment checks were not specified

v01 refers to HR checklists at Appendices 1 and 2 but the policy itself does not state what must be checked. **Regulation 19 and Schedule 3 specify the information required for every person employed**, and it is the list an inspector works through.

v02 sets out all of it — identity, right to work, DBS at the correct level, registration verified **on the register rather than from a supplied certificate**, indemnity, qualifications, full employment history **with written explanations of gaps**, references, and health.

Two of these are the commonest findings at inspection: **employment history gaps without explanation**, and **DBS obtained at the wrong level**.

### 3. Right to work checks were absent

Given that v01 addressed international candidates at the point of appointment, it is notable that it says nothing about the check that is actually legally required — **verifying right to work before employment begins**.

v02 adds it, with the point that matters most: **the check is done for everyone, in the same way.** Checking only candidates who appear to be foreign nationals is itself race discrimination, and is a common way employers create liability while trying to comply.

### 4. References were requested but not verified

v01 requires two references and structures them well. It does not require anyone to confirm the referee is who they claim to be. `[CONFIRM]` added: contact details obtained independently, not taken from the candidate.

### References

- **"York University HR Dept."** is listed as a reference — another organisation's internal HR function, which is not a citable or checkable source. The same pattern as *"Prof. Sparrow Surgery"* in C01 and C07, and *"Bredon Hill Surgery"* in C01
- The **Equality Act 2010** is mentioned once in the body and **is not in the references** — in a recruitment policy whose principal legal constraint it is
- Regulation 19 is cited as *"Fit and Proper Persons"*. **Regulation 19 is "Fit and proper persons employed"**; the directors' test is **Regulation 5**. Both now cited separately
