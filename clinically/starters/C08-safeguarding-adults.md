## AIM

To ensure that all staff at `[CLINIC NAME]` are alert to suspected or actual abuse of adults at risk who attend the service, are trained to respond appropriately, and know the pathway for alerting the local authority.

Safeguarding adults from harm is a core duty of `[CLINIC NAME]`. The nature of the services we provide means staff may have contact with adults at risk of abuse or neglect.

## POLICY

`[CLINIC NAME]` is committed to the aims of adult safeguarding:

- Prevent harm and reduce the risk of abuse or neglect to adults with care and support needs
- Stop abuse or neglect wherever possible
- Safeguard adults in a way that supports them in making choices and having control over their lives
- Promote an approach that concentrates on improving life for the adults concerned
- Raise public awareness so communities, alongside professionals, play their part in preventing abuse
- Identify and respond to abuse and neglect
- Provide information and support in accessible ways
- Address what has caused the abuse or neglect

To achieve this, `[CLINIC NAME]` will:

- Ensure all managers, employees (paid and unpaid) and students/trainees have access to and are familiar with this policy and their responsibilities within it
- Ensure concerns or allegations of abuse are always taken seriously
- Ensure the Mental Capacity Act 2005 informs any decision-making on behalf of adults at risk unable to make particular decisions for themselves
- Ensure all staff receive training in safeguarding adults at a level relevant to their role
- Ensure patients and, where relevant, their relatives and friends have access to information about how to report concerns
- Ensure there is a **named lead person** to promote adult safeguarding awareness and practice
- Manage services in a way which minimises the risk of abuse occurring
- Work with adults with care and support needs, and other agencies, to end any abuse taking place

**Doing nothing is not an option.** Every member of staff who has reason to believe abuse is or may be taking place has a personal responsibility to act on that information. It must never be assumed that someone else will pass it on.

### The six principles of adult safeguarding

| Principle | What it means | What the person should be able to say |
|---|---|---|
| **Empowerment** | Adults are encouraged to make their own decisions and are supported with information | *"I am consulted about the outcomes I want from the safeguarding process and these directly inform what happens."* |
| **Prevention** | Strategies prevent abuse and neglect, promoting resilience and self-determination | *"I am provided with easily understood information about what abuse is, how to recognise the signs and what I can do to seek help."* |
| **Proportionality** | The least-intrusive response, balanced with the level of risk | *"I am confident that professionals will work in my interest and only get involved as much as needed."* |
| **Protection** | Adults are offered ways to protect themselves, with a coordinated response | *"I am provided with help and support to report abuse."* |
| **Partnership** | Local solutions through services working together | *"I am confident that information will be appropriately shared in a way that takes account of its personal and sensitive nature."* |
| **Accountability** | Accountability and transparency in delivering a safeguarding response | *"I am clear about the roles and responsibilities of all those involved."* |

### Making Safeguarding Personal

Adult safeguarding work is person-led and outcome-focused. `[CLINIC NAME]` will keep the person at the heart of the process, and will strive to understand and support the outcomes *they* want to achieve — not the outcomes we would choose for them.

## DEFINITIONS

### Who is an adult at risk?

A person aged 18 or over who:

- Has needs for care and support (**whether or not those needs are currently being met**), **and**
- Is experiencing, or is at risk of, abuse or neglect, **and**
- Because of those needs is unable to protect themselves against the abuse or neglect or the risk of it

This includes adults with physical, sensory and mental impairments and learning disabilities, whether present from birth or due to advancing age, illness or injury; people with mental illness, dementia or other memory impairment; and people who misuse substances or alcohol where this has led to impaired physical, cognitive or mental health.

### What is abuse?

Abuse may be a single act or repeated acts. The circumstances of the individual must always be considered.

| Type | Includes |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Hitting, slapping, pushing, kicking, misuse of medication, unlawful or inappropriate restraint, inappropriate physical sanctions |
| **Domestic** | An incident or pattern of controlling, coercive or threatening behaviour, violence or abuse by an intimate partner or family member. Includes psychological, physical, sexual, financial and emotional abuse, so-called 'honour'-based violence, forced marriage and female genital mutilation |
| **Sexual** | Rape, sexual assault, or sexual acts to which the adult has not consented, could not consent, or was pressured into consenting |
| **Psychological** | Emotional abuse, threats of harm or abandonment, deprivation of contact, humiliation, blaming, controlling, intimidation, coercion, harassment, verbal abuse, cyber bullying, isolation, unreasonable withdrawal from services or supportive networks |
| **Financial and material** | Theft, fraud, exploitation, pressure in connection with wills, property or inheritance, misuse or misappropriation of property, possessions or benefits |
| **Modern slavery** | Human trafficking, forced labour, domestic servitude |
| **Neglect and acts of omission** | Ignoring medical or physical care needs, failure to provide access to appropriate health, social care or educational services, withholding the necessities of life such as medication, nutrition or heating |
| **Discriminatory** | Abuse based on race, sex, gender, disability, faith, sexual orientation or age; harassment, slurs, hate crime |
| **Organisational** | Neglect and poor practice within an institution or care setting, from one-off incidents to ongoing ill-treatment, arising from structure, policies, processes or practices |
| **Self-neglect** | Neglecting personal hygiene, health or surroundings, including hoarding. A safeguarding response may be appropriate where the impact on wellbeing is substantial |
| **Radicalisation** | Comparable to other forms of exploitation. Vulnerable individuals groomed directly or online. See the Prevent duty below |

Seriousness of harm is not always clear at the point of concern. **All suspicions are approached with an open mind.**

### What is an adult safeguarding enquiry?

Where a local authority believes an adult at risk is experiencing or at risk of abuse or neglect, it must make enquiries or cause others to do so — a duty under **section 42 of the Care Act 2014**. An enquiry establishes whether action is needed to prevent or stop abuse or neglect, and if so by whom. Its objectives are to establish facts, ascertain the adult's views and wishes, assess and address their need for protection and support, decide follow-up action, and enable resolution and recovery.

## PROCEDURE

### If an adult discloses abuse

**Staff should:**

- Assure the person their concerns are taken seriously
- Listen carefully. Stay calm. Get as clear a picture as possible
- Explain the duty to pass the information on to their manager and/or the Safeguarding Lead — **do not promise confidentiality you cannot keep**
- Reassure the person they will be involved in decisions made about them

**Staff should not:**

- Be judgmental or jump to conclusions
- Start to investigate, or ask detailed or probing questions
- Confront the alleged abuser
- Promise to keep it secret

**Staff must:**

1. Act to keep the person safe. **If urgent police presence is needed, or urgent medical assistance, call 999**
2. Inform their line manager and consult the Safeguarding Lead
3. Clearly record what was witnessed or said, in the person's own words where possible, and any actions taken
4. Where a crime may have occurred, be mindful of the need to preserve evidence

**If consulting the Safeguarding Lead would cause undue delay and leave a person at risk, refer to the local authority directly.** The route exists to help, not to gate.

### Timescales

| Stage | By when |
|---|---|
| Immediate risk to life or safety | 999, immediately |
| Concern raised with Safeguarding Lead | Same working day |
| Decision on whether to refer to the local authority | Same working day where risk is immediate; otherwise within `[CONFIRM: default 1 working day]` |
| Referral to local authority | Within `[CONFIRM: default 1 working day]` of the decision |
| **Statutory notification to CQC** | Without delay — see below |
| Internal record completed | Same day as the concern |
| Follow-up with the receiving agency to confirm receipt | Within `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` |
| Reported to the governance meeting | Next meeting |

### Deciding whether to refer to the local authority

Where it is suspected that an adult in need of care and support might be experiencing or at risk of abuse or neglect, this is reported to the relevant local authority — and to the police where a crime is believed or suspected.

**It must never be assumed that someone else will pass on this information.**

Where the person is not well known to `[CLINIC NAME]` and it is unclear whether they have care and support needs, **the local authority is still alerted.** It is for the local authority, not us, to determine eligibility for safeguarding support.

Anyone can raise a safeguarding concern. It is reported to the local authority for the area **where the abuse is taking place or is at risk of taking place** — which may not be the area the clinic is in.

Staff need to establish:

- The current level of risk and immediate steps needed to ensure safety
- The individual's wishes and views, including about sharing information
- Whether, where capacity is in question, the person can make specific decisions about their own protection and understand the safeguarding process
- Where the person lacks capacity, action is taken in line with the Mental Capacity Act 2005 (Appendix B)

**Wherever possible concerns are raised with the person's consent — but consent is not required to raise a safeguarding concern.**

Where a referral has been made, **the responsible clinician follows it up to confirm it has been acted upon.** A referral sent is not a referral received.

### Statutory notification to CQC

Separately from, and in addition to, the local authority referral, `[CLINIC NAME]` must notify **CQC** of any abuse or allegation of abuse concerning a service user, under Regulation 18 of the Care Quality Commission (Registration) Regulations 2009.

`[CONFIRM: who makes CQC statutory notifications — usually the Registered Manager — and who does so in their absence.]`

**These are two separate duties.** Referring to the local authority does not discharge the CQC notification, and notifying CQC does not discharge the referral.

### Sharing information without consent

The priority is the safety and wellbeing of the adult. Where the adult does not want a referral made:

If the decision is to act **without** consent, then unless it is unsafe to do so, the adult is told this is being done and why.

Staff should be vigilant for coercion and for the emotional or psychological impact of abuse, and should:

- Explore the reasons for the objection — what are they worried about?
- Explain the concern and why sharing may matter
- Say with whom information might be shared, and why
- Discuss the benefits of sharing, to them or to others
- Discuss the consequences of not sharing — could someone come to harm?
- Reassure them information will not be shared with anyone who does not need to know
- Reassure them they are not alone and support is available

The decision-making process is **carefully recorded** either way.

### Decision not to share information

Information may be withheld only where the person **has capacity** to make that decision, does not want it shared, **and all of the following apply**:

- Nobody else is at risk
- No serious crime has been or may be committed
- The alleged abuser has no care and support needs
- No staff are implicated
- No coercion or duress is suspected
- The public interest in disclosure does not outweigh the public interest in confidentiality
- The risk is not high enough to warrant a MARAC referral
- No other legal authority has requested the information

**Any decision not to share is taken with the Safeguarding Lead, never alone**, and the responsible clinician then:

- Supports the adult to weigh the risks and benefits of different options
- Ensures they are aware of the level of risk and possible outcomes
- Offers support to build confidence and self-esteem where needed
- Agrees and records the level of risk the adult is taking
- Records the reasons for not intervening or sharing
- **Regularly reviews the situation** — a decision not to refer is reviewed at `[CONFIRM: default every 3 months, or sooner on any change]`, not left standing indefinitely
- Tries to build trust to enable the adult to better protect themselves

The risk *of* sharing is also considered. In domestic abuse or hate crime, sharing can increase risk to the adult.

### Domestic abuse

Where domestic abuse is suspected or disclosed:

- Never raise it in the presence of the person accompanying the patient
- Ensure the patient can be seen alone — a companion who will not leave the room is itself a concern
- Do not give the patient written information they may be found carrying
- Consider a MARAC referral where risk is high `[CONFIRM: local MARAC referral route]`
- Record in a way that does not put the patient at risk if the record is seen

### Prevent duty

Where there is a concern that a person is vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism, staff raise it with the Safeguarding Lead, who considers a **Channel** referral. `[CONFIRM: local Prevent/Channel referral contact.]` Prevent is a safeguarding duty, not a policing one — the threshold is vulnerability, not criminality.

### Historic abuse

Abuse that took place when a person was under 18 is not an adult safeguarding issue but may, depending on the concern, be a **child safeguarding issue — irrespective of how old that person is now.** Children's Social Care may need to be informed where the person who caused harm may pose a continuing risk to children.

Adults disclosing historical childhood abuse can be advised that this is a crime and that they may still report it to the police if they wish.

### Reporting within `[CLINIC NAME]`

In all cases the clinician seeks advice from and discusses the concern with the Safeguarding Lead.

Where a non-clinical member of staff (for example reception) has concerns about a patient about to attend a consultation, **they must see the clinician first**, so the clinician can assess the patient in light of that information.

### Recording

Concerns are fully documented by the first person to report the suspected abuse, and at all subsequent stages by those involved.

Concerns are recorded on the **Adult Safeguarding Form** (Appendix C), completed as soon as possible after a concern is identified — **whether or not it is later substantiated.** The local authority will have its own forms, usually online, but the `[CLINIC NAME]` form is completed first so the notes are contemporaneous.

The responsible clinician records ongoing care and wellbeing during any enquiry, and records safeguarding supervision. **The outcome of the enquiry is clearly documented in the patient record.**

### Abuse within `[CLINIC NAME]`

Where the allegation relates to care provided by `[CLINIC NAME]`, an internal investigation is launched. In addition:

- The local authority referral and CQC statutory notification are made in the normal way — **an internal investigation does not replace either**
- The investigation is conducted by someone not involved in the care in question
- Where a member of staff is implicated, `[CONFIRM: role]` considers whether suspension or restriction of duties is needed while the matter is investigated, and records the reasoning
- A referral to the **DBS** and/or the relevant professional regulator (GPhC, GMC, NMC) is considered and the decision recorded
- Duty of candour obligations are considered under C021

### Supporting staff

Involvement in identifying or reporting suspected abuse can be difficult. The Registered Manager and Safeguarding Lead offer feedback and support to the member of staff.

**All staff facing an accusation or under suspicion will be supported by a trained professional.**

## What changed from C08 v01 — and why

The 2022 policy was well-constructed and substantially correct. The Care Act framework, the six principles, Making Safeguarding Personal, the abuse typology and the information-sharing test have all been kept largely as written. Seven changes were made.

**1. Unfinished template fields were found and made impossible to leave blank.** The signed 2022 policy still contained:

- `"abuse of adults who attend Xxxx"` — in the AIM
- `"not well known to Xxxx"` and `"it is for the local authority, rather than Xxxx, to determine"`
- `"The INSERT LOCAL S/S Multi-Agency Adult Safeguarding Policy"` — twice

This is the single most important finding in the document. The CQC interview specifically tested **"how have you personalised these?"** — and an unreplaced `Xxxx` in the safeguarding policy answers that question in the worst possible way. The new version carries a warning at the head and marks every such field as `[CONFIRM]` so it cannot be missed.

**2. Added the CQC statutory notification duty.** v01 covered referral to the local authority thoroughly but never mentioned notifying **CQC under Regulation 18 of the Registration Regulations**. These are two separate legal duties and doing one does not discharge the other. This was a genuine gap, not a wording issue.

**3. Named individuals, and a deputy, are now required.** v01 referred to "the Adult Safeguarding Lead" throughout without a name. A role with no name attached is not a route a worried receptionist can use. A deputy is now required, and there is a `[CONFIRM]` forcing an external contact where the Safeguarding Lead and Registered Manager are the same person — the normal situation in a small clinic, and a single point of failure.

**4. Added timescales.** v01 said what to do but never when. "As soon as possible" is not auditable and does not answer an inspector.

**5. Added domestic abuse handling and the Prevent/Channel route.** v01 defined domestic abuse and radicalisation in the typology but gave no practical guidance for either. The domestic abuse section addresses the situation that actually presents in clinic — a patient who cannot be seen alone.

**6. Strengthened "abuse within the clinic".** v01 said only that an internal investigation would be launched. Added: the external duties still apply, the investigator must be independent of the care, and DBS and professional-regulator referrals must be considered and the decision recorded.

**7. References updated.** The **Intercollegiate Document** was cited in its 2018 first edition; a later edition exists and the citation is now a `[CONFIRM]` so the clinic records the edition it actually holds. Added the Domestic Abuse Act 2021, Modern Slavery Act 2015, and the Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015. Removed the "Tavistock & Portman NHS Foundation" reference, which was unattributed and did not identify a specific document.

One thing was deliberately **not** changed: the eight-part test for when information may be withheld. It is drawn from SCIE guidance, it is correct, and it is better expressed in your original than most versions of it.
