## Scope

All individuals in the employ of `[CLINIC NAME]` — *employ* meaning any person employed, self-employed, a volunteer, working under practising privileges, or under contract of service.

## AIM

To ensure remote consultations are safe and confidential for the patient, and that the consulting clinician is able to conduct a satisfactory consultation enabling a suitable assessment and treatment plan.

---

## POLICY

1. **The professional standards are identical.** GMC and GPhC standards apply as fully to a remote consultation as to a face-to-face one. Every clinician must:

   - Obtain adequate consent
   - Ensure confidentiality
   - Keep contemporaneous notes
   - Make an appropriate assessment of the patient's symptoms
   - **Communicate with the patient's GP to ensure continuity** — particularly where the patient is registered elsewhere (see C41)
   - Have appropriate indemnity **covering remote work specifically** `[CONFIRM: verified, not assumed — some policies exclude or limit remote consultation]`

2. **Remote consultation is offered where it is clinically appropriate**, considered case by case. It is part of patient-centred care, not a lesser alternative.

3. > **If you need to examine the patient, you need to examine the patient.**
   >
   > Not every problem can be dealt with remotely. **Recognising that a remote assessment is inadequate, and saying so, is good practice — not a failure of the service.** No clinician is under pressure, from targets, from convenience or from the patient, to complete a consultation remotely that should be face to face.

4. **A clinician who is uncomfortable or unsure about conducting remote consultations is not obliged to carry them out.**

5. `[CONFIRM: which services and conditions may be delivered remotely, and which may not. This is the scope decision — see C015. Default exclusions: any consultation requiring physical examination; first prescription of a controlled drug; anything requiring an intimate examination.]`

## PROCEDURE

### Platform

`[CONFIRM: the approved platform(s). v01 named Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Skype and FaceTime. Consumer platforms are not automatically acceptable for clinical use.]`

For each platform in use, the clinic must be able to show:

- **End-to-end encryption or equivalent security**
- A **data processing agreement**, and where the data is processed and stored
- That the **healthcare/enterprise configuration** is used, not the free consumer tier
- `[CONFIRM: whether a DPIA has been completed for the platform — required where processing is likely to result in high risk, which processing special category health data by video generally is]`

Clinicians must be familiar with adjusting picture quality and sound on their own equipment, and with helping the patient optimise theirs.

> **Be alert to the risk of missing significant information** because of lag, sound and picture out of synch, or a frozen image. `[CONFIRM: what to do when the connection is inadequate. Default: stop, and either restart, switch to telephone, or convert to a face-to-face appointment — and record that this happened.]`

### Consultation setup

- **Location** — where working from home, use a neutral background so the patient is not distracted by domestic surroundings
- **Dress professionally**
- **Privacy** — ensure you will not be interrupted and background sound is not intrusive. `[CONFIRM: the clinician must be alone and unable to be overheard — the same standard as a consulting room]`
- **Framing** — landscape rather than portrait; it gives a more comfortable separation. Position yourself centrally; the patient should see your facial expression without straining, and preferably your hands
- **Lighting** — face well-lit, no bright light behind you

### Verifying identity

**The same standard as face-to-face — see C039.**

For a first remote consultation with a patient not previously known:

1. Admin staff ask the patient to have **two forms of ID ready** to show at the start.
2. **One must carry a photograph clearly matching the patient**; the other must confirm the address held on record.
3. **The clinician records having seen them, what they were, and that they are satisfied the patient is genuine.**

`[CONFIRM: acceptable documents. v01 stated that documents "likely to have been posted to the patient, such as a bank statement" are acceptable but that "bills will not meet this criteria" — which directly contradicts C039, where a utility bill is listed as an acceptable identifier. Decide once, and make both policies say the same thing.]`

**Where a patient cannot provide acceptable evidence** — temporary residents, travellers, young people living with parents:

- An authorised member of staff who knows the patient well enough to confirm they are who they say they are may vouch for them, judged case by case
- Failing that, identity may be confirmed using information already in the medical record. **The questions asked must not themselves disclose confidential information to someone whose identity is not yet verified**
- `[CONFIRM: where identity cannot be verified, which treatments are not provided — see C039]`

### Confidentiality and who else is in the room

**The clinician cannot see who is present off-camera.**

- **Ask, at the start of every remote consultation, whether the patient is alone and able to speak freely** — and record the answer
- Where someone else is present, establish who they are and confirm the patient is content
- Where the patient's answers change, or they seem constrained, act on it

### Coercion

`[CLINIC NAME]` is alert to the risk that a patient may be **under duress** to give someone access to their personal and confidential information, or to request a particular treatment, during a remote consultation.

Clinicians must be prepared to raise and address this. **Where the clinician considers it a safeguarding issue, they discuss it with the safeguarding lead** — see C08 and C032.

`[CONFIRM: how a patient can signal they are not safe to speak. Default: the clinician offers a closed-question route — "is it convenient to talk about this now?" — and a named way to make contact later.]`

### Safeguarding

Safeguarding systems must reflect the scope of practice and the type of online service. Consider:

- Is the service provided in **real time**?
- Are **children** being treated?
- Across what **geographical area** is care delivered? `[CONFIRM — this determines which local authority a safeguarding referral goes to, and it will not always be the clinic's own]`

**Where children are treated:** take reasonable steps to be sure any adult involved has **parental responsibility** and can consent on the child's behalf — see C032. `[CLINIC NAME]`'s safeguarding policies apply identically to remote consultations.

### Consent and mental capacity

The **Mental Capacity Act 2005** applies online exactly as it does face to face. Consent is sought and recorded in line with **C05** — including consent to being seen remotely, and the fact that it was explained what a remote consultation can and cannot assess.

### Intimate examinations

**Not conducted remotely.** Where an intimate examination is indicated, a face-to-face appointment is arranged. See C01.

### Recording

`[CONFIRM: whether consultations are recorded. Default: NO. Where YES: separate explicit consent is required (C05), the patient is told who will have access and for how long it is kept, the recording is stored securely as part of the clinical record, and it is never made on a personal device. Patients recording the consultation themselves is a separate matter — they are entitled to, and it is good practice not to object.]`

### Prescribing remotely

**Remote prescribing carries additional requirements — see C07f and GMC/GPhC guidance.** The clinician must:

- Be satisfied there is an adequate assessment on which to prescribe
- Have verified identity and age (C039)
- **Establish the patient's full medication history and allergies**, and not rely solely on self-report where corroboration is available
- Consider whether the medicine is appropriate to prescribe without physical examination
- **Notify the GP** — this is more important remotely, not less, because it is often the only independent corroboration that the patient is who they say they are (C41)
- `[CONFIRM: medicines not prescribed remotely. Default: controlled drugs on first prescription; any medicine requiring physical examination or baseline observations that have not been obtained]`

### Records

Every remote consultation records: that it was remote and by what platform · that identity was verified and how · that the patient consented to a remote consultation · **that the patient confirmed they were alone and able to speak freely** · the assessment and its limitations · and any decision that a face-to-face appointment was needed.

### Training

Clinical staff are given time to become familiar with the platform and process. **Admin staff are trained too** — explaining that ID will be needed, and helping the patient choose a suitable private place for the consultation. `[CONFIRM: who delivers training and how often.]`

## MONITORING COMPLIANCE

`[CONFIRM: audit owner and frequency. Default: clinical lead, six-monthly.]`

- Was identity verified and recorded for every first remote consultation?
- **Is there a record that the patient was asked whether they were alone and able to speak freely?**
- Was consent to remote consultation recorded?
- Were there consultations that should have been face to face — and were any converted?
- Was the GP notified where required?
- Are platforms on the approved list, correctly configured, with a DPIA and DPA in place?
- Is indemnity confirmed as covering remote work for every clinician?
- Were any coercion or safeguarding concerns identified, and how were they handled?

## What changed from C040 v01 — and why

**v01 is the strongest document in the corpus.** It is the only one that reads as though it was written recently, by someone who had actually conducted remote consultations rather than adapted a template. Several passages are better than the published guidance they summarise:

- ***"If you need to examine the patient, you need to examine the patient."***
- The **coercion** section — the risk that a patient is under duress off-camera. Many current remote consultation policies still omit this
- The **identity verification fallbacks** — vouching, then verification from the medical record, with the warning that the questions must not themselves disclose confidential information to an unverified person
- The practical detail on **framing, lighting and background**, and the point that lag and freezing can cause a clinician to miss something
- **"If a clinician is uncomfortable or unsure about conducting remote consultations, they are not obliged to carry them out"**

All retained, most of it verbatim. Four changes.

### 1. It contradicts C039 on what counts as ID

C040 states that acceptable address evidence includes documents *"likely to have been posted to the patient, such as a bank statement"*, and then:

> ***"Bills will not meet this criteria."***

**C039 Patient ID Verification** lists among its core identifiers:

> *"Utility bill or similar with name and address"*

One policy accepts a utility bill; the other explicitly rejects it. A member of staff following the clinic's policies correctly could reach either answer, and the two documents are both live.

This is the first **cross-document** contradiction found — the previous ones (C032 on under-18s, C04 on deadlines) were internal. It is also the harder kind to detect by reading, because neither document is wrong on its own face. `[CONFIRM]` added to both.

### 2. There is no pharmacy regulator in it

v01 cites the GMC three times, the RCGP twice, NHS and CQC guidance — and **not the GPhC**.

For remote consultations this matters more than anywhere else in the corpus, because the GPhC has **guidance written specifically for this**: *Guidance for registered pharmacies providing pharmacy services at a distance, including on the internet*. It is the single most relevant published document for a pharmacist-led online service, and it was absent.

The policy also states that *"**Doctors** must: obtain adequate patient consent…"*, and that *"the **doctor** will then record having seen the ID."* Fifth instance of this pattern.

### 3. Platforms were named without assurance

> *"Ahmeys Clinic uses Zoom, & Microsoft teams as acceptable platforms"* — and the AIM refers to *"video, Skype, FaceTime and Zoom."*

Zoom and Teams both have healthcare-appropriate configurations, but **the consumer tier is not the same product as the enterprise one**, and FaceTime and Skype raise questions that v01 does not address: where the data is processed, whether there is a data processing agreement, and whether a **DPIA** has been done — which processing special category health data by video generally requires.

Added, along with what to do when the connection is inadequate mid-consultation, which v01 identifies as a risk but gives no instruction on.

### 4. Remote prescribing was not covered

v01 references GMC prescribing guidance in its reference list but the body of the policy does not address prescribing at all — no requirement to establish medication history and allergies, no consideration of which medicines are unsuitable for remote prescribing, and no link to GP notification.

**For an online prescribing service this is the highest-risk activity in the policy**, and it was the gap. Added, with a `[CONFIRM]` for the medicines the clinic will not prescribe remotely.

### Additions

- **"Are you alone and able to speak freely?"** asked and **recorded** every time. v01 identifies the risk and says a check "can be undertaken"; making it a recorded step is what turns it into a control
- **Intimate examinations are not conducted remotely** — stated explicitly, linking to C01
- **Recording of consultations** — absent from v01, including the point that a patient recording the consultation themselves is entitled to
- **Geographical scope determines the safeguarding referral route** — v01 asks the right question but does not draw the conclusion, which is that the relevant local authority is the patient's, not the clinic's
- **Indemnity verified as covering remote work**, since some policies limit it
