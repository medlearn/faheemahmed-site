### The four prohibited behaviours

| | |
|---|---|
| **Direct discrimination** | Treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic. **Cannot be justified** (except limited exceptions for age) |
| **Indirect discrimination** | A provision, criterion or practice applied to everyone that puts people sharing a characteristic at a particular disadvantage. Unlawful **unless objectively justified** as a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim |

| **Victimisation** | Treating someone badly because they have complained about discrimination, or supported someone who has |

1. **`[CLINIC NAME]` opposes all forms of discrimination** against staff and patients on the grounds of any protected characteristic.

## DIGNITY AT WORK

`[CLINIC NAME]` is committed to a working environment free of harassment and bullying, where everyone — staff and patients — is treated with dignity and respect.

**Some harassment is unlawful discrimination, and serious harassment may be a criminal offence.**

**Bullying** is offensive, intimidating, malicious or insulting behaviour, or an abuse or misuse of power, meant to undermine, humiliate or injure the person on the receiving end. Examples include picking on someone, setting them up to fail, or making threats or comments about their job security without good reason.

**Harassment** is unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic which:

- has the **purpose** of violating a person's dignity, or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating or offensive environment for them; **or**
- is reasonably considered by that person to have that **effect** — **even if the effect was not intended**

Examples include: physical conduct from unwelcome touching to serious assault · unwelcome sexual advances · demeaning comments about appearance · unwelcome jokes or comments of a sexual or racial nature, or about age · obscene gestures · the open display of material with sexual or racial overtones, **even if not directed at any particular person**.

### How to judge it

- **Conduct may be harassment whether or not the person intended to offend.** Something intended as a joke may still offend
- **Everyone has the right to decide what behaviour is acceptable to them** and to have their feelings respected
- **Behaviour any reasonable person would realise is likely to offend is harassment without the recipient having to say so in advance** — for example sexual touching
- For behaviour where this is less obvious — certain banter, flirting, asking someone for a drink after work — **first-time conduct that unintentionally causes offence is not harassment, but becomes harassment if it continues after the person has made clear it is unwelcome**
- **A single incident can be harassment if it is sufficiently serious**

> **Third-party harassment.** Staff are also protected from harassment by patients, visitors and contractors. `[CONFIRM: what the clinic does where a patient harasses a member of staff. Default: the member of staff may withdraw from the situation immediately without needing permission; the incident is reported under C017; and the clinic decides whether to continue treating that patient — while never withholding urgent care.]`

## RAISING A CONCERN

**Informally.** The person may not know their behaviour is unwelcome. You may feel able to approach them yourself, or with support from someone else here — telling them what you find offensive and asking that it stop.

**Formally.** Where an informal approach does not resolve it, or the situation is too serious for that, make a formal complaint under the **grievance procedure**. Where the concern is about risk to others rather than to you personally, use **S03 Speaking Up**.

**Patients** raising concerns about discrimination use the complaints procedure (C04).

### What happens

- All complaints are **investigated promptly**
- Where appropriate, the parties may be **separated temporarily** while the investigation proceeds. This may include transfer or suspension of the alleged perpetrator **on full pay**, and **does not constitute disciplinary action**
- **You have the right to be accompanied by a colleague of your choice** at any meeting
- You are kept informed of progress and of the outcome
- Complaints are treated **sensitively and confidentially** to the maximum extent possible
- **You have the right not to be victimised for complaining in good faith, even if the complaint is not upheld.** Making a complaint you know to be untrue may lead to disciplinary action

## What changed from S01 v01 — and why

The substantial part of v01 — the **Dignity at Work** section — is genuinely good. Its treatment of harassment is more careful than most policies of its length: intent is irrelevant, the recipient decides what is acceptable, some conduct is obviously offensive without warning while other conduct becomes harassment only if it continues after being challenged, and a single incident can suffice. All retained close to verbatim, as is the complaints procedure and the protection against victimisation.

Three changes.

### 1. The list of protected characteristics was wrong in both places it appeared

The **AIM** of v01 states the policy exists to ensure people are treated equally *"regardless of faith, age, sexual orientation, physical or mental capability."*

**Four characteristics. The Act has nine.** Missing from that sentence: race, sex, pregnancy and maternity, marriage and civil partnership, and gender reassignment.

The **POLICY** section lists more, but still not the statutory set: *"gender, gender reassignment, sexual orientation, marital/civil partnership status, pregnancy/maternity, religion, religious belief, age, disability or race."* It uses **"gender"** where the Act says **"sex"**, and splits religion from religious belief where the Act treats *"religion or belief"* as one characteristic including lack of belief.

An equality policy that misstates the protected characteristics cannot be relied on by the people applying it — and here the two lists in the same document do not even agree with each other.

### 2. The Equality Act 2010 was not cited

v01's references are:

- *"CQC: Essential standards of quality and safety"*
- *"GMC: Equality and Diversity 2011 to 2014"*
- *"NHS Employers.org"*

**The Equality Act 2010 does not appear**, in a policy whose entire subject it is.

The two named documents are both out of date. **CQC's *Essential Standards of Quality and Safety* was replaced by the Fundamental Standards in 2015.** And the GMC document is a **time-limited scheme that expired in 2014** — cited here as current, and bundled into the policy as **Appendix One**, with the instruction that *"Clinicians and all other staff will follow the GMC guidelines on equality and diversity."* Staff are being directed to follow a scheme that ended eleven years before this review.

### 3. It covered employment far better than it covered patients

v01 is essentially an employment policy with two sentences about patients. For a CQC-registered provider, the patient-facing half carries **Regulation 10** and is what an inspector examines.

Added:

- **Reasonable adjustments as an anticipatory duty** — with a `[CONFIRM]` that asks the clinic to be honest about what the premises genuinely cannot do and what happens instead. The real inspection identified wheelchair access as a live gap, and an honest workaround is a far stronger answer than an aspiration the building cannot support
- **Accessible communication** — interpreters including BSL, accessible formats, and recording a patient's communication needs so they are met at every contact rather than rediscovered each time
- **The four prohibited behaviours** — direct and indirect discrimination, harassment and victimisation — with the point that direct discrimination **cannot be justified**, which is the distinction that matters when someone argues a decision was reasonable in the circumstances
- **Third-party harassment** — staff harassed by patients or visitors. v01 protects staff from each other and patients from staff, but not staff from patients, which in a small clinic is the situation most likely to arise
