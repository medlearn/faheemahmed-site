## AIM

To ensure that all health records, paper and electronic, are accurate, contemporaneous, complete and legible, so that they support safe care, demonstrate the reasoning behind clinical decisions, and stand as a reliable account of what happened.

> **The record is written for the next clinician, and for the patient — not for the person writing it.** If a colleague picking it up cold cannot tell what was found, what was decided and why, the entry has not done its job.

---

## POLICY

1. **Every entry is attributable, dated and timed.** The name and role of the person making it must be identifiable. Electronic entries are made under the individual's own login — **never under another person's**.

2. **Entries are contemporaneous.** Where that is not possible, the entry is made at the earliest opportunity and **records both the time of the event and the time of writing**.

3. **Records are legible and in plain language.** Abbreviations are avoided; only those on the approved abbreviation list may be used. `[CONFIRM: who maintains the approved abbreviation list.]`

4. **Records are factual and objective.** They record what was observed, what was said (in the patient's words where it matters), what was decided and why. They do not contain speculation about a patient, judgemental language, or comments about colleagues.

5. **Records are never altered to conceal.** An error is corrected by a **dated, timed, attributed amendment** that leaves the original visible — a single line through paper entries, an audit-logged addendum in electronic ones. **Original entries are never deleted, overwritten or obscured.**

6. **A record of what was not done, and why, is part of the record.** A decision not to treat, not to refer, not to prescribe, or not to share information with a GP is a clinical decision and is documented with its reasoning.

7. **Records are retained for a defined period and then securely destroyed.** Indefinite retention is not permitted — see the retention schedule below.

## PROCEDURE

### What every patient record must contain

**Identification and demographics**

- Clinic record number or other unique reference
- Full name, address, postcode and telephone numbers
- Date of birth and gender
- **GP details, or a recorded statement that the patient has no registered GP** (see C41)
- Next of kin or emergency contact, where the patient has given one, **and the patient's recorded wishes about what may be shared with them** (see C03)
- Contact preferences — how the patient has agreed to be contacted

**Clinical**

- **Allergies and adverse drug reactions — recorded prominently and easily found**, or an explicit "no known drug allergies". A blank allergy field is not the same as no allergies
- Presenting problem and reason for attendance
- Source of referral, where there was one
- Present and past medical history
- Current medication, including over-the-counter and herbal preparations
- Family history and social circumstances where relevant
- Findings on examination, including relevant negatives
- Working diagnosis or impression, with the degree of uncertainty
- Investigations requested, and their results, with evidence they were **seen and acted upon**
- Treatment plan, including what the patient was advised to do if things worsen (safety-netting)
- **Consent** — the discussion, not merely that a form was signed (see C05)
- Prescriptions issued, by a qualified prescriber, with drug, dose, quantity and duration
- Monitoring required, its frequency, and **who is responsible for it**
- Correspondence sent and received, including GP letters
- Name and role of every clinician involved
- Follow-up arranged, or discharge from the service

### Entries must also record

- Telephone contacts and out-of-hours contacts, with the same standard as face-to-face
- Remote consultations — that the consultation was remote, that the patient consented to it, and that identity was confirmed (see C040)
- Chaperone offered, accepted or declined, and the chaperone's name (see C01)
- Interpreter used, and that they were professional rather than a family member
- Any incident or near miss affecting the patient. **The incident form is completed separately and does not replace the clinical entry** — but the clinical record notes what happened to the patient, not the investigation

### Who may make an entry

Only authorised personnel. `[CONFIRM: role]` maintains a **Record of Authorised Signatures** — a current register of the signatures and initials of everyone who may write in the record, including bank, locum and agency staff, so any entry can be verified.

`[CONFIRM: who may amend a record, and who may not. Default: a clinician may add an addendum to their own entry at any time; nobody may amend another person's entry — they add their own, cross-referenced.]`

### Corrections and addenda

| Situation | What to do |
|---|---|
| Error spotted in a paper entry | Single line through, so the original stays readable. Write the correction, sign, date and time it. **Never use correction fluid, never obliterate** |
| Error spotted in an electronic entry | Add a dated, attributed addendum. Do not overwrite. Where the system permits editing, the audit trail must retain the original |
| Information arriving after the entry was written | Addendum, dated and timed at the time of writing |
| Entry made in the wrong patient's record | Report as a data breach under C03 **and** correct in both records with an explanatory addendum |
| Patient disputes the accuracy of their record | Handle under the **right to rectification** (UK GDPR Article 16) via C028. Where the clinician's opinion is disputed but not factually wrong, the patient's statement of disagreement is added to the record; the original entry stays |

**Retention periods apply to the record regardless of format.** Electronic and paper records are retained for the same period and destroyed on the same schedule.

| Child or young person | Until their **25th birthday**, or **26th** if the entry was made when they were 17, or **8 years after death** if they died before 18 — whichever is longer |
| Patient who has died | **8 years** after death |
| Mental health records | `[CONFIRM: against the current Code]` |
| GP records | `[CONFIRM: against the current Code — the period differs from other clinical records]` |
| Records subject to litigation, a complaint, an investigation, or a public inquiry | **Retained until the matter is fully concluded**, regardless of the schedule. `[CONFIRM: who places and lifts a retention hold]` |

**Indefinite retention is not permitted.** UK GDPR Article 5(1)(e) requires that personal data is kept no longer than necessary. `[CONFIRM: the destruction schedule — who reviews records reaching the end of their retention period, and how often. Default: annual review by the Registered Manager.]`

### Destruction

1. `[CONFIRM: role]` reviews records due for destruction and confirms no retention hold applies.
2. Paper records are destroyed by a professional confidential-waste service, or by **cross-cut shredder**. Never in general waste.
3. Electronic records are deleted from live systems **and from backups**, in line with the backup retention cycle.
4. **A destruction log is kept**, recording what was destroyed, when, by whom and by what method. `[CONFIRM: who holds the destruction log.]` The log is retained permanently — it is the evidence that destruction was authorised rather than accidental.

## What changed from C09 v01 — and why

v01 was **898 words** — the shortest document in the corpus, covering the subject that CQC assesses under Regulation 17 and that both the GMC and GPhC treat as a personal professional standard. Its list of what a record must contain was sound and is largely retained. Five changes.

### 1. It instructed indefinite retention of electronic records

v01 states, twice:

> *"Electronic records are to be retained indefinitely (ref. NHS Choices)."*
>
> *"Electronic patient records must not be destroyed or deleted for the foreseeable future (ref: NHS Choices)."*

**This conflicts with the storage limitation principle** — UK GDPR Article 5(1)(e) requires personal data to be kept no longer than is necessary for the purpose. A standing instruction never to delete electronic records is not a cautious position; it is a documented policy of indefinite retention of special category health data.

The attribution is also unusable: *NHS Choices* was a public-facing health information website, not a records management authority, and it was replaced by NHS.uk in 2018.

v02 applies **one retention schedule to the record regardless of format**, which is the correct principle.

### 2. The References cite an Act that does not exist

v01 lists **"Data Protection Act 1989"** among its references. There is no such Act. The body of the policy then cites the **Data Protection Act 1998**, which was repealed and replaced in 2018.

So the shortest policy in the corpus manages to cite the wrong data protection statute twice, in two different wrong ways, in a document whose entire subject is the handling of health records.

### 3. The retention schedule came from a superseded source

The table in v01 reproduces the older Department of Health retention schedule. The operative source is now the **Records Management Code of Practice** published by NHS England, which changed several periods.

Rather than substitute a schedule that may itself age, v02 keeps the common cases and marks the others `[CONFIRM]` against the current Code. Retention periods are exactly the kind of content that must be verified at adoption rather than trusted from a template — which is also why they are a strong candidate for the governed library's review cadence.

### 4. There was no procedure for correcting a record

v01 described what must go into a record and never addressed what happens when something in it is wrong. This is a significant omission: **improperly altered records are among the most serious findings a clinician can face**, professionally and legally, and the rule (strike through, never obliterate, never overwrite, always attribute) is simple enough that its absence is the only reason anyone gets it wrong.

Added alongside it: the **right to rectification** under UK GDPR, and the correct handling of a patient who disputes a clinical opinion rather than a fact — the opinion stays, the patient's disagreement is recorded beside it.

### 5. It was written for an inpatient unit

v01 covers *"admission to discharge"*, *"the patient's stay"*, and a *"register of signatures and initials of permanent, bank and agency members of **nursing staff**"* — in a clinic whose records are made by prescribers.

### Additions

- **Allergies recorded positively**, including an explicit "no known drug allergies" — a blank field is ambiguous and the ambiguity is dangerous
- **Evidence that results were seen and acted on** — the single commonest record keeping failure that causes harm
- **Safety-netting** — what the patient was told to do if things worsen
- **Recording what was *not* done, and why** — a decision not to prescribe or not to refer is a clinical decision, and an undocumented one is indistinguishable from an oversight
- **Chaperone, interpreter and remote-consultation entries**
- **Backup restoration testing** — an untested backup is an assumption rather than a control, and this is a business continuity dependency too (theme 22)
- **A destruction log**, retained permanently — without it there is no way to distinguish authorised destruction from lost records
- **Retention holds** for records under complaint, litigation or investigation, which override the schedule
