## AIM

To provide a safe, comfortable environment in which patients and staff can be confident that best practice is followed, and where the dignity and safety of everyone is protected.

---

## POLICY

1. **Every patient is entitled to a chaperone** for any consultation, examination or procedure where they want one. **A chaperone is offered — the patient does not have to ask.**

2. **A clinician may also request a chaperone** at any stage of a consultation, and may decline to proceed without one.

3. **A chaperone must be a trained member of staff.**

   > **The GMC advises against using family members or friends as chaperones**, because a person close to the patient is not impartial and cannot fulfil the protective function the role exists for. A relative may of course be present **in addition** to a chaperone, if the patient wishes — but they do not replace one.

4. **The offer, and the patient's response, are always recorded** — including where a chaperone was declined.

5. **Where no trained chaperone is available and one is needed, the examination does not go ahead.** `[CONFIRM: what happens instead. Default: the appointment is rescheduled, or the patient is referred, and the reason is recorded. Proceeding without a chaperone because none was available is not an acceptable outcome for either party.]`

6. **A chaperone is a safeguard for the clinician as well as the patient.** It is the only reliable protection against an allegation arising from a private examination.

---

## THE CHAPERONE'S ROLE

There is no single definition, and the role varies with the needs of the patient, the clinician and the examination. It may include:

- Emotional comfort and reassurance to the patient
- Assisting with the examination
- Assisting the patient to undress or dress
- Being alert to any sign of distress
- **Protecting the clinician against allegations**

> **A chaperone is not an interpreter.** Where interpretation is needed, a **professional interpreter** is used in addition — see C05. Where both are present, both are recorded in the notes.

## PROCEDURE — intimate examinations

> **An intimate examination** includes examination of the breasts, genitalia or rectum, and **any examination where it is necessary to touch or be close to the patient in a way they may find intrusive.** Whether an examination feels intimate is determined by the patient, not by the clinician.

**Chaperones are relevant to any intimate examination, regardless of the gender of the patient or of the clinician.**

1. **Establish there is a genuine clinical need** for the examination, and discuss it with the patient.
2. **Explain why it is necessary**, what it will involve, and give the patient the opportunity to ask questions.
3. **Offer a chaperone.** The chaperone will normally be the same sex as the patient, and **the patient may decline a particular individual** for any reason without having to explain it.
4. **Obtain and record consent** before the examination begins — see C05.
5. **Record in the notes:** the name and role of the chaperone (e.g. HCA), what they were present for, and **the times they were present** (e.g. arrived 11.05, left 11.20).
6. **Where a chaperone is declined, record that the offer was made and declined.**
7. Once the chaperone is in the room, **introduce them by name.**
8. **Allow the patient privacy to undress and dress.** Use drapes or curtains to maintain dignity. **There should be no undue delay between the patient undressing and the examination beginning.**
9. **Explain what is being done at each stage**, the outcome when complete, and what is proposed next.
10. **Keep discussion relevant. Avoid personal comment** of any kind.
11. The chaperone stands where they **can actually observe the examination.** A chaperone positioned behind a curtain, or facing away, is not performing the role and provides no protection to either party.
12. **Be prepared to stop at any point at the patient's request**, without question or persuasion.

## REMOTE CONSULTATIONS

`[CONFIRM: the clinic's position — this was absent from v01 and matters for any service offering video consultations.]`

Where a consultation is conducted remotely and any examination or exposure is involved:

- The same offer of a chaperone applies
- **The patient is asked to confirm they are alone and in a private place, and this is recorded**
- The clinician confirms whether anyone else is present off-camera
- **Where the clinician judges that remote assessment is not adequate for an intimate examination — which will usually be the case — a face-to-face appointment is arranged.** See C040
- Nothing is recorded or screenshotted without separate, explicit consent — see C05

## TRAINING

Anyone acting as a chaperone must be trained **before** doing so. `[CONFIRM: what the training covers and who delivers it.]` It must include:

- What to observe, and where to stand
- Maintaining the patient's dignity
- Confidentiality
- **What to do if they are concerned by what they see** — including how to raise it under S03, and that they are expected to
- Recording their presence

## What changed from C01 v01 — and why

v01 was a good chaperone policy. The intimate-examination checklist is thorough, and the requirement to record the chaperone's **name, role and the times they were present** is a detail many policies omit and which is exactly what protects a clinician if an allegation is made years later. All retained.

Four changes.

### 1. A single word reversed the meaning of the central rule

> *"The GMC advise against family members as chaperones as they may be **impartial**."*

The word required is **partial** — or *"may not be impartial"*. As written, the policy states that the GMC advises against family members **because they would be neutral**, which is the opposite of the reason and makes the rule read as arbitrary.

It is a one-letter error, and it sits on the sentence that explains why the most common shortcut — asking the husband or the daughter in the waiting room to step in — is not acceptable.

### 2. Its references were two other practices, and one of them is not a document

v01 cites:

- **"Bredon Hill Surgery"**
- **"Prof. Sparrow Surgery CQC no. 15"** — with **Appendix 2: "Prof. Sparrow Advice"**

These are other practices' materials, incorporated as appendices and cited as authority. Neither is a published source that can be checked, reviewed, or relied on. The same *"Prof. Sparrow"* reference appears in **C07 Medicines Management** — so it is a recurring source across the corpus, and an unverifiable one.

The GMC is listed only as *"General Medical Council"*, without naming the guidance that actually governs this subject: ***Intimate examinations and chaperones***. It is now cited properly.

### 3. The framing was gendered and dated

> *"Chaperones are most often required or requested where a **male examiner** is carrying out an intimate examination or procedure on a **female patient**."*

Current GMC guidance applies **regardless of the gender of either party**. Allegations are not confined to one configuration, and neither is patient discomfort. The observation may still be true of frequency, but writing it into the policy shapes practice — it implies a chaperone is less necessary in other combinations, which is not the position.

v02 also adds a definition of an intimate examination, and the point that **whether an examination feels intimate is determined by the patient**, not by the clinician's view of the anatomy involved.

### 4. There was no rule for when no chaperone is available

v01 requires that chaperones be trained members of staff, but says nothing about what to do when none is available — which, in a clinic with one or two trained people, will happen.

Without a rule, the default in practice is to proceed anyway. v02 states that the examination does not go ahead, with a `[CONFIRM]` for what happens instead, and an audit question that asks directly whether it has occurred.

### Additions

- **Remote consultations** — absent from v01, and relevant to any video service: confirming the patient is alone and in private, and recognising that remote assessment is usually inadequate for an intimate examination
- **Where the chaperone stands.** v01 says *"an appropriate place to observe"*; v02 states that a chaperone who cannot see the examination is not performing the role and protects no one
- **Chaperones are expected to raise concerns** about what they observe, and the training must tell them how (S03). A chaperone who witnesses something and says nothing is the failure mode this role exists to prevent
- **DBS `[CONFIRM]`** for anyone acting as a chaperone
- **A patient-facing chaperone notice**, written in plain language, including the right to decline a particular individual without giving a reason
