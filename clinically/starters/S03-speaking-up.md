## Scope

All individuals working at or for `[CLINIC NAME]` — employed, self-employed, bank, agency, volunteers, students and trainees, contractors, and those working under practising privileges or contract of service. It applies regardless of length of service and applies to former workers in respect of concerns about the period they worked here.

## AIM

Every serious failure in healthcare has, in retrospect, been visible to someone before it became serious. The question is never whether someone knew. It is whether they felt able to say so, and whether anything happened when they did.

`[CLINIC NAME]` treats speaking up as a normal part of working here, not an exceptional or hostile act. This policy sets out how to raise a concern, what will happen to it, what protection the person raising it has, and — the part most policies leave out — what feedback they will get.

**Speaking up is a professional duty, not a matter of personal courage.** GPhC Standard 8 requires registered pharmacy professionals to speak up when they have concerns; equivalent duties apply to doctors and nurses. A worker who stays silent about a risk to patients may themselves be in breach of their own professional standards.

---

## DEFINITIONS

**Speaking up / raising a concern.** Raising something that risks harm to patients, to staff, or to the organisation. The person raising it usually has no personal stake in the outcome.

**A grievance.** A complaint that *you personally* have been treated badly — a breach of your own employment rights, bullying, or a dispute about your terms. Grievances are handled under the Grievance Policy, which gives you rights of redress this policy does not.

**Overlap is common and does not need resolving first.** A concern about a colleague's conduct may be both. Raise it under this policy; `[CONFIRM: role]` will decide the right route and will tell you which route it has gone down. **It is not your job to categorise it correctly before you speak.**

**Protected disclosure.** A disclosure qualifying for legal protection under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998, which makes it unlawful to dismiss or subject a worker to detriment for making it.

## POLICY

1. **Anyone can raise a concern, about anything, at any time.** No threshold of certainty applies. **You do not need proof, and you do not need to be right.** A concern raised honestly and found to be unfounded is a good outcome, not a false alarm.

2. **Types of concern covered include, but are not limited to:**
   - Risk or actual harm to a patient
   - Unsafe clinical practice, or practice outside a person's competence
   - Medicines errors, and near misses
   - Poor or absent record keeping
   - Safeguarding concerns about a child or adult at risk (see also C08/C032 — these have their own mandatory referral route and must not be delayed by this process)
   - Bullying, harassment, or discrimination
   - A colleague working while unfit — through illness, substance use, or fatigue
   - Fraud, financial maladministration, or unauthorised use of funds
   - Breaches of confidentiality or information governance
   - Failure to comply with a legal or regulatory obligation
   - Risks to health, safety, or the premises
   - Concealment of any of the above
   - Anything a worker believes is putting people at risk

3. **No one is penalised for speaking up.** Victimisation, detriment, exclusion, or informal disadvantage as a result of raising a concern is a disciplinary matter and will be treated as gross misconduct. **This protection applies whether or not the concern turns out to be justified.** It is withdrawn only where a concern is raised maliciously, knowing it to be false — which is a separate disciplinary matter and, in practice, rare.

4. **You do not have to go to your line manager.** If the concern is about your manager, or you do not feel able to approach them, go to any of the named routes below. **No one may require you to raise a concern with them first before going elsewhere.**

5. **Concerns can be raised anonymously, but named concerns are handled better.** Anonymous concerns will be investigated as far as is possible. However, we cannot ask you clarifying questions, cannot give you feedback, and cannot protect you if we do not know who you are. Where you are willing to be named, **your identity will be kept confidential and shared only with those who need it to investigate** — you will be told before it is shared.

6. **Every concern gets an outcome, and the person who raised it is told what it was.** Silence following a concern is a failure of this policy. Feedback is given even where the answer is "we looked and found nothing."

7. **Speaking up is not a substitute for the routes that already exist.** Incidents go through incident reporting (C017); safeguarding goes through the safeguarding referral route (C08/C032) *immediately and in parallel*; things that have already harmed a patient trigger duty of candour (C021). This policy exists for the concern that has no other home, or that the normal route has failed to deal with.

8. **You may raise a concern externally, and we would rather you did than stay silent.** We would prefer you told us first so we can act. But you are entitled to go directly to your regulator or another prescribed person, and you retain full legal protection when you do. External routes are listed below. **Nothing in this policy, and no contract or settlement agreement issued by `[CLINIC NAME]`, restricts your right to make a protected disclosure.**

9. **Speaking up is tracked as a quality signal.** The number and nature of concerns raised, and their outcomes, are reported to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`. **A period with no concerns raised is investigated as a possible warning sign, not celebrated.**

### How to raise it

Verbally or in writing. There is no form to complete and no required wording. It helps if you can say what you saw or heard, when, who was involved, and whether anyone is at immediate risk — but **an incomplete concern is better than no concern.**

If anyone is at **immediate risk of harm, act first**: make the situation safe, escalate urgently to the most senior person available, and follow the safeguarding or emergency route. Then raise it under this policy.

### What happens next

| Stage | Timescale |
|---|---|
| Acknowledgement to the person who raised it | `[CONFIRM: default 2 working days]` |
| Initial assessment — is anyone at risk now? which route applies? | `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` |
| The person raising it is told the route, the named investigator, and the expected timescale | With the initial assessment |
| Investigation | `[CONFIRM: default 20 working days; where longer, the person is told why and given a revised date]` |
| Outcome and feedback to the person who raised it | Within `[CONFIRM: default 5 working days]` of conclusion |
| Actions, owners and completion dates recorded and tracked | At conclusion |
| Reported to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` | Next meeting |

Where the concern involves the person who would normally investigate, `[CONFIRM: role]` appoints someone else, and where necessary someone external. **No one investigates a concern about themselves.**

### Feedback

The person who raised the concern is told: that the investigation is complete; broadly what was found; and what has changed as a result. Where confidentiality or another person's employment rights limit what can be shared, they are told that this is the reason, rather than simply hearing nothing.

### Records

Concerns are recorded in a confidential Speaking Up log held by `[CONFIRM: role]`, separate from personnel files, containing: date received, route, nature (not the identity, in the summary view), assessment, investigator, outcome, actions, and date closed.

**A concern being raised is never recorded in the personnel file of the person who raised it**, and is never referred to in a reference, appraisal, or disciplinary process concerning them.

### Support

Anyone raising a concern may be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative at any meeting, may seek free confidential advice from Protect before or during the process, and has access to `[CONFIRM: occupational health / EAP arrangements, if any]`.

Support is also offered to anyone who is the *subject* of a concern. Being investigated is stressful, and being the subject of an unfounded concern is not a wrong done to you by the person who raised it.

## What changed from S03 v01 — and why

The 2022 policy was structurally sound and its list of disclosable malpractice has been kept. Six changes were made.

**1. Reframed from "whistle-blowing" to "speaking up."** The word *whistle-blowing* describes a last resort against the organisation — dramatic, adversarial, and rarely how a real concern begins. Every current framework (the National Guardian's Office, CQC's well-led assessment, GPhC Standard 8) uses *speaking up* to describe an everyday professional behaviour. The change is not cosmetic; it changes who feels the policy applies to them.

**2. Added the professional-duty framing.** v01 said concerns were *encouraged*. GPhC Standard 8 makes speaking up a **named professional standard** for every pharmacy professional. For a pharmacist-led service this is the single most relevant regulatory hook, and its absence from v01 was the reason theme 23 was split out during the regulator mapping.

**3. Added mandatory feedback with timescales.** v01 described how a concern would be investigated but never committed to telling the person what happened. This is the most common reason people who have spoken up once do not do so again, and it is the gap most visible at inspection: *"you raised it — what did they tell you?"*

**4. Added named alternative routes, including one outside the management line.** v01 relied on the general open-door culture. In a small clinic where the Registered Manager may be the subject of the concern, "raise it with management" is not a route. Item 4 and the `[CONFIRM]` in the routes table exist to force a real named person into that gap.

**5. Reference updated.** v01 cited *GMC: Good Medical Practice 2013*, superseded by the 2024 edition.

**6. Added the "no concerns raised is a warning sign" rule.** A period of silence in a small team is more likely to mean people do not feel able to speak than that nothing has gone wrong. Making that an audit question turns a comfortable statistic into a prompt.

The most consequential `[CONFIRM]` is the independent route in the routes table. For a clinic of five people it is genuinely hard to name someone outside the management line — but "we couldn't think of anyone" is not an answer that survives an inspection, and the point of the field is to make the difficulty visible now rather than at interview.
