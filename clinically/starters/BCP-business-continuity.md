## AIM

This plan sets out the responsibilities of management and staff in the event of sudden closure of `[CLINIC NAME]`, or an event causing reduced or limited services. It is a **reference tool for the actions required during or immediately after an emergency or incident** that threatens to disrupt normal activities.

- An **emergency** is an actual or impending situation that may cause injury, loss of life, destruction of property, or disruption to normal business operations to such an extent that it poses a threat.
- An **incident** is any event that may be, or may lead to, an interruption, disruption, loss or crisis.

The plan ensures continuation of care by minimising the impact of damage to staff, patients, premises, equipment or records, and helps identify actions that can be taken **in advance** to reduce the risk of an incident happening.

## BUSINESS CRITICAL PROCESSES

Where an incident occurs, priority is given to restoring the processes or services critical to the safety and wellbeing **first of patients, and second of staff.**

**Business critical processes** are those where loss of delivery would endanger finances, damage the reputation of the business with patients, or seriously affect the ability to comply with legislation such as the CQC fundamental standards.

These processes are given preferential access to premises, staff, equipment and records where an emergency restricts availability.

> `[CONFIRM: this section in full. It was absent from v01, and it is now the most likely business continuity event for a small clinic.]`

**Where a cyber incident is suspected — ransomware, malware, unauthorised access, or suspected data theft:**

1. **Disconnect affected devices from the network. Do not power them off** — volatile evidence is lost. `[CONFIRM: who decides]`
2. **Do not pay a ransom, and do not communicate with the attacker**, without taking advice. `[CONFIRM: who authorises any decision]`
3. Notify `[CONFIRM: IT support provider, and their out-of-hours contact]`
4. **Assess whether personal data has been affected.** If so, this is a **personal data breach** — the **72-hour ICO notification clock starts from awareness.** See C03
5. Notify `[CONFIRM: cyber insurer, and the policy number]` — most policies require early notification
6. Report to **Action Fraud**, and consider reporting to the **NCSC**
7. Revert to the paper contingency below
8. Report as an incident under C017, and consider duty of candour under C021 where patients are affected

## What changed from the 2022 plan — and why

The 2022 plan was well-structured — a clear aim, a defensible definition of business critical processes, a realistic set of disruption scenarios, two planning windows, and a sensible set of forms. All retained.

Four changes.

### 1. Four of its planning assumptions are the literal character "X"

> *"In the event of a partial failure of a server the network could be unavailable for up to **X hours**"*
> *"it could take up to **X days** to restore a limited desktop service"*
> *"Availability of the internal telephone network historically runs at **X %**"*
> *"there could be loss of service for up to **X hours**"*

These are the assumptions the plan's IT and telephony response is built on, and they were never filled in. This is the same defect class as the `Xxxx` placeholders in C08 and the `XXXXX` on the C05 consent form — a template adopted and not completed.

An assumption of *"up to X hours"* cannot be planned against, and it cannot be tested.

### 2. There is no cyber incident scenario

v01 lists *"loss of IT/data"* as a scenario, which covers hardware failure. It contains **nothing on a cyber attack** — no ransomware, no unauthorised access, no data theft.

For a small clinic in 2026 this is the **most likely business continuity event there is**, and it behaves differently from every other scenario in the plan:

- The immediate action is **disconnect but do not power off** — the opposite of the instinct
- It triggers a **72-hour ICO notification** clock (C03) that no other continuity scenario does
- **Backups may be encrypted too** if they were connected to the network — so "we have backups" is not automatically an answer
- There are decisions — ransom, insurer, law enforcement — that need to be pre-assigned, not made under pressure

Added as its own section, with a `[CONFIRM]` for the paper contingency, because that is what the clinic actually falls back on.

### 3. The plan requires review but not testing

v01 states it *"will be reviewed regularly, with a full update on an annual basis."* Reviewing a document is not the same as finding out whether it works.

Added: an annual desktop exercise, quarterly verification that the contact numbers still work, annual backup restoration testing, and annual confirmation that the patient transfer arrangement still exists — each recorded, with actions tracked.

### 4. The patient transfer arrangement may not exist

v01 states that where care is jeopardised, *"patients must be transferred to other independent doctors that have previously agreed to consult patients on a temporary basis."*

**No provider is named.** For a clinic with patients on titration or monitored medicines, this is the most consequential sentence in the plan, and as written it describes an arrangement that may never have been made. `[CONFIRM]` added asking directly whether a written agreement exists with a named provider.

### Additions

- **Where the plan is kept.** A continuity plan held only on the clinic's server is unavailable during an IT failure or cyber incident — the two scenarios most likely to invoke it. Now requires a printed copy on site, one off-site, and an electronic copy reachable without the clinic network
- **Loss of a key supplier**, with a named alternative for each critical one. v01 lists it as a scenario but plans nothing for it
- **Cancel or redirect today's appointments** in Form A, including who holds the appointment list when the system is down
- **Patients mid-treatment take priority**, and how they are identified quickly
- **Who has authority to invoke the plan**, and who does so in their absence
- Where a **cloud-hosted** clinical system is used, the supplier's published availability and recovery commitments replace the server assumptions — obtained in writing rather than assumed
