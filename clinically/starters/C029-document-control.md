### The policy register

`[CONFIRM: who maintains the policy register and where it is held.]` It lists every policy with its number, title, current version, responsible person, authorisation date, review date, and status.

**The register is the single source of truth for which version is current.** Where the register and a document on a shelf disagree, the register wins and the document is investigated.

### Review

- **Every policy is reviewed at least every two years**, or sooner where triggered
- **Review triggers:** a change in law, regulation or professional guidance · an incident, complaint or audit finding that touches the policy · a change in the service · a near miss revealing the policy is wrong or unusable
- `[CONFIRM: who runs the review schedule and how overdue reviews are escalated. Default: reviews due in the next quarter are a standing item at the governance meeting]`
- **A policy past its review date is flagged, not silently left in force.** An out-of-date review date is one of the first things an inspector checks, because it is visible on the front page

> **When a policy is replaced, the old version is removed from every location it exists in** — the policy folder, shared drives, printed copies, staff handbooks, and any copy shared with another organisation.

## PART TWO — RECORDS MANAGEMENT

`[CLINIC NAME]` holds records relating to patients and to its commercial and business activity. **Both are treated in the same confidential manner**, with access and use only by relevant persons.

This policy establishes the governance arrangements for all documents and records produced and received, in accordance with best practice and the principles of **ISO 15489**.

### The information lifecycle

| Stage | |
|---|---|
| **1. Creation and receipt** | Putting pen to paper, making a database entry, or starting an electronic document — created here or received from an external source. It must be **complete and accurate** |
| **2. Distribution** | Managing information internally or externally — email, photocopy, print, hand delivery. See Appendix 2 |
| **3. Use** | The active phase — records used day to day to inform decisions, document action or support operations. Whether patient or business records, they are handled confidentially, on a need-to-know basis, and **their whereabouts must be known at all times** |
| **4. Maintenance** | Records no longer in daily use, held in the records management system for legal or financial reasons until their retention period is met. Includes filing, transfer and retrieval. **Records are not removed from the system — information is copied and tracked so no amendments are made** |
| **5. Disposal** | Reviewed at the end of the retention period and, where appropriate, destroyed under confidential destruction conditions. Records with genuine historical value are archived rather than destroyed |

### Altering records

> **Staff must not alter, deface, block, erase, destroy or conceal a record with the intention of preventing disclosure** in response to a request under data protection law.
>
> **This is a criminal offence under section 173 of the Data Protection Act 2018.** It applies to the individual who does it, not only to the organisation.

Legitimate correction of a record is a different thing entirely, and is governed by **C09** — struck through, attributed, dated, with the original still visible.

### Retention

Keeping unnecessary records wastes time and space, incurs cost, and **creates liability when responding to information requests**. **UK GDPR Article 5(1)(e)** requires that personal data is kept no longer than necessary for the purpose for which it was collected.

- **Patient record retention:** see **C09** and Appendix 1
- **Business and financial records:** `[CONFIRM: retention periods — take advice from the clinic's accountant and solicitor. Company and tax records have their own statutory periods]`
- **Employment records:** `[CONFIRM]`
- `[CONFIRM: who reviews records reaching the end of their retention period, and how often]`

### Legal holds

**Where records are, or may become, relevant to an investigation, complaint, claim, inquest or inquiry, they are placed on hold and are not destroyed** — regardless of the retention schedule.

`[CONFIRM: who places and lifts a legal hold, and how it is recorded. A hold that nobody records is a hold that expires when the person who knew about it goes on leave.]`

### Requests from external bodies

Patient information requested by an external body is handled under **C03**, applying the **Caldicott Principles**. Requests for a patient's own record are subject access requests under UK GDPR and are handled under **C028**.

### Equality

Records about individuals are managed in accordance with this policy **irrespective of race, disability, sex, gender reassignment, age, sexual orientation, religion or belief, marriage or civil partnership status, pregnancy or maternity, or socio-economic status.**

## What changed from C029 v01 — and why

v01's records management content is good. The five-stage information lifecycle, the requirement that the whereabouts of a record must always be known, the rule that records are copied and tracked rather than removed from the system, and the distinction between destruction and archiving are all sound and are retained.

Four changes.

### 1. The document control policy has the wrong document control

v01's own header block reads:

> **Policy No and Version: C027 Version 01**

The policy is **C029**. Its filename, its footer and every cross-reference to it in the corpus say C029. Its front page says C027.

In any other policy this would be a minor typographical error. In the **document control policy** it is the specific failure the document exists to prevent, and it is the first thing anyone checking version control would look at.

### 2. It did not control documents

This is the substantive gap. v01 is a **records management** policy — it governs patient records and business records through their lifecycle. It contains nothing on controlling **the clinic's own policy documents**:

- No version numbering convention
- No approval or authorisation workflow
- **No policy register**
- No review schedule, or escalation when a review is overdue
- No distribution and acknowledgement process
- **No procedure for withdrawing a superseded version from circulation**

That last omission is directly connected to what this review has found. Several defects identified across the corpus — unreplaced `Xxxx` placeholders, references a decade out of date, two recruitment policies both carrying the same unlawful clause — persisted because **nothing in the system was responsible for noticing.** A policy register with review dates and an escalation route is the control that would have surfaced them.

Part One is new, and includes an explicit route for the situation this review has created: **withdrawing a policy because it was wrong**, which is treated as urgent, recorded, and accompanied by an assessment of whether the incorrect version was relied on while it was in force.

### 3. It cited a repealed Act — including for a criminal offence

v01 refers to the **Data Protection Act 1998** twice, including in its most important prohibition:

> *"Staff members must not alter, deface, block, erase, destroy or conceal records with the intention of preventing disclosure under a request relating to the Data Protection Act 1998."*

The prohibition is correct, but the statute was repealed in 2018. The offence now sits at **section 173 of the Data Protection Act 2018**, and it is a **criminal offence committed by the individual**, not only a breach by the organisation — which v01 does not say, and which is the part that makes staff take it seriously.

This is one of **eight documents** in the corpus still anchored to DPA 1998.

### 4. Smaller corrections

- **"ISO 15489 (the International British Standard for Records Management)"** — ISO 15489 is an **International Standard**. "International British Standard" is a contradiction; a document is one or the other
- **"Caldicott Principals"** — *Principles*. Twice
- The Caldicott Principles are referenced without a date; they were revised in **2020** and there are now **eight**
- References include *"BMA"* and *"NHS England: Document & Records Management Policy"* — one an organisation with no document title, the other a policy belonging to a different organisation
- *GMC Good Medical Practice 2013* → 2024
- The equality list omitted **sex, marriage and civil partnership, and pregnancy and maternity** — the same incomplete set found in S01
