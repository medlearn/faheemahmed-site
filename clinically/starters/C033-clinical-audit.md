## Scope

Anyone engaged in the clinical audit process for or on behalf of `[CLINIC NAME]`.

---

## AIM

To develop and sustain a culture of best practice in clinical audit, as a **quality improvement process** rather than data collection for its own sake.

---

## POLICY

1. **Clinical audit measures practice against defined standards, identifies the gap, changes something, and then measures again.** An audit that stops after the first measurement is not an audit — it is a snapshot.

2. **All clinicians take part in clinical audit** as part of their work here.

3. **Audit provides assurance about the quality of services** and is expected under CQC Regulation 17 and across the five key questions.

4. **Audit data is anonymised** unless there is a compelling reason otherwise. Where identifiable data is necessary, advice is sought from `[CONFIRM: the Caldicott Guardian or information governance lead — this must be a real named person]`.

5. **Audit findings that reveal a risk go on the risk register** (C017); findings that reveal a training need go into the training plan (C010).

## PROCEDURE

### 1. Agreeing topics and planning

`[CONFIRM: role]` presents an **annual Clinical Audit Programme** to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` for agreement.

The programme is driven by:

- **Incidents and near misses** from the preceding twelve months
- **Complaints** themes
- Areas of known clinical risk
- Changes in guidance, or new services
- Required assurance for CQC and `[CONFIRM: other regulators]`
- Previous audits showing incomplete improvement

**A timetable states when each audit takes place and when it reports back.**

`[CONFIRM: the minimum annual audit set. A starting position for most clinics: record keeping · consent documentation · prescribing against formulary or guideline · infection control and hand hygiene · safeguarding documentation · medicines storage and expiry · complaints handling timescales.]`

### 2. Undertaking the audit

Clinical audit measures practice against **predetermined standards of best practice**. Standards are presented with:

- **Measurable criteria**
- **A target percentage compliance**
- The evidence base for the standard — based on robust research evidence wherever possible
- The sample size and how the sample was selected

Audits are referenced to the CQC Fundamental Standards `[CONFIRM: and to GPhC standards where the clinic is dual-regulated — the same audit can evidence both]`.

All proposals are signed off by `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` using the audit proposal form (Appendix 2).

### 3. Presenting the outcome

Outcomes are reviewed by `[CONFIRM: role]`. Results and recommendations, with an action plan, go to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`.

**Every action has a named owner and a completion date.** An audit outcome form (Appendix 3) is attached to the audit evidence.

### 4. Closing the loop — re-audit

> **This is the step most often missed, and it is the step that makes it an audit.**

- **Every audit that identifies a shortfall is re-audited** after the change has been implemented
- `[CONFIRM: default re-audit interval — 6 months, or 3 months where the finding was significant]`
- The re-audit uses **the same standards and the same method**, so the results are comparable
- **Where the re-audit shows no improvement, that is itself a finding** requiring escalation to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` — not a quiet third audit
- The audit programme explicitly schedules re-audits alongside new topics

### 5. Information governance

All information collected must comply with **UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018** and the **Caldicott Principles**. Data must be:

- Adequate, relevant and **limited to what is necessary**
- Accurate
- Processed for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes
- Held securely
- **Kept no longer than necessary** — audit datasets are deleted once the audit is complete and reported `[CONFIRM: retention period for audit data]`

Patient information is anonymised — reference numbers rather than initials where possible, since **initials plus a date can identify a patient in a small clinic.**

### 6. Involving patients and clinicians

Patients and carers assess quality differently from professionals, and each perspective is valid. Where a patient survey is used for audit, it is designed to determine whether **defined clinical standards** are being achieved, not simply to collect satisfaction ratings.

`[CONFIRM: how patient feedback is gathered and how it feeds the audit programme.]`

## What changed from C033 v01 — and why

v01 was **810 words** and its methodology was correct — measuring against predetermined standards, requiring measurable criteria and target compliance, and framing audit as quality improvement rather than data collection. All retained.

Four changes.

### 1. There was no re-audit requirement

v01's step 4 is *"Measuring improvement, follow up and statistic gathering"*, and says:

> *"The number of incidents in twelve months following the audit can be measured (with the aim of incidents lowering once an audit action plan has been implemented)."*

That is not closing the audit cycle. **Re-audit means measuring the same standards by the same method after the change**, so the results are comparable. Counting incidents afterwards is a proxy, and a weak one — incident numbers move for many reasons, including changes in reporting.

Without re-audit an audit programme cannot demonstrate improvement, which is precisely what Regulation 17 asks it to demonstrate. Added, with intervals, the requirement to use the same method, and the rule that **no improvement on re-audit is itself a finding requiring escalation** rather than a quiet third attempt.

### 2. Its only reference was an NHS trust that no longer exists

v01's reference list, in full, is: **"University Hospitals Bristol NHS Foundation Trust"**. No document title, so nothing can be checked against it. **That trust was dissolved in 2020** when it merged to form University Hospitals Bristol and Weston.

Neither Regulation 17, the NICE audit methodology, nor any data protection source was cited.

### 3. Its information governance section was built on repealed law

> *"All information collected to carry out an audit must take into account the **Data Protection Act 1998** and the **Caldicott Principles (1997)**."*

DPA 1998 was replaced by the **Data Protection Act 2018** and UK GDPR. The Caldicott Principles were revised in 2013 and again in **2020**, and there are now **eight**, not six.

This is the same pre-GDPR anchoring found across the corpus — 8 documents still citing DPA 1998, only 12 of 119 mentioning GDPR at all.

### 4. The audit programme was three rows

Appendix 1's audit tool lists exactly three areas: *Consent · Infection Incidents · SUIs*. That is a start, not a programme, and it contains no standards, criteria or targets.

v02 adds a starting set of seven audit topics covering the highest-risk areas, and — more importantly — makes the programme **driven by the clinic's own incidents, complaints and risks** rather than by a fixed list.

### Additions

- **Audit findings feed the risk register and the training plan** — v01 left audit as a closed loop reporting only to the governance committee
- **Sample size and selection method** stated in the audit design. An audit of five records chosen by the auditor is not evidence
- **Initials plus a date can identify a patient in a small clinic** — v01 permitted anonymisation *"with patient initials or reference numbers"*. Reference numbers are preferred
- **A real named Caldicott Guardian or IG lead.** v01 refers to *"Ahmeys Clinic's Caldicott Guardian"* without naming one, and nothing elsewhere in the corpus appoints one
