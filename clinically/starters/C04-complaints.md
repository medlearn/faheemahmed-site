## Scope

All individuals in the employ of `[CLINIC NAME]` — *employ* meaning any person employed, self-employed, a volunteer, working under practising privileges, or under contract of service.

---

## AIM

To ensure that concerns and complaints are dealt with promptly, fairly and openly, that people who complain are treated well, and that what we learn changes what we do.

---

## POLICY

1. **Patients, and their relatives or representatives, have a right to complain, and it should be easy to do so.** Complaints are opportunities to learn and improve.

2. **Complaining never affects the care a patient receives.** No patient is treated differently, deprioritised, or refused an appointment because they have complained. This is stated in the patient leaflet, because people worry about it.

3. **This procedure is not about apportioning blame**, establishing negligence, or awarding compensation. It is not part of the disciplinary policy — though a complaint may separately reveal a matter requiring action under it.

4. **Most complaints, dealt with early, openly and honestly, are resolved locally.** Failure to listen aggravates the problem, and is the commonest reason a complaint escalates.

5. **Duty of candour applies alongside this policy, not instead of it.** Where a notifiable safety incident has occurred, the duty under C021 is triggered **regardless of whether anyone has complained** — and it is not discharged by responding to a complaint.

6. **An apology is not an admission of liability.** Saying sorry that something happened, and meaning it, is expected. `[CONFIRM: check the clinic's indemnity terms — no reputable indemnifier prohibits an apology, but confirm rather than assume.]`

7. **A complaint about a fee is handled here**, on the same timescales and with the same escalation rights as a clinical complaint. It is not dealt with informally because it concerns money (see S12).

8. **Every complaint is recorded** — verbal and written alike — in the complaint register, and the outcome is recorded in the patient's file.

## PROCEDURE

### Verbal complaints and concerns

**All verbal complaints are taken seriously, however minor they seem.**

1. Front-line staff seek to resolve the problem immediately where they can.
2. Where they cannot, they offer to involve their line manager **that day**.
3. Staff remain calm, polite and professional. **Nothing is gained by being defensive.** Staff do not accept blame, make excuses, or blame colleagues — and equally do not argue with the person.
4. Where the concern is resolved on the spot, it is still **recorded** — resolved-at-source concerns are the richest source of improvement data and the easiest to lose.
5. Where the proposed resolution is not acceptable, the complainant is invited to put it in writing to `[CONFIRM: role]`, and is given the complaints leaflet.

**Complaints made on someone's behalf.** Where a relative or representative complains for a patient, verify they have the patient's authority before discussing anything confidential. **It is easy to assume an advocate has authority when they do not.** Where in doubt, the patient's explicit permission is required. Where the patient lacks capacity, proceed under the MCA. Where the patient has died, see below.

### Written and serious complaints

1. Passed to `[CONFIRM: named complaints manager]`, recorded in the complaint register, and acknowledged within **2 working days** by the same medium it arrived in.
2. The complaints leaflet is sent with the acknowledgement.
3. Where the complaint is made on the patient's behalf, consent is obtained from the patient — preferably in writing.
4. **Contact the complainant early, by telephone where they are willing.** Ask what happened from their point of view and **what outcome they are looking for.** A complaint answered without knowing what the person actually wanted is usually answered wrongly.
5. Investigate: gather records, statements from those involved, and the relevant policies. The investigation is proportionate to the seriousness.
6. **The investigation is carried out by someone not personally involved in the matter complained about.** `[CONFIRM: who investigates where the complaint concerns the Registered Manager — this must be a named alternative.]`
7. Consider in parallel, and record the decision on each:
   - Does this meet the **duty of candour** threshold? (C021)
   - Is this also an **incident** requiring reporting? (C017)
   - Is there a **safeguarding** concern? (C08/C032 — refer immediately, do not wait for the complaint process)
   - Does it require a **statutory notification** to CQC?
   - Does it need to go to the clinic's **indemnity provider**?

### Complaints about clinical care

Advice is sought from `[CONFIRM: the clinical lead]`, who takes advice from the clinic's **indemnity provider** — `[CONFIRM: MDU / MPS / MDDUS / other]`.

Where the complaint concerns the clinical lead, it is shared with `[CONFIRM: named alternative clinician]`.

> **Professional regulators do not advise providers on handling complaints.** The GPhC, GMC and NMC receive fitness-to-practise concerns; they are not a source of advice on a live complaint. That is what the indemnity provider is for. A complainant retains a separate right to raise a concern with the relevant regulator at any time, and this is stated in the leaflet.

### Complaints involving children

Treated seriously and investigated with care. **Where the complaint is of a safeguarding nature, the safeguarding lead is contacted immediately and leads.** The complaints process does not delay a safeguarding referral.

### Complaints where the patient has died

Handled under this policy. Authority to receive information is limited to a personal representative or someone with a claim arising from the death — see C03 and the Access to Health Records Act 1990. **Duty of candour and the offer of a meeting apply with particular care.**

### Meetings

Where a meeting is offered:

- The complainant may bring a friend, relative or advocate
- A detailed explanation of the findings is given
- **An apology is given where appropriate** — for what happened, and for any failing found
- A written record of the meeting is sent afterwards

### The written response

Every response contains:

- What was complained about, in the complainant's terms
- What was investigated and how
- **What was found — including where we got it wrong**
- An apology where appropriate
- **What we are changing as a result, who owns it, and by when**
- What to do if they remain dissatisfied — the escalation route below
- A named contact for further questions

> **A response that finds no fault and changes nothing should be rare.** Where it happens, it must still explain the reasoning clearly enough that the complainant can see how the conclusion was reached.

### If the complainant is not satisfied

> ⚠️ **The CQC does not investigate or arbitrate individual complaints.** CQC contact details must be provided (Reg 16 requires it, and CQC uses the information as intelligence about the service) — but CQC must never be presented as the appeal route, because it is not one.

### Where legal action is started

Where a complainant begins legal proceedings, `[CONFIRM: role]` notifies the indemnity provider immediately and takes their advice on how to proceed.

> **A complaint is not automatically abandoned because a claim has been made.** Where the clinic can still answer the complainant's questions and put things right, it should — subject to the indemnity provider's advice. Duty of candour under C021 is a **legal duty that continues regardless of litigation**, and is not suspended by it.

### Unreasonably persistent contact

Very rarely, contact becomes so frequent, or so repetitive of matters already answered, that it prevents the clinic from working. In that case:

1. **The complaint itself is first properly answered.** Persistence is never a reason not to investigate.
2. `[CONFIRM: named decision-maker — must be senior and must not be the person receiving the contact]` decides, and records the reasons.
3. The person is written to, told what has been decided, why, what contact remains available, and for how long.
4. **A right of review is offered**, to someone not involved in the decision.
5. Reviewed at `[CONFIRM: default 6 months]`. Restrictions are not permanent.
6. **Urgent clinical need and safeguarding are never restricted.** Care continues.
7. Recorded and reported to `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`.

> This is applied because of **behaviour**, never because a complaint is unwelcome, difficult, or because the clinic has been unable to give the person the outcome they want. Where someone keeps coming back, the first question is whether we actually answered them.

## LEARNING AND IMPROVEMENT

- **All complaints are reviewed at least quarterly** at `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]`, for themes as well as individually.
- Every upheld complaint generates at least one action, with a **named owner and a date**, tracked to completion.
- Actions are checked at the following meeting. **A complaint is not closed when the letter is sent; it is closed when the change is done.**
- Lessons are shared with staff at team meetings and supervision — anonymised where necessary.
- Complaint themes feed the clinical audit programme (C033) and the risk register (C017).

### Training

`[CONFIRM: role]` organises training. All staff are trained in handling verbal and written complaints, at induction and `[CONFIRM: default annually]`. Complaint issues are discussed at staff meetings and supervision so everyone can learn from them.

### Making it easy to complain

- The complaints leaflet is available in the waiting area, on the website, and given on request
- `[CONFIRM: accessible formats and languages available]`
- The process is explained to anyone who expresses dissatisfaction, whether or not they use the word "complaint"

## What changed from C04 v01 — and why

v01 was a decent complaints policy with good instincts — welcoming complaints, resolving early, apologising without admitting liability, involving the governance committee. All retained. Six corrections.

### 1. It contradicted itself on the escalation route — and the route it gave does not exist

v01 tells complainants that the written response *"includes details of how to approach the Care Quality Commission if the complainant is not satisfied with the outcome"* — and then states, correctly, near the end:

> *"the CQC will not arbitrate in a complaint but require the provider to make their contact details available to the service user"*

Both cannot be true. The second is correct: **CQC does not investigate individual complaints.** Directing a dissatisfied complainant to CQC as their appeal route sends them somewhere that will not help them, and it will produce a second, angrier complaint about the first.

Underneath this is a bigger gap: **a privately funded patient has no Ombudsman.** The PHSO covers NHS care. The real independent route for private healthcare is a scheme such as **ISCAS**, and it is only available to subscribing members. v02 makes membership an explicit `[CONFIRM]`, and requires that where the clinic subscribes to nothing, it says so honestly and offers a genuine internal review instead. **Promising independent adjudication the clinic cannot deliver is worse than offering an honest internal review.**

### 2. Three different deadlines, in one policy

v01 states, within a few lines of each other:

- *"All complaints are investigated and responded to in writing within **21 days**"*
- *"Patients will be updated on the progress of the investigation if the **28 working days** cannot be met"*
- *"within **21 working days** should be in a position to provide a full explanation"*

21 days, 28 working days, and 21 working days are three different periods. A complainant reading the leaflet, a member of staff following the policy, and an inspector checking compliance would each get a different answer. v02 uses one set of figures, marked `[CONFIRM]`, and requires the patient leaflet to match exactly.

### 3. It instructed that investigation stop the moment legal action began

> *"If legal action is taken at this stage, any investigation by the organisation under the complaints procedure immediately ceases."*

This is unsafe advice. **Duty of candour under Regulation 20 is a legal obligation that does not pause because a claim has been issued**, and stopping all contact the moment a solicitor appears is exactly the behaviour that hardens a complaint into litigation. The correct step is to notify the indemnity provider immediately and take their advice — which is usually to keep answering what can properly be answered.

### 4. "Vexatious complainers" was the weakest section in the document

v01 reads:

> *"there are service users who can be treated as vexatious complainers **due to the inability of the company to meet the outcomes of the complaints**, which are never resolved"*

As written, that says people become vexatious **because we could not resolve their complaint** — which describes a failure of the service, not a fault in the complainant. The paragraph then ends with two sentences about sharing information with CQC that do not connect to the subject and use *"they"* without an antecedent.

v02 reframes it as **unreasonably persistent contact**: applied to behaviour and never to unwelcome complaints, requiring the complaint to be answered first, with a named senior decision-maker, written notice, a right of review, a review date, and an absolute carve-out for urgent clinical need and safeguarding.

### 5. It named the wrong source of advice, and assumed a doctor

v01 has the manager seek advice from the *"medical director who will take advice from the **GMC** and/or the MDU"*.

**Professional regulators do not advise providers on live complaints.** The GMC and GPhC receive fitness-to-practise concerns — going to them for advice on handling a complaint misunderstands what they are for. Advice comes from the **indemnity provider**. And in a pharmacist-led clinic there may be no medical director at all; the role is now `[CONFIRM: clinical lead]`, with the GPhC added.

### 6. References

*GMC Good Medical Practice 2013* → 2024. *DoH: Listening, Responding, Improving* (2009) is archived. Neither **Regulation 16** — the regulation this policy exists to satisfy — nor **Regulation 20** was cited at all.

### Additions

- **Complaining never affects care** — stated in the policy and in the leaflet, because patients genuinely fear it
- **Ask what outcome the person wants**, early. A complaint answered without knowing what the person was looking for is usually answered wrongly
- **The investigator must not be involved in the matter complained about**, with a named alternative where the complaint concerns the Registered Manager
- **A parallel-duties checklist** — candour, incident, safeguarding, CQC notification, indemnity — recorded for every complaint
- **Fee complaints** routed here rather than handled informally (S12)
- **Complaints where the patient has died**
- **Actions tracked to completion** — a complaint closes when the change is made, not when the letter is sent
- **"Is the number of complaints plausible?"** as an audit question. Very few complaints in a busy service means they are not reaching you
