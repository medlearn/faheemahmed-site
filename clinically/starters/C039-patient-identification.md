## AIM

Failure to identify a patient correctly is one of the most serious risks to patient safety. Correct identification, using core identifiers, reduces and where possible eliminates the risk of mis-identification.

**For a service that prescribes, identification carries a second purpose:** confirming that the person requesting treatment is who they say they are, is the age they say they are, and is the person the treatment is for.

---

## POLICY

1. **The identity of every patient is verified at every stage of their pathway.**

2. **Core patient identifiers** used at this clinic:

   | Identifier | Use |
   |---|---|
   | **Full name** | Always |
   | **Date of birth** | Always |
   | **Address** | Always |
   | **NHS number** | Where known — the single most reliable identifier, and it should be captured wherever available |
   | Telephone number | Supporting |
   | **Photographic ID** — current passport or driving licence | At registration |
   | Utility bill or similar showing name and address | Supporting, for address |

3. **Identity is always confirmed using open questions.**

   > **Ask:** *"Can I have your full name and date of birth?"*
   > **Never ask:** *"Can you confirm your name is …?"*
   >
   > A closed question invites agreement, and a patient who is anxious, hard of hearing, or expecting to be recognised will say yes. This is the single most effective control in this policy.

4. **Lack of ID is not a barrier to urgent care.** Every effort is made to obtain identification, but it must never prevent a patient receiving urgent medical care.

5. **Lack of ID is a barrier to some non-urgent treatment.** `[CONFIRM: which treatments require verified photographic ID before they may be provided. Default: any controlled drug, any long-term or high-risk medicine, and any treatment requiring monitoring. Where identity cannot be verified, those are not provided.]`

6. **Every mis-identification, including near misses, is reported** under C017.

## PROCEDURE

### Registration — adults

1. The patient completes the registration form (Appendix 1).
2. They present **photographic ID** to a member of staff.
3. The staff member **records on the form that they have seen it**, what it was, and signs.
4. Where the patient has no ID — for example a visitor to the country — staff assist them to complete as much of the form as possible, and **record the reason ID was not verified** in the office-use section.

### Registration — children

1. The parent or guardian completes the child registration form (Appendix 2).
2. They present the child's **birth certificate**, and **evidence of their own identity**.
3. The staff member records on the form that both have been seen, and signs.
4. `[CONFIRM: how parental responsibility is established — see C032. Bringing a child to an appointment is not evidence of PR.]`

### Attending a consultation

On arrival, staff carry out an identity check using open questions:

- **Adult:** *"Please can I have your full name and date of birth?"*
- **Child:** *"Please can I have the child's full name and date of birth, and the mother's or father's full name?"* — then cross-check against the record.

### A child attending with someone other than a parent or guardian

1. Where a child attends with someone **not named by the parent or guardian as authorised**, staff **contact the parent or guardian** to verify who is accompanying the child and why.
2. **The person accompanying the child is recorded in the clinical record**, by name and relationship.
3. The clinician **contacts the parent to discuss any prescribing or other outcome** of the consultation.
4. It is useful to record in the child's notes the names and relationships of adults the parents permit to bring the child — for example a grandparent, childminder or nanny — so authority can be confirmed quickly.

> **Where the accompanying adult cannot be verified and the parent cannot be reached, this is a safeguarding consideration, not only an administrative one.** See C032.

### When identity cannot be verified

- Every effort is made to obtain identification. **This must not prevent urgent medical care.**
- The clinician uses their clinical judgement, the information provided at registration and during the consultation, in deciding what treatment — **including any prescribing** — is appropriate.
- **The fact that identity was not verified, and the reasoning for proceeding, is recorded in the clinical record.**

## What changed from C039 v01 — and why

v01 was practically strong, and two parts of it are better than most equivalent policies:

- **The open-question rule** — *"Do not ask: 'Please can you confirm your name is …'"* — is the single most effective identification control there is, and stating the wrong version explicitly is what makes it stick
- **The protocol for a child attending with someone other than a parent** — contact the parent, record who accompanied the child, contact the parent about any prescribing — is thorough and correct

Both retained in full. Three changes.

### 1. Its stated authority was an organisation abolished in 2012

The AIM of v01 opens:

> *"The **National Patient Safety Agency (NPSA)** has recognised that failure to correctly identify patients constitutes one of the most serious risks to patient safety."*

The NPSA was abolished in **2012**. The observation is correct and remains correct — but the policy's opening sentence attributes it to a body that has not existed for over a decade, and there is no current source cited in its place.

This is one of **nine documents** in the corpus referencing the NPSA.

### 2. There was nothing on verifying identity remotely

v01's entire procedure assumes the patient is standing at reception: showing ID to *"an appropriate member of staff"*, being asked their name on arrival, presenting a birth certificate.

For a service that consults and prescribes **at a distance**, this is the largest gap in the document. Remote identity verification is the primary control against:

- Someone obtaining a prescription in another person's name
- **A young person obtaining an age-restricted treatment by self-declaring an age**
- One person obtaining supplies from several services simultaneously
- A patient registering more than once under different details

**GPhC guidance on providing pharmacy services at a distance** is directly on point and was not cited. Neither was GMC guidance on remote prescribing, nor CQC's guidance for online providers.

v02 adds a remote verification section, an explicit rule that **treatment is not provided remotely where identity cannot be verified to the required standard**, and a `[CONFIRM]` on indicators of misuse.

### 3. ID was described as never being a barrier — without the other half

v01 states, correctly, that lack of ID *"will not be a barrier to treating them"* and *"must not prohibit a patient from receiving urgent medical care."* That principle is right and is retained.

But it is stated without limit, and the only guidance that follows is that *"the consulting doctor will use their clinical experience … to determine any prescribing that may be required."*

There is a difference between **urgent care**, which is never withheld for want of ID, and **elective supply of a controlled or high-risk medicine to an unverified person**, which should be. v02 keeps the first principle intact and adds a `[CONFIRM]` for the second, so the clinic decides deliberately which treatments require verified identity rather than leaving it to case-by-case judgement at the desk.

### References and other corrections

- v01's references were **"British Medical Association"** and **"NHS England"** — organisation names with no document titles, so nothing can be checked against them
- The patient registration form states information is *"protected under the **Data Protection Act**"* with no year — on a **patient-facing** form. Now the Data Protection Act 2018
- *"The consulting **doctor**"* appears twice in a pharmacist-led service — the fourth instance of this pattern in the corpus
- **NHS number** added as a core identifier. It is the most reliable single identifier available and did not appear in v01's list at all
