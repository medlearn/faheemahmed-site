## AIM

To ensure that information about patients — their identity, attendance, diagnosis, treatment and records — remains confidential, and that it is disclosed only where the patient has agreed, or where the law requires or permits it.

---

## POLICY

1. **Patient information is confidential.** It is discussed only with those who need it to provide or support that patient's care, and only to the extent they need it.

2. **The fact that someone is a patient here is itself confidential.** Confirming or denying attendance is a disclosure. This matters particularly in a private clinic, where the service attended may itself disclose something sensitive.

3. **Disclosure requires a lawful basis.** In practice, one of:
   - The patient's **consent**
   - A **legal obligation** — a court order, a statutory duty, or the FGM mandatory report
   - A **safeguarding** concern (C08, C032) — consent is not required
   - An **overriding public interest**, where the benefit outweighs the duty of confidence
   - **Vital interests** — where disclosure is necessary to prevent serious harm and the patient cannot consent

   In every case, disclose **only what is necessary, only to those who need it**, and **record what was disclosed, to whom, on what basis, and why**.

4. **"Next of kin" has no legal standing.** A relative — including a spouse, parent of an adult, or someone recorded as next of kin — has **no automatic right to information about a patient with capacity**. Information is shared with a relative only where the patient has agreed, or under the Mental Capacity Act where the patient lacks capacity. `[CONFIRM: how patient preferences about who may be told what are recorded at registration.]`

5. **The Caldicott Principles apply to every use of patient information:**

   | | |
   |---|---|
   | 1 | Justify the purpose for using confidential information |
   | 2 | Use confidential information only when it is necessary |
   | 3 | Use the minimum necessary |
   | 4 | Access should be on a strict need-to-know basis |
   | 5 | Everyone with access should be aware of their responsibilities |
   | 6 | Comply with the law |
   | 7 | **The duty to share information for individual care is as important as the duty to protect confidentiality** |
   | 8 | Inform patients about how their information is used |

   **Principle 7 is not optional.** Withholding information that a patient's GP or another clinician needs is a failure of confidentiality practice, not an example of it.

6. **Data protection law is not a barrier to sharing for safeguarding or for direct care.** It is a framework for sharing appropriately. Where a decision is taken not to share, that decision is recorded with its reasons — in the same way a decision to share would be.

7. **Every worker signs a confidentiality agreement** on commencing work (Appendix 1). The obligation continues after they leave.

8. **All staff undertake confidentiality and information governance training** at induction and `[CONFIRM: default annually]`.

## PROCEDURE

### Talking about patients

- Patient information is not discussed outside the clinic, and is not discussed inside it where it may be overheard — reception areas, corridors, waiting rooms, lifts
- Patients are not discussed with colleagues who are not involved in their care
- **Nothing about a patient is posted on social media**, including anything anonymised that could still identify them — a rare condition, a distinctive circumstance, or a timing that makes them identifiable to those who know them
- Staff do not look up records of patients they are not caring for. **This includes their own record, and those of family, friends, colleagues and people known to them.** `[CONFIRM: whether the clinical system logs access and who reviews the log.]`

### Telephone enquiries about a patient

1. **Do not confirm that the person is a patient.**
2. Decline to answer questions. Take the caller's name, relationship and telephone number, and say someone will call back.
3. Check the patient's recorded wishes about who may be told what.
4. Where nothing is recorded, **ask the patient before disclosing anything**.
5. Call back on a number verified from the record, not one given by the caller.
6. Record the enquiry, the decision and the reasoning in the patient's record.

### Calling, texting and emailing patients

| | |
|---|---|
| **Calling** | Only agreed numbers, at agreed times. Leave a voicemail only where the patient has agreed. Leave a message with another person only where the patient has specifically agreed to that person |
| **Texting** | Only to an agreed number. **The message must not reveal the clinical reason for contact or the nature of the service** — this matters most where attendance itself is sensitive. `[CONFIRM: approved text templates]` |
| **Emailing** | Only the agreed address, by a secure route. `[CONFIRM: the route. Default: NHSmail to NHSmail, or password-protected attachment. Standard unencrypted email is not acceptable for identifiable clinical information]` |

Contact preferences are recorded in the patient's record and **checked before use**, not assumed from what was done last time.

### Requests from third parties

| Requester | Route |
|---|---|
| **The patient, about their own record** | This is a **subject access request** under UK GDPR. Handle under C028 — free of charge, and within **one month**. It is not refused, and it does not require a reason |
| **Solicitor acting for the patient** | Requires the patient's written authority. Verify it |
| **Insurer** | Requires the patient's specific, informed consent to the disclosure of that information for that purpose |
| **Police** | Not automatically entitled. Disclose where there is a court order, a statutory duty, or a justified public interest. `[CONFIRM: who decides — default: the Registered Manager or clinical lead, never the person who took the call]`. Record the request, the decision and the basis |
| **Court order or statutory notice** | Comply, disclosing only what the order requires. Verify it is genuine |
| **Another clinician providing care to the patient** | Share what is needed for that care — Caldicott principle 7 |
| **Relative or friend** | Only with the patient's agreement, or under the MCA where the patient lacks capacity |
| **Media** | Never. Referred to `[CONFIRM: role]`. No member of staff confirms or denies anything to a journalist |
| **Family of a deceased patient** | Access is under the **Access to Health Records Act 1990**, and is limited to a personal representative or someone with a claim arising from the death. `[CONFIRM: who handles these]` |

**Where there is any doubt, do not disclose — escalate.** Information not disclosed today can be disclosed tomorrow. Information disclosed wrongly cannot be recalled.

### Records and screens

- Records — paper and electronic — are stored securely and are not left where they can be seen
- **Screens are locked when unattended.** Passwords are individual, confidential, and never shared. `[CONFIRM: automatic screen lock timeout. Default: 5 minutes]`
- Patient details are not displayed where visible to others — no whiteboards, lists or diaries in public view
- Documents carrying patient identifiers are covered or enclosed when moved
- Confidential waste is disposed of by professional confidential-waste collection or **cross-cut shredding**. Never in general waste

### Personal data breaches

> **This is a legal duty with a statutory deadline. It was absent from v01.**

A **personal data breach** is any breach of security leading to accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to personal data. It includes an email sent to the wrong person, a lost paper record, a lost or stolen device, a mis-filed letter, and a conversation overheard.

**If you think a breach may have occurred, report it the same day. You are never in trouble for reporting one promptly.**

| Stage | By when | Who |
|---|---|---|
| Report internally | **Immediately on discovery** | The person who discovers it |
| Contain — recall the email, secure the device, retrieve the record | Immediately | `[CONFIRM: role]` |
| Assess risk to the individuals affected | Within 24 hours | `[CONFIRM: role]` |
| **Report to the ICO where there is a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms** | **Within 72 hours of becoming aware** | `[CONFIRM: role — usually the Registered Manager]` |
| **Tell the affected individuals where there is a high risk to them** | Without undue delay | `[CONFIRM: role]` |
| Record in the breach log — **including breaches not reported to the ICO, and why not** | Same week | `[CONFIRM: role]` |
| Review, lessons learned, actions | Next governance meeting | `[CONFIRM: the governance meeting]` |

**ICO breach reporting line:** 0303 123 1113 · **72 hours runs from awareness, not from the end of the investigation.** A partial report on time is better than a complete one late.

Duty of candour obligations under C021 are considered separately — a data breach affecting a patient may require candour as well as ICO notification.

## What changed from C03 v01 — and why

v01 was **1,211 words** — one of the shortest documents in the corpus, for one of the most inspected subjects in healthcare. Its practical content on telephone, text and email contact was genuinely good and has been kept almost intact. Five changes.

### 1. There was no data breach procedure at all

v01 contained nothing on what to do when confidentiality fails. **Reporting a personal data breach to the ICO within 72 hours is a legal duty**, and the clinic had no procedure, no named person, no log and no timescale for it.

This is the most serious gap. Confidentiality policies are not primarily tested by whether staff know to keep quiet — they are tested by what happens in the hour after an email goes to the wrong patient.

### 2. Staff were signing an agreement citing a repealed Act — with a typo

Appendix 1 of v01 states:

> *"…can result in prosecution for an offence under the **Date** Protection Act **1998**…"*

Two errors in one line, on the document every worker signs: a typo (*Date* for *Data*), and a statute repealed in 2018. It is now the Data Protection Act 2018.

### 3. "Next of kin" was treated as conferring a right

The AIM of v01 permitted disclosure with written permission *"from the patient / next of kin / guardian / court-appointed persons"*, and elsewhere allowed information to go to *"solicitors and/or relatives"*.

**Next of kin has no legal standing in England and Wales.** A relative — including a spouse — has no right to information about an adult patient with capacity. This is now stated explicitly, because it is the confidentiality error most likely to happen in practice, and most likely to happen with entirely good intentions.

### 4. It had no data protection in it

For a confidentiality policy written in 2022, v01 contained no reference to UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018, the Caldicott Principles, the ICO, lawful bases for disclosure, or subject access rights. Its references were *GMC Good Medical Practice 2013* and *CQC Fundamental Standards*.

The eight Caldicott Principles are now set out in full, including **principle 7** — that the duty to share for individual care is as important as the duty to protect. That principle exists precisely because over-caution harms patients, and it connects this policy to C41.

The GMC's actual confidentiality guidance — *Confidentiality: good practice in handling patient information* (2017) — was not cited. It now is, alongside the GPhC standards, which appeared nowhere.

### 5. It was written for an inpatient unit

v01 refers to *"their stay in the clinic"*, *"at the time of admission"*, *"press inquiries and anonymity"*, *"discharged patient"*, *"x-rays or pathology specimens"* leaving the ward, *"nursing carers"* and *"consultant/s"*.

The discharged-patient procedure was also unworkable and, in one respect, wrong: it required a third party to write to the patient, who would then write to the clinic. That is not how a **subject access request** works — a patient asking for their own record is entitled to it within one month, free, without giving a reason. Routed to C028.

### Additions

- **No looking up your own record, or those of family, friends and colleagues** — the commonest disciplinary confidentiality breach in healthcare, and absent from v01
- **Social media**, including the trap of "anonymised" posts that remain identifiable to those who know the patient
- **A third-party request table** with a defined route for police, insurers, courts, media and the families of deceased patients — v01 covered only general telephone enquiries
- **The fact of attendance is itself confidential** — which matters more in a private clinic than in a general practice, because the service attended may disclose the condition
