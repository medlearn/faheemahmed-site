## Scope

All individuals in the employ of `[CLINIC NAME]` — *employ* meaning any person employed, self-employed, a volunteer, working under practising privileges, or under contract of service. It covers incidents affecting patients, staff, visitors and contractors.

---

## AIM

To ensure that incidents, near misses and hazards are reported, graded, investigated proportionately and learned from; that risks are identified and managed before they cause harm; and that staff involved are supported.

---

## POLICY

1. **Reporting is expected, and never punished.** Incident reporting exists to improve systems, not to attribute blame. **No member of staff is disciplined for reporting an incident promptly and honestly.**

2. **Near misses are reported.** An incident that caused no harm this time is free information about the harm it will cause next time. A service reporting only incidents that caused harm is not reporting.

3. **The response is proportionate to the severity**, but every incident is recorded.

4. **Safety is never traded against another objective.** It is understood throughout `[CLINIC NAME]` that it is unacceptable to meet other objectives at the expense of safety.

5. **Every incident is assessed for parallel duties** — duty of candour, safeguarding, statutory notification, external reporting — and the assessment is recorded, including where the answer is no.

6. **Incidents are not closed when investigated. They are closed when the action is done.**

## PROCEDURE

### Reporting an incident

| Step | Timescale |
|---|---|
| Make the situation safe and attend to anyone injured | Immediately |
| **Verbal report to the line manager or `[CONFIRM: role]` where the incident is severe** | Immediately |
| Report to line manager | As soon as possible |
| Complete the incident report form | **Within one working day**, maximum |
| Where there is an injury: entry in the accident book | Same day |
| Needle stick injury — follow C02 in addition | Immediately, PEP is time-critical |

**IF IN DOUBT, REPORT IT.** Grading is not the reporter's job.

### The line manager ensures

- Immediate actions have been completed and noted on the form
- Any further documentation is complete
- **The grading is appropriate** — checked, not assumed
- Everyone in the organisation who needs to know, knows
- Communication with the patient, relatives, visitors or contractors has happened — see C021
- The investigation includes an action plan and lessons shared, proportionate to severity
- **External reports are made on time** — RIDDOR, MHRA, CQC

### Deciding what else this triggers

Recorded for every incident, including where the answer is no:

| Question | Where |
|---|---|
| Does this meet the **duty of candour** threshold? | C021 |
| Is there a **safeguarding** concern? | C08 / C032 — refer immediately, do not wait for the investigation |
| Is a **CQC statutory notification** required? | Below |
| Is this **RIDDOR** reportable? | Below |
| Does this involve a **medicine or a medical device** — Yellow Card? | Below |
| Is this a **Never Event**? | C036 |
| Does the **indemnity provider** need to be told? | `[CONFIRM]` |
| Does this need to go on the **risk register**? | Below |

### External reporting

**RIDDOR — to the HSE**

Under the **Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013**. Reportable events include death, specified injuries, injuries causing more than seven days' incapacity, certain occupational diseases, and **dangerous occurrences — which include injury from a sharp contaminated with a blood-borne virus**.

`[CONFIRM: named person]` reports, and a copy is retained. `[CONFIRM: default timescales — specified injuries and dangerous occurrences within 10 days; over-seven-day injuries within 15 days.]`

**MHRA — the Yellow Card scheme**

> **Two distinct duties, and v01 covered only one of them.**

| Report | When |
|---|---|
| **Medical device adverse incidents** | Where the incident led to, or could lead to: death or life-threatening illness or injury; deterioration in health; the necessity for medical or surgical intervention; or unreliable test results leading to inappropriate diagnosis or therapy. **Also report minor faults and discrepancies** — they gain significance in aggregate and may indicate a quality problem at the manufacturer. **Report even where the cause was human error** |
| **Adverse drug reactions** | **All suspected ADRs for medicines under additional monitoring (black triangle ▼), and all serious suspected ADRs for any medicine** — even where the reaction is well recognised, and even where causality is uncertain. Suspicion is enough |

**For a prescribing service, ADR reporting is the more frequently relevant of the two.** `[CONFIRM: who is responsible for Yellow Card reporting, and how the clinic satisfies itself it is happening.]`

### Action plans and learning

- Proportionate to severity, with **the person responsible and an expected completion date** for every action
- A section for reflection, capturing what was learned
- **Discussed at the next governance meeting and monitored through it until complete**
- Lessons shared — as a minimum with the team where the incident occurred, and more widely where relevant

### The risk register

`[CONFIRM: this section — v01 referred to how risks are managed but described no register process.]`

- Risks identified from incidents, audits, complaints, safeguarding, and risk assessments are entered on a **risk register**
- Each entry has: description, likelihood and impact, current controls, residual score, **a named owner**, planned mitigation, and a review date
- `[CONFIRM: who holds the register and how often it is reviewed. Default: Risk Management Lead, reviewed at every governance meeting]`
- **The register is a live document**, not an annual exercise. A risk that has not been reviewed in a year is not being managed

### Communicating with patients, relatives, visitors and contractors

Communication may be needed both before and after investigation, and should happen **as close to the incident as possible**, by the most appropriate person. It follows the principles of being open — see **C021**, and note that where the duty of candour is triggered, specific statutory steps apply.

**The communication is recorded.** Where someone is raising a concern, they are told how to complain (C04).

### Where media interest is likely

**Patients, staff, relatives and affected contractors are informed before the media.** `[CONFIRM: named individual]` co-ordinates all media communication. No other member of staff speaks to a journalist. See S04.

### Supporting staff

Involvement in an incident is distressing, and being investigated is more so. Staff are supported under **S08**. `[CONFIRM: what support is available — occupational health, EAP, peer support.]`

Where there is reason to believe a member of staff has committed a criminal act, the clinic takes steps to preserve its position and advises them early to obtain separate legal advice or representation.

### Raising concerns

Where a member of staff feels unable to raise a safety concern through the normal route, they may use the **Speaking Up Policy (S03)**, which provides a safe framework free from detriment.

## DEFINITIONS

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Incident** | Any untoward or unusual event — a deviation from the normal pattern of activity, therapeutic wellbeing or smooth running of the workplace — involving patients, staff or visitors, which may adversely affect health, safety or welfare, then or later |
| **Serious incident** | Any accident or incident where a patient, member of staff or member of the public suffers serious injury, major permanent harm or unexpected death (or the risk of death or injury) on clinic premises, or where the actions of staff are likely to cause significant concern |
| **Near miss** | Any incident that did not lead to harm, but could have under different circumstances |
| **Harm** | Any injury (physical or psychological), disease, suffering, disability or death. Harm is unexpected where it is not related to the natural course of the patient's illness or underlying condition |
| **Hazard** | Something with the potential to cause harm — including hazards that are not obvious, such as noise, hazardous chemicals or blood-borne viruses |
| **Risk** | The likelihood of harm occurring, combined with its severity |
| **Adverse event** | Any incident that did lead to harm |
| **Never Event** | A serious, largely preventable patient safety incident that should not occur if available preventative measures are implemented. See C036 |
| **Physical assault** | The intentional application of force to another, without lawful justification, resulting in physical injury or personal discomfort |
| **Non-physical assault** | The use of inappropriate words or behaviour causing distress or constituting harassment |

## TYPES OF INCIDENT TO REPORT

- **Personal accident** — any accident, however small, that did or could have adversely affected any person
- **Clinical incident** — an event or omission during clinical care causing physical or psychological injury (treatment error, **prescribing or medicines error**, equipment failure); unexpected deaths
- **Medicines incidents** — wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong patient, omitted dose, dispensing error, prescribing error, **and near misses of any of these**
- **Medical device failure**
- **Near miss, clinical** — an act or omission during care that could have injured a patient but where the outcome was prevented
- **Near miss, non-clinical**
- **Physical and non-physical assault** — including whether police were involved, with any crime reference number
- **Ill health** — work-related, known or suspected (blood-borne infection, dermatitis, latex allergy)
- **Fire incident** — however small, including false alarms; note whether the building was evacuated and whether the fire brigade attended
- **Security incident** — theft, loss or damage to property, intrusions, false alarms (other than fire)
- **Vehicle incident** during work-related activity
- **Information governance incident / data breach** — see C03, and note the **72-hour ICO duty**

`[CONFIRM: audit owner and frequency. Default: Risk Management Lead, annually.]`

- Were incidents reported within one working day?
- Was the grading appropriate and checked?
- **Was the parallel-duties assessment recorded for every incident, including where the answer was no?**

- **Have any suspected adverse drug reactions been Yellow Carded?** A prescribing service reporting none over a year is very unlikely to be reporting

- **Are near misses being reported at all?** A service reporting only harm-causing incidents is under-reporting
- Is the total number of incidents plausible for the activity level?

## What changed from C017 v01 — and why

v01 was a well-structured incident policy with a good definitions set, a sensible proportionality principle and a clear statement that reporting supports safety rather than blame. Most of it is retained, including the definitions and the incident-type list. Five changes.

### 1. It cited RIDDOR in a repealed edition

> *"A RIDDOR incident relates to the items reportable under the Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations **1995**"*

RIDDOR 1995 was replaced by **RIDDOR 2013**, which changed the reportable categories, the over-seven-day injury threshold, and the reporting timescales. A policy pointing staff at the 1995 regime is pointing them at the wrong list.

### 2. Staff were consenting to data sharing with an abolished body

> *"By completing the incident reporting form, staff agree to this information being shared with the **National Patient Safety Agency (NPSA)**."*

The **NPSA was abolished in 2012**. Its functions moved to NHS England, and the National Reporting and Learning System has since been replaced by the **Learn from Patient Safety Events** service.

This is the second appearance of the NPSA in the corpus — C07f cites *National Patient Safety Agency alerts* as a current source. The consent statement here is worse, because staff are being asked to agree to a disclosure to an organisation that does not exist.

There is a further point for a private provider: the national patient safety system is designed around NHS organisations, and independent providers participate voluntarily. v02 makes this an explicit `[CONFIRM]` so the clinic decides and records what it actually does, rather than describing participation it does not have.

### 3. Adverse drug reactions were missing entirely

v01 covers MHRA reporting thoroughly — **for medical devices only.** The thresholds, the instruction to report minor faults for aggregate significance, and the point that human error is still reportable are all correct and retained.

But there is nothing on **Yellow Card reporting of suspected adverse drug reactions.** For a **prescribing service**, that is the more frequently relevant of the two duties by a wide margin: all suspected ADRs for black triangle (▼) medicines, and all serious suspected ADRs for any medicine, with suspicion sufficient and causality not required.

Given the clinic's activity — ADHD titration, weight management, hormone treatment — this is not a theoretical gap. Added, with an audit question: **a prescribing service that has Yellow Carded nothing all year is almost certainly not reporting.**

### 4. Named roles were partly hard-coded and partly blank

v01 names an individual directly in the procedure — *"give to Faheem Ahmed"*, twice — while leaving the media co-ordinator as **`<INSERT NAME>`**, unreplaced.

Both are now `[CONFIRM]` fields, along with a named deputy for each role and an explicit alternative route where the incident concerns the Risk Management Lead or Registered Manager.

The policy also refers to a *"Risk Manager (Health & Safety)"*, a *"Health and Safety Manager"*, *"senior management"* and *"the directorate"* — an NHS trust structure that does not exist here.

### 5. Its references were two NHS trusts, one of which no longer exists

v01 lists **"Heart of England NHS Foundation"** and **"Southern Health NHS Foundation"** as its references — organisation names with no document titles, so nothing can be checked against them. **Heart of England NHS Foundation Trust was dissolved in 2018.** Neither RIDDOR, the CQC Regulations, nor MHRA guidance was cited.

### Additions

- **A risk register process** — v01 pointed to a template but described no register: no owner, no scoring, no review cadence. A risk that has not been reviewed in a year is not being managed
- **Medicines incidents named explicitly** as a reportable type, including prescribing and dispensing near misses. v01's clinical incident category mentioned "drug error" once in passing, which is thin for a prescribing service
- **Information governance incidents** as a reportable type, linked to the **72-hour ICO duty** in C03
- **A parallel-duties checklist** recorded for every incident — candour, safeguarding, notification, RIDDOR, MHRA, Never Event, indemnity — including where the answer is no
- **Investigation timescales**, and **verification that actions were effective** rather than merely marked complete
- **Aggregate review of low-grade incidents.** v01 said themes might prompt investigation but named nobody to look for them and set no frequency
- **"Are near misses being reported at all?"** as an audit question. Under-reporting of near misses is the commonest failure of an incident system, and it looks identical to safety on a chart
