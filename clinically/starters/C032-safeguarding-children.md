## Scope

All individuals in the employ of `[CLINIC NAME]` — *employ* meaning any person employed, self-employed, a volunteer, a student or trainee, working under practising privileges, or under contract of service. This includes agency staff and contractors.

**Throughout this policy, "child" and "young person" mean anyone who has not yet reached their 18th birthday.**

This policy applies to any child who is:

- Cared for by, or has contact with, a patient of `[CLINIC NAME]`
- A patient of `[CLINIC NAME]`, where the service sees under-18s
- Visiting `[CLINIC NAME]`
- Employed by or volunteering for `[CLINIC NAME]`
- Felt to be at risk from any adult who is a patient, visitor, staff member or volunteer

> **Staff who work only with adults are fully within scope.** The commonest way a clinic encounters a child safeguarding concern is not by treating a child — it is by treating an adult who has children at home. Every clinician seeing an adult must think about who else is in that household.

## AIM

Protecting children from harm and promoting their welfare is everyone's responsibility. This policy gives all `[CLINIC NAME]` staff the guidance and procedures to fulfil their role in safeguarding children, whether or not the clinic treats children directly.

It ensures that:

- There is an appropriate safeguarding framework and a clear referral procedure
- All staff are trained to the level required for their role
- **The welfare of the child is paramount.** Where there is a conflict of interest between a child and their parent or carer, the child's welfare comes first
- Services respect dignity, privacy and belief, and do not discriminate on grounds of race, gender, language, religion, sexual orientation or disability
- Where English is not a first language, **a professional interpreter is used — never a family member, and never the child**
- All information relating to child protection is treated as confidential

## DEFINITIONS

### Who is a child?

Anyone who has not yet reached their 18th birthday (**Children Act 1989**). The fact that a young person has reached 16, is living independently, is in further education, is a member of the Armed Forces, is in hospital, in prison or in a Young Offender Institution **does not change their status or entitlement to protection.**

### A child in need

**Section 17(10), Children Act 1989** — a child who, without the provision of local authority services:

- Is unlikely to achieve or maintain a reasonable standard of health or development
- Whose health or development is likely to be significantly impaired without those services
- Is disabled

### Harm and significant harm

**Section 31(9), Children Act 1989**, as amended by the Adoption and Children Act 2002:

- **Harm** — ill treatment, or the impairment of health or development, including impairment suffered from seeing or hearing the ill treatment of another
- **Development** — physical, intellectual, emotional, social or behavioural development
- **Health** — physical and mental health
- **Ill treatment** — includes sexual abuse and forms of ill treatment which are not physical

**Significant harm** is the threshold justifying compulsory intervention in family life. There are no absolute criteria. Consideration of severity may include the degree and extent of physical harm, the duration and frequency of abuse or neglect, the extent of premeditation, and the presence or degree of threat, coercion, sadism, or bizarre or unusual elements.

More often, significant harm is a **compilation of events**, both acute and longstanding, which interrupt, change or damage the child's physical and psychological development. This is why a single apparently minor concern is still shared — it may be one piece of a picture held elsewhere.

### Types of abuse

| Type | Includes |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Hitting, shaking, throwing, poisoning, burning or scalding, drowning, suffocating. Also where a parent or carer fabricates the symptoms of, or deliberately induces, illness |
| **Emotional** | Persistent emotional maltreatment causing severe and adverse effects on emotional development. Conveying that a child is worthless, unloved or inadequate; developmentally inappropriate expectations; overprotection and limitation of exploration; preventing normal social interaction; seeing or hearing the ill treatment of another; serious bullying; exploitation or corruption. Some level of emotional abuse is present in **all** types of maltreatment |
| **Sexual** | Forcing or enticing a child to take part in sexual activities, whether or not the child is aware of what is happening. Physical contact, penetrative or non-penetrative; and non-contact activities including involving children in looking at or producing sexual images, watching sexual activity, or grooming in preparation for abuse, including online |
| **Neglect** | Persistent failure to meet basic physical and/or psychological needs. May occur during pregnancy through maternal substance misuse. Failure to provide adequate food, clothing or shelter; to protect from physical and emotional harm; to ensure adequate supervision; to ensure access to appropriate medical care; or unresponsiveness to basic emotional needs |
| **Child sexual exploitation (CSE)** | A form of sexual abuse where a child is coerced, manipulated or deceived into sexual activity in exchange for something they need or want, or for the financial advantage or increased status of the perpetrator. The child may believe they are in a loving relationship |
| **Child criminal exploitation (CCE), including county lines** | Where a child is coerced or manipulated into criminal activity, frequently drug transportation and supply. Indicators include unexplained money or possessions, going missing, and relationships with controlling older individuals |
| **Online abuse** | Grooming, sexual exploitation, bullying and exposure to harmful content facilitated through the internet, gaming platforms and social media |
| **Domestic abuse** | Under the **Domestic Abuse Act 2021**, a child who sees, hears or experiences the effects of domestic abuse is a **victim in their own right**, not a witness |
| **Female genital mutilation (FGM)** | See the mandatory reporting duty below |
| **Forced marriage** | A marriage in which one or both parties is married without their consent or against their will. Distinct from an arranged marriage. **The police must always be contacted** — urgent action may be needed |
| **Radicalisation** | See the Prevent duty below |

### Parental responsibility

- If the parents are married to each other at the time of the birth, or have jointly adopted, **both** have parental responsibility
- If not married, the father has PR if his name is on the birth certificate at registration. A father may acquire PR by re-registering the birth where his name was not on the original certificate
- PR may also be acquired by agreement registered with the **Principal Registry**, or by court order
- **Parents do not lose parental responsibility on divorce** — this applies to both the resident and the non-resident parent

## PROCEDURE

### Responding to a concern

**If a child is in immediate danger, call 999.** Do not wait for advice.

Otherwise:

1. **Share the concern with the Safeguarding Lead the same day.** Non-clinical staff report to their manager or directly to the lead
2. Where a receptionist or administrator has a concern about a patient about to be seen, **they must inform the clinician first**, so the clinician can assess in light of that information
3. Record what was seen or said, in the person's own words where possible, the same day
4. The Safeguarding Lead decides whether a referral to Children's Social Care is required
5. **If seeking advice from the lead would cause delay that leaves a child at risk, refer directly.** The route exists to help, not to gate

**Staff should:** listen, stay calm, take it seriously, explain that they have a duty to pass the information on.

**Staff should not:** investigate, ask detailed or leading questions, confront a parent or carer, or promise to keep it secret.

**Doing nothing is not an option.** It must never be assumed that someone else will pass the information on.

### FGM — the mandatory reporting duty

**This is a personal legal duty on the individual professional, not on the organisation.**

Under the Female Genital Mutilation Act 2003 as amended, a regulated health or social care professional or teacher who, in the course of their work, **discovers that an act of FGM appears to have been carried out on a girl under 18** must report it to the police.

- The duty applies where the professional is **informed by the girl** that FGM has been carried out, or **observes physical signs** of it
- The report is made to the **police, by calling 101**, by the close of the next working day
- **The duty is personal.** It is not discharged by telling the safeguarding lead, and it cannot be delegated
- The duty does **not** apply to a suspicion of *risk* of FGM, or to women over 18 — but those still require a safeguarding referral through the normal route

### Prevent duty

Where there is concern a child is vulnerable to being drawn into terrorism, the concern goes to the Safeguarding Lead, who considers a **Channel** referral. The threshold is vulnerability, not criminality.

### Where the clinic treats under-18s

`[CONFIRM: complete this section only where the service sees under-18s. Where it does not, state that explicitly and say what happens if one presents anyway — because one will.]`

- Competence to consent is assessed using **Gillick competence**, and contraception and sexual health advice against the **Fraser guidelines**. See C05
- Where a competent young person refuses to involve a parent, that is recorded with the reasoning, as is the clinical decision on whether treatment can safely proceed
- **Sexual activity in under-13s is always a child protection referral** — a child under 13 cannot consent in law (Sexual Offences Act 2003)
- Sexual activity in 13–15 year olds is presumed to require a referral unless a documented assessment concludes otherwise; the assessment considers age gap, power imbalance, coercion, and the child's understanding
- The GP is informed in line with C41 unless doing so would place the child at risk
- Chaperone arrangements apply — see C01

### Confidentiality and information sharing

**The Data Protection Act 2018 and UK GDPR are not a barrier to sharing information for safeguarding purposes.** They provide a framework for sharing it appropriately.

Be open and honest with the child and/or their parents or carers from the outset about why, what, how and with whom information will or could be shared, and seek agreement — **unless doing so would place the child at risk.**

Information shared should be **necessary, proportionate, relevant, accurate, timely and secure.**

All staff should:

- Seek advice where there is doubt, without disclosing identity where possible
- Share with consent where appropriate, and where possible respect the wishes of those who do not consent
- **Share without consent where, in their judgement, lack of consent can be overridden in the public interest** — based on the facts of the case
- Treat the safety and wellbeing of the child as paramount
- **Record the decision and the reasons for it — whether the decision was to share or not to share**
- Where information was shared, record what, with whom, and for what purpose

> **Consent is never required to make a child safeguarding referral.** Where a parent refuses, the referral is still made. Where telling the parent would place the child at greater risk, they are not told beforehand, and the reasoning is recorded.

### Recognition — when to ask further questions

Concerns should be raised where:

- A child is **not brought** to appointments without good reason, and no effort is made to reschedule *(the phrase is "was not brought", not "did not attend" — a child has no control over attendance)*
- A child is presented late after an injury, or the explanation does not fit the injury
- An injury is in a child who is not independently mobile
- A parent's mental ill health is affecting their capacity to care, or medication is being taken chaotically or not at all
- A parent is not giving a child prescribed medicine, resulting in repeated admissions
- A child is not receiving appropriate education
- There is substance misuse by a carer
- There is domestic abuse in the household
- There are indicators of exploitation — unexplained money or possessions, going missing, older controlling associates

**IF IN DOUBT, SHARE YOUR CONCERN WITH THE LOCAL SAFEGUARDING TEAM AT THE EARLIEST OPPORTUNITY.**

### Children with disabilities

Disabled children are *children in need* under section 17 of the Children Act 1989 and are at heightened risk because of:

- Dependence on others, and reduced ability to resist or report abuse
- Carers' inadequate understanding of their needs
- Reduced access to the 'keep safe' strategies available to other children

**A child's parent should never be placed in the position of interpreting for that child** where a safeguarding concern exists. See Appendix 1.

## What changed from C032 v01 — and why

**This document needed more correction than any other in the set so far.** The underlying structure was sound, and the definitions of abuse, significant harm and parental responsibility have been kept. But v01 carried a number of factual errors, and it is worth being clear about where they came from.

### 1. The statute was cited under the wrong name, throughout

v01 refers repeatedly to the **"Children and Young Persons Act 1989"**. There is no such Act. The legislation is the **Children Act 1989**. The Children and Young Persons Acts are 1933, 1963 and 1969, and are different statutes.

The same error appears in **"Adoption and Children and Young Persons Act 2002"** — the correct title is the **Adoption and Children Act 2002**.

This affects every substantive legal citation in the policy: sections 17, 31(9), 46 and 47.

### 2. The cause was a global find-and-replace

The errors are not random. Someone appears to have run a document-wide replacement of *child* → *young person* and *children* → *children and young persons*. That transformation is what turned the **Children Act** into the *"Children and Young Persons Act"*, and it left visible damage elsewhere:

- *"'Children and children and young persons' therefore means 'children and young persons and young people'"* — a sentence that no longer means anything
- *"Working Together to Safeguard Children and Children and Young persons, DCSF, 2010"*
- *"children and children and young persons"* — repeated throughout

v02 uses **"child"** and **"young person"** as ordinary words and cites statute by its correct name.

### 3. The policy contradicts itself on the question that determines all training levels

Section 5.0 of v01 states:

> *"In the case of Ahmeys Clinic, staff do not generally meet the children as **under 18s are not seen at the clinic**."*

Four lines later, in the same section:

> *"NOTE: At Ahmeys Clinic **under 18s are seen** therefore the Safeguarding Lead will have Level 3."*

Both statements cannot be true, and the answer determines the required training level for every member of clinical staff. v02 makes this the **first question in the document**, with a different training table for each answer.

### 4. It was adapted from an acute NHS trust policy and not fully localised

v01 records that it is based on *Barking, Havering and Redbridge University Hospitals NHS Trust Safeguarding Children Policy*. That origin shows: the Intercollegiate level definitions list catering staff, anaesthetists, paediatric surgeons and looked-after-children nurses; Appendix 1 covers callipers, sleep boards and residential-care regimes. None of it describes a small private clinic. Retained where relevant, replaced where not.

`[INSERT Social Services Safeguarding Protocols for Children and Young People]` was also left unreplaced — the referral framework itself was blank.

### 5. References were between 8 and 12 years out of date

| Cited in v01 | Position |
|---|---|
| Working Together to Safeguard Children, **DCSF 2010** | Superseded several times since. DCSF itself was abolished in 2010 and became the DfE |
| Intercollegiate Document **2014** | Superseded |
| **"Data Protection Act 2004"** | No such Act. DPA 1998 was superseded by **DPA 2018** and UK GDPR |
| "CQC Fundamental standard 05" | Safeguarding is **Regulation 13** |

### 6. The FGM mandatory reporting duty was stated but not operationalised

v01 correctly listed the duty among the changes made by the Serious Crime Act 2015 — but only as a line in a list of statutory amendments. It did not say who must report, to whom, by when, or that **the duty is personal to the individual professional and cannot be discharged by telling the safeguarding lead.** That distinction is the whole point of the duty. It now has its own section.

### 7. Additions

- **CQC statutory notification** under Reg 18 — absent from v01, the same gap found in C08
- **Timescales** — including the 48-hour written referral, which v01 mentioned once in passing
- **CSE, child criminal exploitation and county lines, and online abuse** — the 2010 framing predates all three
- **Domestic Abuse Act 2021** — a child who sees or hears domestic abuse is now a victim in their own right, not a witness
- **Gillick and Fraser, and the under-13 rule** — necessary if the clinic sees under-18s, and absent from v01
- **Named deputy** and an external route where the lead and Registered Manager are the same person
- *"Was not brought"* rather than *"did not attend"* — a child has no control over whether they are brought to an appointment, and the language shift is deliberate safeguarding practice

None of this reflects badly on the clinical judgement in the original. It reflects a document that was adapted from a large NHS trust, put through a bulk find-and-replace, and then never read end-to-end again. **That is exactly the failure mode Clinickly's completeness check is designed to catch.**
