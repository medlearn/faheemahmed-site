# What a CQC interview actually asks — evidence base

Source: real **CQC registration interview** (provider + registered manager applications), 2h53m / 32,498 words, transcript owned by Faheem. This is the evidence base for Clinickly's **inspection-readiness** features. Companion: [POLICY-CORPUS.md](POLICY-CORPUS.md).

## Structure of the session
1. **Provider part** — how the company will work; policies and procedures staff follow
2. **Registered manager part** — *your* understanding of HSCA responsibilities and how you'll ensure the provider's policies are worked within
3. **Location walkthrough** — is the premises appropriate
4. Inspector then writes a report recommending **registration** or a **notice of proposal to refuse** (28 days to respond with further evidence)

Questions run through the CQC domains, in this order: **well-led → safe → effective → caring → responsive**, then repeated *"how will you personally ensure…"* for the registered manager.

## ⭐ The five tests the inspector actually applies
These matter more than any individual question — they're the pattern behind all of them.

1. **"Which policy is that in?"** — asked repeatedly (*"what provider policy is that in that explains that process?"*, *"which of the policies are those local guidelines outlined in?"*). **Every verbal claim must be traceable to a named document.** → Clinickly's requirement-theme map + Master Policy Index answer this directly.
2. **"But the policies don't say that."** — the inspector found a live gap between what was described and what was written: *"I understand that, but the policies don't say as such… as your staff members, if it doesn't say it…"* **Practice and policy must match.**
3. **"Do your staff know?"** — *"my point is that do your staff know?"* **Having a policy ≠ staff following it.** → training assignment + completion tracking.
4. **"Is that written down anywhere?"** — applied to decisions *not* to do something: *"in your emergency policy, there's certain medicines you aren't holding on site. Have you done a risk assessment around that? Is that written down?"* **Every opt-out needs a documented risk assessment.**
5. **⭐ "How have you personalised these?"** — the single most important question for Clinickly:
   > *"Obviously some of them are from [a] consultant. So how have you ensured that the policies and procedures have been **personalised** or are **applicable to your service** and your service type?"*
   
   **This validates the whole SOP-builder design.** Bought-in or generic policies are actively probed. The builder's job — clinic facts, named leads, real workflow, `[CONFIRM: …]` placeholders — is precisely the defence against this question.

## Question bank (extracted, by domain)
**Well-led:** service description + evidence of local need · staffing structure and employment basis (employed/sessional/practising privileges) · **fit and proper persons** process for each director, and is it documented · what each board member brings · governance structure and how information flows · meeting cadence and whether minuted · **how policies were personalised** · induction and mandatory training during probation.

**Safe:** how patients access the service (self-refer/referral) · how clinicians ascertain medical history · **process for sharing with the patient's own GP** — and what happens if the patient refuses consent · **children: parental responsibility and ID verification — and which policy covers it** · unlicensed medicines · **controlled drugs, by schedule** · formulary and whether prescribing is restricted · **follow-up where long-term-condition drugs are prescribed** · repeat prescriptions (incl. second appointment without GP consent) · paper vs electronic prescriptions · **how MHRA alerts reach you** · medical-emergency arrangements and training · **risk assessment for emergency medicines not stocked** · emergency procedure *during an online consultation* · whether life-support training is hands-on or online · records: system used, any paper, scanned? · remote-consultation devices and their security · blood-test results: timeliness and follow-up · **incident reporting: how logged, how accessed, how overseen** · infection prevention and control, cleaning between patients, PPE · **safeguarding lead identity, level 3 adults *and* children, and the correct local authority**.

**Effective:** staying skilled and competent · consent — and what happens when consent is refused · **how consent differs for a child** · keeping staff competent after employment · clinical system choice · **how patients are made aware of costs** · oversight that you're doing what you said · are meetings documented.

**Caring:** ensuring the patient understands · **chaperone policy** — in place, and staff understanding of the chaperone's actual role · whether chaperone preference is captured at booking.

**Responsive:** care meeting people's needs (largely covered by earlier answers).

**Registered manager:** basic principles and values · your legal responsibilities (health & safety and beyond) · **who is held responsible for compliance breaches** · how you stay visible and contactable given other business commitments · oversight of safety and of incidents · **what you would do if a staff member reported a safeguarding concern**, and the local authority's role · duty of candour, in your own words · **complaints — what happens if the complaint is about you**.

**Location walkthrough:** reception and waiting area · consultation room · sharps · vinyl/wipeable surfaces · **emergency kit inspected item by item** · cleaning equipment · **accessibility adaptations**.

## Live gaps found during this interview (instructive)
- **Paediatric adrenaline auto-injector strength missing** — only adult held, spotted during the kit check
- **No accessibility handles/adaptations** for wheelchair users in the consultation room
- Director DBS checks held on the *pharmacy* file, not the clinic's
- Risk-assessment rationale existed verbally but wasn't consistently written down

Pattern: **the gaps were all "you do it but haven't documented it" or "you hadn't thought of that specific item"** — exactly what a readiness checklist prevents.

## Product implications
1. **Inspection-readiness checklist** — build directly from the question bank above; each question links to the policy/evidence that answers it. "An inspector will ask you this — can you answer it?"
2. **"Which policy covers this?"** — the requirement-theme map (§5.7) is the answer to the most-repeated question in the interview.
3. **Personalisation is the pass/fail test** — the SOP builder's `[CONFIRM: …]` placeholders and "how this process actually runs in your clinic" field are the direct defence. Never ship generic.
4. **Prompt for documented risk assessments on every opt-out** — "you've said you don't stock X / don't offer Y — record the risk assessment."
5. **Staff-knowledge evidence** — training assignment + completion tracking answers *"do your staff know?"*
6. **Premises/equipment checklist** — emergency kit itemised (incl. paediatric strengths), accessibility, wipeable surfaces, sharps.
