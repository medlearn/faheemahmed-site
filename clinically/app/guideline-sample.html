Verify at source

Chronic facial condition — central redness, flushing, papules/pustules and visible vessels. No comedones (distinguishes it from acne). Manage by subtype and trigger avoidance.

Recognise the pattern

Affects the central face (cheeks, nose, chin, forehead). Subtypes often overlap:

  • Erythematotelangiectatic — persistent redness, flushing, visible vessels.
  • Papulopustular — inflammatory papules/pustules; often mistaken for acne, but no comedones.
  • Phymatous — skin thickening (e.g. rhinophyma).
  • Ocular — dry, gritty, irritated eyes and lid-margin inflammation (commonly missed).

Common triggers: sunlight, heat, alcohol, spicy food, hot drinks, stress.

Assess & rule out

  • No comedones → not acne vulgaris.
  • Consider differentials: perioral dermatitis, seborrhoeic dermatitis, lupus (malar rash), photodermatitis.
  • Always ask about eye symptoms — ocular rosacea is under-recognised.

Manage — general measures (everyone)

  • Identify and avoid triggers.
  • Daily broad-spectrum SPF 30+.
  • Gentle, non-soap cleanser; fragrance-free moisturiser.

Manage — by presentation

  • Papulopustular: first-line topical — ivermectin or metronidazole (or azelaic acid). Moderate–severe/refractory — add an oral option (e.g. low-dose doxycycline). Confirm within your scope/formulary.
  • Persistent redness/flushing: topical brimonidine can reduce redness short-term; prioritise trigger control.
  • Phymatous: refer to dermatology (may need procedural treatment).
  • Ocular: lid hygiene + warm compresses + ocular lubricants; refer optometry/ophthalmology if not settling or vision affected.

Treatment options — doses via BNF

The approach below is guideline-based. Doses, cautions, interactions and monitoring come from the BNF — always check the current entry before prescribing. Clinickly does not generate or store doses.

  • Oral (moderate–severe / refractory): low-dose doxycycline — check BNF ↗ (photosensitivity; avoid in pregnancy & children).

Review & expectations

  • Review at 6–8 weeks.
  • Set expectations: rosacea is controlled, not cured; topicals take several weeks to work.

Refer / escalate

  • Ocular involvement affecting vision.
  • Phymatous change.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty, or not responding to first-line.

Safety-net: advise the patient to seek prompt review for worsening eye symptoms or any change in vision.

Rosacea is a common, long-term skin condition that mainly affects the face. It causes redness, flushing and sometimes small bumps — it's not caused by poor hygiene, and it isn't acne.

What can trigger it

Common triggers are sun, heat, alcohol, spicy food, hot drinks and stress — though everyone's are slightly different. Noticing yours is a big part of managing it.

What helps day to day

  • Wear sunscreen every day (SPF 30 or higher).
  • Use a gentle, fragrance-free cleanser and moisturiser.
  • Try to spot and avoid your personal triggers.

Treatments

Depending on your type of rosacea, your clinician may prescribe a cream — and sometimes tablets for a while. Treatments control rosacea rather than cure it, and creams can take a few weeks to start working.

Your eyes

Rosacea can affect the eyes, making them dry, gritty or irritated. Please tell your clinician if you notice this.

Get seen sooner if…

your eyes become painful or your vision changes — seek advice promptly.
